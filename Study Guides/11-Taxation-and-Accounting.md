## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

This guide breaks down how **accounting** and **taxation** work differently across MHCCA's three main financial products: **Lease Acquisition (Capital Lease)**, **Operating Lease**, and **Installment Sale (Loan)**. The goal is to help you understand these differences so well that you can use them as a **selling tool** when talking to clients and their advisors.

Why does this matter to you as an associate? Because clients and their accountants will ask you questions like "Which option gives me the best tax deduction?" or "Will this show up on my balance sheet?" If you can answer confidently and accurately, you build trust, close more deals, and avoid spreading common myths that could hurt your credibility.

> **Bottom line:** Each product type is treated differently by accountants and by the CRA. Knowing the difference is a competitive advantage.

## SECTION 1: KEY DEFINITIONS

Before we dive into product types, let's nail down two terms you'll hear constantly.

### Accounting

**Accounting** is a set of conventions and rules for collecting and compiling financial data to present the financial situation of an entity. It allows users (investors, lenders, regulators) to **compare, analyze, and make decisions**.

**Why This Matters:** When a client asks "how does this show up on my books?" they are asking an accounting question. The answer changes depending on which product they choose.

### Taxation

**Taxation** is how public administrations (federal and provincial governments) subject a person or company to taxes, fees, contributions, or social security contributions.

**Why This Matters:** When a client asks "how much can I write off?" they are asking a tax question. Again, the answer depends on the product. Accounting treatment and tax treatment are **not the same thing** — this is where most confusion (and myths) come from.

At some point, a client will ask you: **"Should I incorporate?"**

### The Correct Answer

Answer like a good economist: **"IT DEPENDS!"**

An incorporated company has many advantages (limited liability, tax planning flexibility, easier access to financing), but every situation is different. The advantages usually outweigh the disadvantages, but you should **never give direct advice**.

### What You Should Do

- Acknowledge that incorporation has benefits
- Tell the client that each situation is unique

- Never position yourself as a tax or legal advisor

**Why This Matters:** You are a leasing professional, not a CPA or a lawyer. Giving direct incorporation advice could expose you and MHCCA to liability. Your job is to know enough to have the conversation, then point them to the right professionals.

## SECTION 3: THE THREE PRODUCT TYPES — THE BIG PICTURE

This is the core of the entire presentation. MHCCA offers three main products, and each one is treated differently for both accounting and tax purposes. Here is the overview before we go deep on each one.

| Feature | Lease Acquisition (Capital Lease) | Operating Lease (FMV Lease) | Installment Sale (Loan) |
|---------|----------------------------------|----------------------------|------------------------|
| Client owns equipment? | At end of term ($1 buyout) | No (option to buy at FMV or return) | Immediately (dealer holds title until paid) |
| On balance sheet? | YES (asset + liability) | NO (off-balance sheet) | YES (asset + liability) |

## SECTION 4: PRODUCT 1 — LEASE ACQUISITION (CAPITAL LEASE / LEASE-TO-OWN)

### What Is It?

The client leases equipment with the **intention of owning it** at the end of the term. The purchase option is typically **$1** (a bargain purchase option). This is sometimes called a "lease-to-own" or "capital lease."

### Accounting Treatment

- The equipment **appears on the asset side** of the client's balance sheet
- The lease obligation is **capitalized on the liability side** of the balance sheet
- The client records **depreciation** as an expense
- The client records **interest** as a separate expense

**Think of it this way:** Even though it is technically a "lease," the accountant treats it almost like the client bought the equipment with a loan. It shows up on both sides of the balance sheet.

### Tax Treatment

- The **rent of the lease is recorded as an expense**
- The **full lease payment** (interest + principal combined) is **tax deductible**

**Why This Matters:** This is the key selling point. Unlike a loan where only interest is deductible, with a lease acquisition the **entire payment** is deductible for tax purposes. This results in the **highest total tax deductions** of all three products.

---

## SECTION 5: PRODUCT 2 — OPERATING LEASE (FMV LEASE)

### What Is It?

The client rents the equipment with the option to **return it** or **purchase it at Fair Market Value (FMV)** at the end of the term. MHCCA (the lessor) **retains ownership** throughout.

### Accounting Treatment

- The equipment does **NOT appear** on the client's balance sheet — no asset recorded, no liability recorded
- The client records the **full lease payment as an operating expense**
- **No depreciation** is recorded by the client
- **No interest** is recorded separately by the client

**Think of it this way:** It is like renting an apartment. You pay rent each month, that rent is an expense, but the apartment never shows up as your asset or your debt.

### Tax Treatment

- **No asset recorded**, so no CCA (Capital Cost Allowance) is deducted by the client
- The **total amount of lease payments** is deducted for tax purposes

### Off-Balance Sheet Qualification

For an operating lease to qualify as "off-balance sheet," it must meet **4 specific criteria**. This is what makes it attractive to clients who need to maintain certain financial ratios (e.g., debt-to-equity ratios required by their bank).

**Why This Matters:** The operating lease has the **lowest monthly payment** and keeps the client's balance sheet clean. This is a powerful selling point for clients who are managing bank covenants or who want flexibility to upgrade equipment frequently.

---

## SECTION 6: PRODUCT 3 — INSTALLMENT SALE CONTRACT (LOAN)

### What Is It?

The client purchases equipment through a dealer, and MHCCA finances the purchase. This is a **three-party transaction**: customer, MHCCA, and dealer. The dealer retains ownership rights until the client makes full payment.

### Important Detail About Taxes on Purchase

Taxes (GST/HST/QST) are **payable at the time of purchase**. Sometimes these taxes can be financed, and the client gets reimbursed in the 3rd or 4th month after filing.

### Accounting Treatment

- The equipment **appears as an asset** on the balance sheet
- The loan **appears as a liability** on the balance sheet
- The client records **depreciation** as an expense
- The client records **interest** as an expense

### Tax Treatment

- The asset is recorded, so **CCA (Capital Cost Allowance)** applies
- The CRA specifies the depreciation rate for each asset class (e.g., **30% for excavators**)
- **Loan repayments (principal) are NOT tax deductible**
- **Interest IS tax deductible**

**Why This Matters:** With a loan, the client only gets to deduct interest and CCA. The principal repayments give zero tax benefit. Compare this to lease acquisition where the ENTIRE payment is deductible.

## SECTION 7: THE BIGDAN VACHON EXCAVATION EXAMPLE

This is the real-world example from the presentation. Pay close attention to the numbers.

### The Setup

- **Equipment:** Excavator
- **Cost:** $300,000
- **Term:** 60 months
- **Interest Rate:** 7.50%

### Monthly Payment Comparison

| | Purchase (Loan) | Operating Lease | Lease Acquisition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Monthly payment** | $6,011 | $4,308 | $6,011 |
| **Purchase option** | N/A | $85,000 (FMV) | $1 |
| **On balance sheet?** | YES (asset + liability) | NO | YES (asset + liability) |
| **Depreciation recorded?** | YES (20% accounting rate, 30% tax rate) | NO | YES |
| **Interest recorded?** | YES | NO (full payment is expense) | YES (but full payment is tax deductible) |

### Notice the Key Differences

- The **operating lease** has the **lowest monthly payment** ($4,308 vs. $6,011) — but the client does not own the equipment at the end
- The **loan** and **lease acquisition** have the **same monthly payment** ($6,011) — but they are treated very differently for tax purposes
- The operating lease has an **$85,000 FMV buyout** at the end if the client wants to keep the equipment

### After 5 Years — The Tax Comparison That Matters

| | Purchase (Loan) | Operating Lease | Lease Acquisition |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Added operating expenses** | $250,091 | $258,490 | $250,091 |
| **Total tax deductions** | $299,458 | $258,490 | **$360,683** |

### The Key Insight

**Lease Acquisition gives the HIGHEST total tax deductions at $360,683.** This is because the full lease payment (interest + principal) is tax deductible.

- Lease Acquisition: **$360,683** in total tax deductions
- Purchase (Loan): **$299,458** in total tax deductions
- Operating Lease: **$258,490** in total tax deductions

Read those numbers again. The lease-to-own gives over **$100,000 more** in tax deductions than the operating lease, and over **$60,000 more** than the loan — on the same $300,000 excavator.

## SECTION 9: BEWARE OF MYTHS — THINGS PEOPLE GET WRONG

This section is critical. These myths are commonly repeated by clients, brokers, and even some accountants. You need to know the truth so you never accidentally spread misinformation.

### Myth #1: "Your monthly rents are seen as an expense"

**VERDICT: FALSE for Lease-to-Own (Capital Lease)**

People assume that because it is called a "lease," the payments are just operating expenses. **Wrong.** For a lease-to-own (lease acquisition), the payments are treated like loan repayments at the accounting level — split into depreciation and interest. The difference is at the **tax level**, where the full payment IS deductible.

### Myth #2: "The operating lease is more advantageous for tax purposes"

**VERDICT: FALSE**

This is one of the most common misconceptions. Look at the numbers from the Bigdan Vachon example:

| Product | Total Tax Deductions |
|---------|---------------------|
| Operating Lease | $258,490 |
| Loan | $299,458 |
| **Lease Acquisition** | **$360,683** |

The operating lease actually provides the **LEAST** total tax deductions of all three options. Its advantages are about balance sheet management, not tax optimization.

### Myth #3: "Leasing is an advantage because you recover taxes later"

**VERDICT: FALSE / MISLEADING**

This is not how the tax declaration law works. People sometimes confuse the timing of tax payments with the total tax benefit. Do not repeat this claim.

### Myth #4: "Your taxes are spread over 60 months in a lease"

**VERDICT: FALSE**

Since leasing is a taxable service, taxes (GST/HST/QST) are charged **on each payment**. They are not deferred or spread differently than other taxable transactions.

## SECTION 10: ADVANTAGES OF THE OPERATING LEASE — WHEN TO SELL IT

Even though the operating lease does not provide the highest tax deductions, it has powerful advantages that matter to certain clients. Here are the five key selling points:

### 1. Faster Equipment Change/Upgrade Cycle

The client can return the equipment at end of term and get newer, better equipment. Ideal for industries where technology changes quickly (e.g., construction, IT, transportation).

### 2. Off-Balance Sheet Qualification

The lease does not appear as debt on the client's balance sheet. This keeps their debt-to-equity ratio healthier.

### 3. Compliance with Financial Ratios

Banks often impose financial covenants (e.g., "your debt-to-equity ratio cannot exceed 3:1"). An operating lease helps clients stay within these limits because it does not add to reported debt.

### 4. Smaller Rent/Payments

The operating lease has the **lowest monthly payment** of all three options. For cash-flow-sensitive businesses, this can be the deciding factor.

### 5. No Residual Risk at End of Lease

The client is not stuck with aging equipment they cannot sell. They can simply return it to MHCCA. The residual value risk stays with the lessor.

**Why This Matters:** Not every client's priority is maximizing tax deductions. Some clients need low payments, clean balance sheets, or equipment flexibility. The operating lease is the right tool for those needs.

## SECTION 11: TAX MANAGEMENT — GST, HST, QST, AND MORE

### The Sales Taxes You Need to Know

| Tax | What It Stands For | Rate | Where It Applies |
|-----|-------------------|------|-----------------|
| **GST** | Goods and Services Tax | **5%** | Federal — all of Canada |
| **HST** | Harmonized Sales Tax | Varies (combines GST + PST) | PEI, New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, Ontario, Newfoundland and Labrador |
| **QST** | Quebec Sales Tax | **9.975%** | Quebec only |

### Registration Threshold

Businesses with **taxable supplies over $30,000 per quarter** must register for GST/QST. This is a key threshold to remember.

### Tax Recovery Mechanisms

| Abbreviation | Full Name | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **ITC** | Input Tax Credit | Allows businesses to recover **GST** paid on business inputs |
| **RTC** (or RTI) | Refund of Input Tax | Allows businesses to recover **QST** paid on business inputs |

**Think of it this way:** If your client buys supplies for their business and pays GST/QST, they can claim ITCs and RTCs to get that tax money back. This is not a "deduction" — it is a direct credit/refund against taxes owed.

### Zero-Rated vs. Exempt

These are two categories of goods/services that are treated specially for tax purposes.

**Zero-Rated (taxed at 0% — business CAN claim ITCs):**
- Basic food products
- Agricultural products
- Prescription drugs
- Medical devices
- Exports

**Exempt (not taxed — business CANNOT claim ITCs):**
- Residential rent
- Health services
- Child care
- Education

**Why This Matters:** The distinction between zero-rated and exempt matters because zero-rated suppliers can still claim ITCs on their business inputs, while exempt suppliers cannot.

### MHCCA and Tax Collection

As the **lessor**, MHCCA collects taxes on lease payments. Taxes are due on the date the tenant is required to pay rent. This means the client pays tax on each monthly payment, not in one lump sum at the beginning.

## SECTION 12: PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER — YOUR DECISION FRAMEWORK

When a client or their accountant asks "which product should I choose?", use this framework:

| Client Priority | Best Product | Why |
|----------------|-------------|-----|
| Maximize tax deductions | **Lease Acquisition** | Full payment (interest + principal) is tax deductible — highest total deductions |
| Lowest monthly payment | **Operating Lease** | Residual value reduces monthly obligation |
| Keep debt off balance sheet | **Operating Lease** | Does not appear as asset or liability |
| Own equipment immediately | **Loan (Installment Sale)** | Immediate ownership (dealer holds title as security) |
| Fastest capital accumulation | **Loan** | Building equity from day one |
| Flexibility to upgrade | **Operating Lease** | Return equipment at end of term, no residual risk |

**Always remember:** You are not the client's accountant or tax advisor. Present the options, explain the differences, and recommend they consult their professionals for the final decision.
