## What Is This Document About?

This guide breaks down everything you need to know about **Operating Leases** at Mitsubishi HC
Capital Canada (MHCCA). In plain English:

- MHCCA buys equipment, then rents it to a client for a set period.
- At the end, the client can **return** the equipment or **buy** it at whatever it is worth at
  that time (Fair Market Value).
- The big deal: because MHCCA keeps ownership, the client might not have to show this "debt" on
  their balance sheet at all. That makes the client's finances look cleaner.

**Why should you care?** Operating leases are a major growth product for MHCCA. The company has
set a target of **$50 million** in operating leases next year. This is not a side product -- it is
a strategic priority, and you will be tested on it.

## 1. Operating Lease vs. Capital Lease -- The Core Difference

### Plain English

Think of it like renting vs. buying a car:

- **Operating Lease = Renting.** You use the car, you pay monthly, and at the end you hand back
  the keys. You never owned it. It never shows up as "your car" on any paperwork.
- **Capital Lease = Buying on a payment plan.** You are paying it off. At the end, it is yours.
  It shows up on your records as your asset and your debt from day one.

### At MHCCA Specifically

| Aspect | Operating Lease | Capital Lease |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| Who owns the equipment? | **MHCCA** retains ownership | Client effectively owns it |
| End-of-term options | Return it OR buy at **Fair Market Value** | Client keeps it (often $1 buyout) |
| Who takes residual risk? | **MHCCA** (or shared with dealer) | Client (they own it) |
| Balance sheet treatment | Potentially **off** balance sheet | **On** balance sheet |
| Monthly payments | **Lower** (residual reduces financed amount) | Higher (financing full cost) |

### Why This Matters

When a client uses an operating lease, they get **lower monthly payments** because they are not
financing the full cost of the equipment -- they are only financing the difference between the
equipment cost and what it will be worth at the end (the residual value). The client also avoids
showing a big debt on their balance sheet, which makes their financial ratios look better to
banks and investors.

**For MHCCA**, the trade-off is that the company takes on **residual value risk**. If the
equipment is worth less than expected at the end of the term, MHCCA could lose money.

## 2. THE 4 CRITERIA FOR OFF-BALANCE SHEET TREATMENT

### THIS IS THE MOST IMPORTANT SECTION IN THIS ENTIRE GUIDE

> Siraaj specifically mentioned these criteria. Memorize them. Know them cold. Be able to recite
> them in your sleep.

For a lease to qualify as **off-balance sheet** (i.e., to be treated as an operating lease for
accounting purposes), **ALL FOUR** of the following criteria must be met. Not three. Not most of
them. **ALL FOUR.**

---

### Criterion 1: Lease Term < 75% of Useful Life

> **The lease term must be LESS THAN 75% of the useful life of the asset.**

**Plain English:** If a piece of equipment is expected to last 10 years, the lease cannot be
longer than 7.5 years (75% of 10). If you lease it for 8 years, this criterion fails, and the
lease goes ON the balance sheet.

**Example:** A forklift has a useful life of 8 years.
- Lease term of 5 years = 62.5% of useful life. **PASSES** (less than 75%).
- Lease term of 7 years = 87.5% of useful life. **FAILS** (more than 75%).

**Why this rule exists:** If you are leasing something for almost its entire useful life, you are
basically using it up completely. That looks a lot more like ownership than renting.

---

### Criterion 2: Present Value of Payments Cannot Exceed 90% of FMV

> **The present value of all lease payments CANNOT EXCEED 90% of the Fair Market Value of the
> asset.**

**Plain English:** Add up all the lease payments (adjusted to today's dollars using present value
math). If that total is more than 90% of what the equipment costs, the lease goes ON the balance
sheet.

**Example:** Equipment costs $100,000.
- Present value of all lease payments = $85,000. That is 85% of FMV. **PASSES** (less than 90%).
- Present value of all lease payments = $95,000. That is 95% of FMV. **FAILS** (more than 90%).

**Why this rule exists:** If you are paying for almost the entire value of the equipment through
lease payments, you are basically buying it. The accounting rules say "if it walks like a
purchase and talks like a purchase, it is a purchase."

---

### Criterion 3: No Bargain Purchase Option

> **There can be NO bargain purchase option. No $1 buyout. The purchase option must be at Fair
> Market Value (FMV).**

**Plain English:** The lease cannot give the client the right to buy the equipment at the end for
some sweetheart deal price (like $1 or 10% of original cost). If there is a purchase option, it
must be at whatever the equipment is actually worth on the open market at that time.

**Example:**
- Lease says client can buy for FMV at end of term. **PASSES.**
- Lease says client can buy for $1 at end of term. **FAILS.** A $1 buyout is a bargain purchase
  option, which means the client is effectively guaranteed to buy it. That is ownership in
  disguise.

**Why this rule exists:** If you can buy something for $1 at the end, everyone knows you are
going to buy it. You are the real owner. The lease is just a financing arrangement.

---

### Criterion 4: No Automatic Transfer of Ownership

> **There can be NO automatic transfer of ownership at the end of the lease term.**

**Plain English:** The lease cannot say "and when the term is over, the equipment automatically
becomes yours." Ownership must stay with MHCCA unless the client actively chooses to purchase at
FMV.

**Example:**
- Lease says: "At end of term, client may return equipment or purchase at FMV." **PASSES.**
- Lease says: "At end of term, title transfers to client." **FAILS.**

**Why this rule exists:** If ownership automatically transfers, the lease is just a financing
tool to buy the equipment. The client was always going to end up owning it.

---

### The 4 Criteria at a Glance

| # | Criterion | Threshold | Memory Cue |
|---|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Lease term vs. useful life | **< 75%** of useful life | "Three-quarters life" |
| 2 | PV of payments vs. FMV | **< 90%** of FMV | "Ninety percent value" |
| 3 | Bargain purchase option | **NONE allowed** | "No dollar buyout" |
| 4 | Auto transfer of ownership | **NONE allowed** | "No free handover" |

### Critical Rule: ALL FOUR Must Be Met

If even **one** criterion fails, the entire lease is classified as a **capital lease** (on
balance sheet). Think of it like a chain -- one broken link and the whole thing falls apart.

| Scenario | Criterion 1 | Criterion 2 | Criterion 3 | Criterion 4 | Result |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|--------|
| A | PASS | PASS | PASS | PASS | **Off balance sheet** |
| B | PASS | PASS | **FAIL** | PASS | **On balance sheet** |
| C | **FAIL** | PASS | PASS | PASS | **On balance sheet** |
| D | PASS | **FAIL** | PASS | **FAIL** | **On balance sheet** |

## 3. IFRS vs. ASPE -- Why It Matters for MHCCA Clients

### The Big Change in 2019

Starting **January 1, 2019**, new **IFRS** (International Financial Reporting Standards) rules
made it much harder for companies to keep leases off their balance sheets. Under the new IFRS 16
standard, almost all leases go on the balance sheet for companies that follow IFRS.

### But Here Is the Good News for MHCCA

**Most MHCCA clients are small and mid-size private companies.** They do not follow IFRS. They
follow **ASPE** (Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises). Under ASPE, the traditional
4 criteria still apply, and operating leases can still be kept off the balance sheet.

### Quick Comparison

| Standard | Who Uses It | Off-Balance Sheet Possible? | The 4 Criteria Apply? |
|----------|------------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| **IFRS** | Large / public companies | Very difficult since 2019 | Mostly overridden by IFRS 16 |
| **ASPE** | Small / mid-size private companies | **YES** | **YES -- all 4 still apply** |

### Why This Matters

When you are talking to a client about the off-balance sheet benefits of an operating lease, you
need to know **which accounting standard they follow:**

- **ASPE client** (most MHCCA clients): You can confidently pitch the off-balance sheet benefit.
  The 4 criteria apply, and if all are met, the lease stays off their balance sheet.
- **IFRS client** (large or public companies): The off-balance sheet pitch is much weaker. Under
  IFRS 16, most leases end up on the balance sheet regardless.

### Important Tax Note

> **There is NO tax advantage to on-balance sheet vs. off-balance sheet treatment.**

The advantage is purely in the **presentation of financial statements**. Off-balance sheet makes
the client's debt-to-equity ratio look better, their return on assets look better, and their
overall financial health look stronger on paper. But the tax man does not care either way.

## 4. FPO vs. FMV Leases

MHCCA offers two types of operating leases: **FPO** (Fixed Purchase Option) and **FMV** (Fair
Market Value). Both are operating leases, but they differ in how the end-of-term purchase option
works.

### FPO -- Fixed Purchase Option

**Plain English:** At the start of the lease, the client knows exactly what percentage of the
original cost they can buy the equipment for at the end. It is a fixed, pre-determined amount.

- The purchase price is **known at signing** (expressed as a fixed percentage of original cost).
- Because this gives the client more certainty, MHCCA charges a **rate premium of 0.50% to
  1.00%** above the standard rate.
- MHCCA builds in a **3% cushion** on the residual value to protect against loss.
- **Lower risk for MHCCA** because the buyout amount is known and the client is more likely to
  exercise the option.

### FMV -- Fair Market Value

**Plain English:** At the end of the lease, an appraiser determines what the equipment is
actually worth, and that is the purchase price. Nobody knows the exact number until that time.

- The purchase price is **unknown until end of term** -- determined by fair market appraisal.
- Standard rate applies (target: **4.00% NIM** -- Net Interest Margin).
- **No cushion** on the residual value.
- **Higher risk for MHCCA** because the company must estimate what the equipment will be worth
  years from now, and there is no guarantee the estimate is right.
- **Lower monthly payments for the client** because there is no rate premium built in.

### Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | FPO Lease | FMV Lease |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Purchase option | **Known at signing** (fixed %) | **Unknown** -- determined at end of term |
| Rate premium | **0.50% - 1.00% higher** than standard | **Standard rate** (4.00% NIM target) |
| Residual cushion | **3% cushion** on residual value | **No cushion** on residual |
| Risk to MHCCA | **Lower** (known buyout amount) | **Higher** (must estimate residual) |
| Client appeal | **Predictability** -- know the buyout upfront | **Lower monthly payments** |
| Who prefers this? | Risk-averse clients who want certainty | Cost-sensitive clients who want lowest payment |

### Why This Matters

When you are talking to a client, you need to understand what they value more:

- **"I want to know exactly what it will cost me to keep this equipment."** --> Pitch **FPO**.
- **"I want the lowest possible monthly payment."** --> Pitch **FMV**.
- **"I am not sure if I will keep or return the equipment."** --> Pitch **FMV** (they are not
  paying a premium for a purchase option they might not use).

## 5. Target Industries

### Industries MHCCA Targets for Operating Leases

Not every industry is a good fit for operating leases. MHCCA focuses on industries where
equipment holds its value reasonably well and residual values can be estimated with confidence.

| Industry | Why It Works | Equipment Examples |
|----------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Manufacturing / Commercial / Industrial** | Equipment has predictable useful life and resale value | CNC machines, industrial presses, packaging lines |
| **Material Handling** | Forklifts and warehouse equipment have strong resale markets | Forklifts, pallet jacks, conveyor systems |
| **Construction** | Heavy equipment retains value; strong dealer networks for resale | Excavators, loaders, haulers, cranes |
| **Technology** | Regular upgrade cycles make operating leases attractive to clients | Servers, networking equipment, IT infrastructure |

### The One Industry to AVOID

> **Transportation is NOT a target industry for operating leases.**

**Why?** Used asset prices in transportation (trucks, trailers) are **too volatile** for MHCCA
to confidently estimate residual values. If a truck's resale value drops unexpectedly, MHCCA
takes a big loss. The residual risk is simply too high and too unpredictable.

**Memory trick:** "Trucks are too TRUCULENT for residual estimates." (Truculent = unpredictably
aggressive -- just like used truck prices.)

## 7. Residual Risk and Risk Sharing

### What Is Residual Risk?

**Residual risk** is the risk that the equipment will be worth **less** than MHCCA estimated when
the lease was set up. Since operating leases depend on the equipment having value at the end of
the term, this is a critical risk for MHCCA.

**Example:** MHCCA leases a $500,000 excavator with an estimated residual value of $150,000
after 5 years. If the client returns it and MHCCA can only sell it for $100,000, MHCCA just
lost $50,000.

### How MHCCA Manages This Risk: Dealer Risk Sharing

MHCCA does not always take on residual risk alone. OEM and dealer partners can share the risk
through two mechanisms:

---

### Option A: Full Residual Repurchase Agreement

**What it is:** The dealer agrees to **buy back ALL returned equipment** at the end of the
lease, at the agreed residual value.

**Plain English:** The dealer says, "If the client gives it back, we will buy it from you at the
price we agreed on." This completely eliminates MHCCA's residual risk on returned equipment.

**Why a dealer would agree to this:** The dealer wants MHCCA to finance their customers'
equipment purchases. Making the financing partner comfortable with residual risk means more
leases get approved, which means more equipment gets sold.

---

### Option B: Partial Residual Guarantee (First Loss)

**What it is:** The dealer covers the **first loss up to 9.9%** of the residual value. MHCCA
covers anything beyond that.

**This is easier to understand with a number example:**

> **Scenario:** Equipment has a residual value of **$100,000** at end of lease.
> Client returns the equipment.
> MHCCA sells the equipment on the open market for **$85,000**.
> That is a **$15,000 loss**.

**Who pays what?**

| Loss Layer | Amount | Who Pays |
|-----------|--------|----------|
| First loss (up to 9.9% of $100K = $9,900) | **$9,900** | **Dealer** |
| Remaining loss ($15,000 - $9,900) | **$5,100** | **MHCCA** |
| **Total loss** | **$15,000** | Split between dealer and MHCCA |

**Why 9.9%?** This is a negotiated threshold. It means the dealer has meaningful skin in the
game (covering almost 10% of residual value) without being on the hook for catastrophic losses.

### Comparison of Risk Sharing Options

| Feature | Full Repurchase | Partial Guarantee (First Loss) |
|---------|----------------|-------------------------------|
| Dealer commitment | **Buys back ALL returned equipment** | Covers **first 9.9% loss** on residual |
| MHCCA residual risk | **Eliminated** (on returned units) | **Reduced** but not eliminated |
| Dealer risk | **High** -- must buy back at set price | **Moderate** -- capped at 9.9% of residual |
| Typical use case | Strong dealer partnership, high confidence | Most common arrangement |

## 9. Key Selling Points for Clients

When you are talking to a client about why they should choose an operating lease, these are the
benefits you emphasize:

### The Big Five Advantages

**1. Lower Monthly Payments**

Because the client is not financing the full cost of the equipment (the residual value is
excluded), monthly payments are lower than a capital lease or loan for the same equipment.

> **Example:** $500,000 excavator, 5-year term.
> - Capital lease: client finances the full $500,000.
> - Operating lease with 30% residual: client effectively finances $350,000.
> - Lower financed amount = lower monthly payments.

**2. Off-Balance Sheet Treatment (for ASPE Clients)**

If all 4 criteria are met and the client follows ASPE, the lease does not appear as a debt on
their balance sheet. This makes their financial statements look healthier.

> **Why clients care:** Banks look at debt-to-equity ratios when deciding whether to lend.
> Less visible debt = better ratios = easier access to other financing.

**3. Better Financial Ratios**

Directly related to point 2. Keeping the lease off the balance sheet means:
- Better **debt-to-equity ratio** (less debt showing)
- Better **return on assets** (fewer assets on the books)
- These ratios matter for banking covenants and investor perception.

**4. Smaller Monthly Payments**

Worth emphasizing twice because this is often the single most compelling argument for
cost-conscious clients.

**5. No Residual Risk for the Client**

At the end of the lease, if the equipment is worth less than expected, that is MHCCA's problem
(or the dealer's, if there is a risk-sharing agreement). The client can simply **walk away** and
return the equipment with no penalty.

> **Client-friendly way to say it:** "You get to use the equipment for as long as you need it.
> When the lease is up, you decide: keep it at fair market value, or hand it back and upgrade to
> something newer. Either way, you are never stuck with equipment you do not want."

### Additional Selling Points

- **Equipment refresh cycle alignment:** Operating leases naturally align with how often clients
  want to upgrade their equipment. Lease terms can match the client's preferred upgrade cycle.
- **Flexibility at end of term:** Return or purchase -- the client has options, not obligations.
- **Faster equipment change/upgrade cycle:** Clients can stay on the cutting edge without the
  hassle of selling old equipment.

## 10. Tax Treatment of Operating Leases

### How Operating Leases Are Treated for Tax Purposes

This section connects the Operating Lease training to the Taxation training materials.

**On the client's books (operating lease):**

| Item | Treatment |
|------|-----------|
| Equipment on balance sheet? | **NO** -- does not appear as an asset |
| Liability recorded? | **NO** -- no lease liability on the books |
| Depreciation recorded? | **NO** -- client does not own the asset, so no depreciation |
| Interest expense recorded? | **NO** -- there is no separated interest component |
| How is the payment recorded? | **FULL lease payment** is recorded as an **operating expense** |
| CCA (Capital Cost Allowance)? | **NO** -- client does not own the asset, so no CCA deduction |
| Tax deduction? | **YES** -- the **TOTAL lease payments** are tax-deductible as operating expenses |

### Comparison: Operating Lease vs. Loan (for tax purposes)

| Feature | Operating Lease | Loan / Capital Lease |
|---------|----------------|---------------------|
| Asset on balance sheet | **No** | Yes |
| Depreciation / CCA deducted | **No** | Yes |
| Interest deductible | **No** (no separated interest) | Yes (interest portion is deductible) |
| Lease payments deductible | **Yes -- full payment** | No (only interest portion) |
| Principal repayment deductible | N/A | **No** (principal is never deductible) |

### Why This Matters

The tax treatment is actually simpler for operating leases:

- **Operating lease:** Write off the entire payment as an expense. Done.
- **Loan/capital lease:** Split the payment into principal and interest. Only deduct the
  interest. Separately calculate and deduct CCA on the asset. More complex.

**Key reminder:** There is no tax ADVANTAGE to one vs. the other. The total tax deductions over
the life of the arrangement end up being roughly similar. The difference is in timing and
simplicity, not total amount.

## 12. Memory Tricks and Mnemonics

### The 4 Criteria: "75-90-No-No"

Say it out loud: **"Seventy-Five, Ninety, No, No."**

1. **75** -- Lease term must be less than **75%** of useful life.
2. **90** -- PV of payments must be less than **90%** of FMV.
3. **No** -- No bargain purchase option.
4. **No** -- No automatic transfer of ownership.

**"75-90-No-No"** -- repeat it five times right now.

---

### The Numbers: "Less is More"

Both number criteria use "LESS THAN":
- Less than **75%** of useful life
- Less than **90%** of fair market value

Both "No" criteria prohibit things:
- **No** bargain buyout
- **No** auto ownership transfer

**Pattern:** Two "less thans" and two "nos."

---

### FPO vs. FMV: "F-P-O = Fixed Price Option"

- **FPO** = The **P** stands for a price you can **P**redict.
- **FMV** = The **M** stands for **M**ystery (you do not know the price until the end).

---

### Why No Transportation: "Trucks are Trouble"

Transportation equipment has volatile resale values. The three T's:
- **T**rucks
- **T**railers
- **T**oo risky

---

### Risk Sharing Number: "9.9% -- Think 'Almost 10'"

The partial residual guarantee threshold is **9.9%** -- just under 10%. The dealer covers the
first "almost 10 percent" of loss.

and 11 months would pass; a lease of exactly 9 years (which is 75%) would fail because it must
be LESS than 75%.

</details>

### Question 4

**Equipment has a Fair Market Value of $200,000. The present value of all lease payments totals
$185,000. Does this pass or fail the second criterion?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

$185,000 / $200,000 = **92.5%**. This **FAILS** because it exceeds 90% of the FMV. The present
value of payments must be less than $180,000 (90% of $200,000) to pass.

</details>

### Question 5

**A lease meets criteria 1, 2, and 4, but includes a $1 buyout option. Is it off-balance sheet

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**On balance sheet.** A $1 buyout is a bargain purchase option, which violates criterion 3.
ALL FOUR criteria must be met. Failing even one means the lease is classified as a capital lease
(on balance sheet).

</details>

### Question 6

**What accounting standard do most MHCCA clients follow, and why does this matter for operating
leases?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

Most MHCCA clients follow **ASPE** (Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises) because they
are small to mid-size private companies. This matters because under ASPE, the traditional
4 criteria for off-balance sheet treatment still apply. Under **IFRS** (which large/public
companies follow), new rules since January 1, 2019 (IFRS 16) make it very difficult to keep
leases off the balance sheet.

</details>

### Question 7

**Is there a tax advantage to having a lease off-balance sheet vs. on-balance sheet?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**No.** There is NO tax advantage. The advantage is purely in the **presentation of financial
statements**. Off-balance sheet treatment makes the client's financial ratios (like
debt-to-equity) look better, but the tax treatment is the same either way.

</details>

### Question 8

**What is the difference between an FPO lease and an FMV lease?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

- **FPO (Fixed Purchase Option):** The purchase option price is known at signing (a fixed
  percentage of original cost). It carries a rate premium of 0.50% to 1.00% and has a 3%
  residual cushion. Lower risk for MHCCA.
- **FMV (Fair Market Value):** The purchase option price is unknown until end of term (determined
  by appraisal at fair market value). Standard rate applies (4.00% NIM target), no residual
  cushion. Higher risk for MHCCA but lower monthly payments for the client.

</details>

### Question 9

**Why does MHCCA NOT target the transportation industry for operating leases?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

Because **used asset prices in transportation are too volatile** for MHCCA to reliably estimate
residual values. If a truck or trailer's resale value drops unexpectedly, MHCCA takes a
significant loss on the residual. The residual risk is too high and unpredictable.

</details>

### Question 11

**Explain the Partial Residual Guarantee (First Loss) arrangement using an example where the
residual value is $80,000 and the equipment sells for $70,000.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

Total loss = $80,000 - $70,000 = **$10,000**.

The dealer covers the first loss up to 9.9% of the residual value.
9.9% of $80,000 = **$7,920**.

- **Dealer pays:** $7,920 (the first loss up to 9.9%).
- **MHCCA pays:** $10,000 - $7,920 = **$2,080** (the remaining loss beyond 9.9%).

</details>

### Question 14

**How does a client record operating lease payments for tax purposes?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

The client records the **full lease payment as an operating expense**, which is tax-deductible.
They do NOT record the asset on their balance sheet, do NOT record depreciation, do NOT record
interest expense separately, and do NOT claim CCA (Capital Cost Allowance). The entire payment
is simply deducted as a business expense.

### Question 17

**In an FPO lease, what is the rate premium and residual cushion, and why do they exist?**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

- **Rate premium:** 0.50% to 1.00% above the standard rate.
- **Residual cushion:** 3% on the residual value.

They exist because the FPO gives the client a **known, fixed purchase price** at signing. This
certainty is a benefit to the client, so MHCCA charges slightly more for it. The residual
cushion provides additional protection for MHCCA in case the equipment's actual market value
falls slightly below the fixed purchase option price.

</details>

### Question 19

**Explain the difference between a Full Residual Repurchase Agreement and a Partial Residual
Guarantee.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

- **Full Residual Repurchase Agreement:** The dealer agrees to **buy back ALL returned
  equipment** at the end of the lease at the agreed residual value. This completely eliminates
  MHCCA's residual risk on returned units.
- **Partial Residual Guarantee (First Loss):** The dealer only covers the **first loss up to
  9.9%** of the residual value. MHCCA covers any loss beyond that threshold. This reduces but
  does not eliminate MHCCA's residual risk.

</details>

can still qualify as an operating lease for balance sheet purposes.**

<details>
<summary>Click to reveal answer</summary>

**FALSE.** ALL FOUR criteria must be met. If even one criterion fails, the lease is classified
as a capital lease and goes ON the balance sheet. There are no exceptions or partial passes.

</details>
