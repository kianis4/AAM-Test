# STUDY GUIDE: Spreads Cheat Sheet — Financial Analysis for Large Deals

---

## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

When a large deal comes across your desk, you need to **spread** the client's financials — meaning you take their financial statements and break them down into a structured analysis that tells the credit story. This guide is your step-by-step playbook for how to analyze a company's financials, what to look for, what the benchmarks are, and how to present everything in the **4Bloc** format that MHCCNA uses for deal submissions.

Think of spreads as translating raw financial statements into a narrative: Is this company profitable? Can it cover its debts? Does it have enough cash to survive a bad quarter? Is it over-leveraged? The spread answers all of these questions with numbers AND context.

> **Bottom line:** Spreads are how you prove to credit that a deal is solid. A strong spread doesn't just list numbers — it explains them, flags risks, and offers mitigants.

## SECTION 1: Revenue & Margins

This is where you start every spread. Revenue and margins tell you whether the company is **making money** and whether that trend is **improving or declining**.

### What to Analyze

**Revenue Growth Rate**
- Calculate the year-over-year (YoY) percentage change in revenue
- If revenue jumped or dropped significantly, **you must explain why** — don't just state the number
- Example: "Revenue increased 22% YoY driven by the addition of 3 new municipal contracts in Q2"

**Net Profit and Net Profit Margin**
- Net profit = what's left after ALL expenses (operating costs, interest, taxes, depreciation)
- **Net Profit Margin = Net Profit ÷ Revenue**
- **Benchmark: Over 10% is good**
- If the margin is below 10%, explain what's dragging it down (high interest costs? one-time write-off? start-up phase?)

**Revenue Streams**
- Break down WHERE the money is coming from — is it sales, rentals, services, subcontracting?
- This matters because diversified revenue is more stable than a single stream

**Interim Comparisons**
- If you have comparable interim statements (e.g., 6 months of 2024 vs 6 months of 2023), compare the growth rate
- Interims show the **current trajectory** — are things improving or declining since the last year-end?

**Why This Matters:** Revenue growth alone doesn't tell the full story. A company can grow revenue 50% but lose money on every dollar if margins are thin. You need both the top-line growth AND the bottom-line margin to paint an accurate picture.

### EBITDA Margin

**EBITDA** = Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. This is the purest measure of **operational profitability** — it strips out financing decisions, tax strategies, and accounting methods to show how well the core business generates cash.

- **Benchmark: EBITDA margin over 10% is good**
- If EBITDA margin is under 10% for a specific year, **explain why** (was it a one-time event or a structural issue?)
- **Increasing EBITDA year over year** = strong signal of operational profitability — always highlight this
- Look at **costs repassed**: compare operational expenses YoY and see which have stayed flat while revenues increased
  - Example: "SG&A remained at $1.2M while revenue grew from $8M to $10M, demonstrating strong cost control and operating leverage"

**Interest Payments**
- Check if interest expense has increased YoY
- Rising interest costs can eat into margins even when revenue is growing — this is especially relevant in a high-rate environment

**Why This Matters:** EBITDA is the number that credit analysts care about most because it shows whether the business itself is profitable regardless of how it's financed. A company with growing EBITDA can support more debt; a company with declining EBITDA is a risk even if revenue is flat.

## SECTION 2: FCCR

### What Is FCCR?

**FCCR = Fixed Charge Coverage Ratio.** It measures whether the company generates enough cash to cover all of its fixed obligations (loan payments, lease payments, interest, etc.).

**The formula (simplified):**
```
FCCR = (EBITDA + Fixed Charges) ÷ (Fixed Charges + Interest + CPLDT + Proposed Payments)
```

### The Benchmark

| FCCR Level | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Over 1.0x** | The company can cover its fixed costs — this is the minimum acceptable level |
| **Under 1.0x** | The company **cannot** cover its fixed costs from operations — red flag |
| **Well above 1.0x** (e.g., 1.5x+) | Strong coverage, comfortable margin of safety |

### What to Watch For

**If FCCR is under 1.0x:**
- This raises serious concerns about the client's ability to service its debt
- **Mitigants that can help:** A cash down payment or **Personal Guarantee (PG) support** from the owner
- Always present the mitigant alongside the concern — don't just flag the problem, show how it's addressed

**If FCCR looks inflated (suspiciously high):**
- Check whether the financial statements actually report **CPLTD (Current Portion of Long-Term Debt)**
- Some financial statements — especially NTRs (Notice to Reader) — don't break out CPLTD separately
- If CPLTD isn't reported, the FCCR will look artificially high because the denominator is missing a key component
- **Always note this in your analysis:** "FCCR may be inflated as financials do not report CPLTD"

**Why This Matters:** FCCR is the single most important ratio for determining whether the client can afford the new financing you're proposing. If FCCR is weak, the deal needs structural support (down payment, PG, shorter term). If it's strong, you can present the deal with confidence.

### Memory Trick

> **"FCCR = Can they pay their bills?"** Over 1x = yes. Under 1x = not from operations alone.

## SECTION 3: Liquidity

Liquidity answers a simple question: **Can the company pay what it owes in the short term?** A profitable company can still go bankrupt if it runs out of cash. This section tells you whether that's a risk.

### Current Ratio

**Current Ratio = Current Assets ÷ Current Liabilities**

| Current Ratio | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Over 1.0x** | The company has more short-term assets than short-term debts — healthy |
| **Under 1.0x** | The company may struggle to cover short-term expenses — concern |

**Why This Matters:** If the current ratio is under 1.0x, the client doesn't have enough liquid resources to meet obligations due within the next 12 months. This needs to be explained and mitigated.

### Working Capital

**Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities**

- This is the dollar amount of the company's short-term financial cushion
- Positive working capital = the company has breathing room
- Negative working capital = the company is operating tight and may need to borrow to meet short-term needs

### Line of Credit (LOC) Analysis

This is a **critical** part of the liquidity analysis. Look at:

- **Availability vs. Utilization**: How much of the LOC is available vs. how much is drawn?
- **YoY Trend**: Track utilization over time
  - Example: "Company had $500K available on its LOC in 2023 vs $1M available in 2024. This is a positive sign — they repaid their revolving facility and improved liquidity."
- **Group of Companies**: If dealing with a group, clarify whether the LOC is available to the **whole group or just one entity**
- **Covenants Compliance**: Note if the LOC has covenants and whether the client is in compliance
- **Tolerance Letter**: If covenants are breached, is a tolerance letter required?

### Cash on Hand

- Report the cash balance from the balance sheet
- **If little to no cash is reported on the financial statements:**
  - Ask for **bank statements** or an explanation of where cash is being held
  - Could be in a cash reserve, a related company, or an investment account
  - **This directly affects working capital and current ratio** — if cash is parked elsewhere, the ratios look worse than reality

### Other Liquidity Indicators

| Indicator | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **Liquidity Coverage Ratio** | Should be over 1.0x |
| **Bank Statements** | Discuss if available — show actual cash flow patterns |
| **Overdraft Protection** | Note the limit if there are negative balances on the balance sheet |
| **Refinancing Impact** | If the client is refinancing, note that they will be able to inject working capital into their business and improve liquidity |
| **Main Source of Liquidity** | Identify whether the company relies on cash, A/R collections, LOC draws, or asset sales to fund operations |
| **Major Cash Purchases** | Note any significant recent cash outlays that temporarily reduced liquidity |

### A/R Aging List

- Review the **percentage of current vs. past due** receivables
- Look for **good quality clients** in the A/R — are they reputable, large companies?
- Check for **concentration risk**: Is one client responsible for a huge portion of the receivables?
- **Diversification** of the A/R base is a positive

**Why This Matters:** Liquidity is about survival. A company can be profitable on paper but still fail if it can't make payroll next month. The LOC analysis and cash position tell you whether the company has a safety net.

## SECTION 4: Leverage

Leverage tells you how much debt the company is carrying relative to its equity (what the owners have invested). Too much debt = too much risk.

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio

**Debt-to-Equity = Total Debt ÷ Total Equity**

| D/E Ratio | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Under 3.0x** | Acceptable leverage — the company isn't over-leveraged |
| **Over 3.0x** | The company is highly leveraged — needs explanation and mitigants |

### Senior Funded Debt (SFD)

- **Senior Funded Debt** is the total long-term debt the company carries (loans, leases, bonds)
- **Better to see SFD decreasing** — it means the company is paying down its obligations
- If SFD increased or decreased significantly, **explain why/how**
  - Example: "SFD increased $2M due to the acquisition of 5 new excavators to support a 3-year municipal contract"

### Special Situations

**Rental Houses:**
- If the client is a rental company, they naturally add equipment to keep their fleet young
- Adding debt in this context **makes sense** and isn't necessarily a red flag — it's the business model

**Highly Leveraged but Actively Deleveraging:**
- If D/E is above 3.0x but the company is actively reducing SFD year over year, **note this as a positive trend**
- Example: "While D/E remains at 3.8x, SFD has decreased 15% from $5.2M to $4.4M, indicating active deleveraging"

**Mitigating High Leverage:**
- A **cash down payment** on the proposed deal reduces the incremental debt added
- **PG support** from the owner provides additional security

### Equity vs. Tangible Net Worth (TNW)

This is an important distinction:

- **Equity** = Total Assets − Total Liabilities (includes intangible assets)
- **TNW (Tangible Net Worth)** = Equity − Intangible Assets (goodwill, patents, investments in related companies)

**If TNW is significantly lower than equity, explain why:**
- Common reasons: goodwill from an acquisition, investments in related companies, or capitalized intangible assets
- Credit analysts prefer TNW because intangible assets can't be liquidated easily

### Leverage Ratios from the 15.8 Template

| Ratio | Preferred Benchmark |
|---|---|
| **D/TNW** | Below 3.5x |
| **SFD/EBITDA** | Below 3.5x (may be higher for startups/young companies) |

**Mitigants for high leverage:**
- PG from owner with decent personal net worth (PNW)
- Injected shareholder loans (subordinated debt treated as quasi-equity)
- Related company with strong credit grade (CG)
- The company itself has good equity

**Why This Matters:** Leverage determines how much financial stress the company can absorb. A highly leveraged company has little room for error — one bad quarter could make debt payments unmanageable. Knowing the leverage story helps you structure the deal appropriately (more down payment, shorter term, PG support).

## SECTION 5: The 4Bloc Format

The **4Bloc** is the standardized format MHCCNA uses to present deals for credit approval. Think of it as a four-quadrant summary of everything the credit committee needs to know.

### Bloc 1: Company & Situation (Top Left — "KYC")

This is the **Know Your Customer** quadrant. You need a **strong top left** — this is where first impressions are made.

| Element | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **Business Description** | What does the company do? What industry? What services? |
| **Management** | Who runs the company? If the customer hasn't been in business long, talk about what the principals did **prior** and for how long |
| **Management Details** | Project experience, industry experience, ownership percentage |
| **Location** | Office vs. yard address — and do they **own** these locations? |
| **Website** | Include it — shows the business is established and verifiable |
| **Fleet Size** | How many units? What types? This is critical for equipment finance |
| **Related Parties** | List all related companies and give a brief description of each |
| **Deal Details** | Vendor, structure (lease vs loan), and the specific request (amount, term, equipment) |

**Why This Matters:** The Company & Situation bloc sets the stage. A credit analyst who doesn't understand what the company does and who runs it will be skeptical of everything that follows. Make this section clear, complete, and confidence-building.

### Bloc 2: Key Considerations / Transaction Rationale (Top Right)

This is where you make the **case for the deal** while honestly flagging risks. Always pair a concern with a mitigant.

| Element | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **Commercial Lending Experience** | Has the company borrowed before? From whom? How did they perform? |
| **Character (TIB)** | Time in Business — how long has the company been operating? |
| **Top Customers** | Review A/R and A/P lists. Look at top customers by balance |
| **Customer Concentration Risk** | If 80% of revenue comes from 1 customer, that's a negative — they lose that customer, the business is in trouble |
| **Customer Quality** | Are the top customers well-known, reputable companies? Big names in the market? |
| **TNW vs. Ask** | How does the company's tangible net worth compare to the amount being requested? |
| **Contracts** | Are they renewing existing contracts? Adding new ones? Discuss completed projects under transaction rationale |
| **Industry Outlook** | Is the industry trending positive or negative? Note both |
| **Vendor Relationship** | How strong is the relationship with the equipment vendor? |
| **UNL (Underwriting Net Loss)** | Vendor's willingness to absorb loss — strong UNL = more risk tolerance |
| **Quality of Financials** | NTR, Reviewed, or Audited? (see Section 6 for details) |
| **Financial Results Summary** | Quick summary of liquidity, profitability, and TNW |
| **Mitigants** | **ALWAYS include mitigants under key considerations** — every risk flagged needs a balancing factor |

**Memory Trick for Customer Concentration:**
> **"80/1 Rule"** — If 80% of revenue comes from 1 customer, flag it as concentration risk. Even 50% from one source is worth noting.

### Bloc 3: Financial Analysis (Bottom Left)

This is where your spread work lives — everything from Sections 1-4 of this guide goes here. Revenue, margins, FCCR, liquidity, leverage.

### Bloc 4: Proposed Structure & Conditions (Bottom Right)

Deal terms, conditions, covenants, and any special requirements.

## SECTION 6: Income Statement Analysis

This section covers the **15.8 Spreads Template** approach for analyzing the income statement — the structured framework you follow when spreading financials.

### Financial Quality — Know the Tier

The **quality of the financial statements** determines how much you can trust the numbers:

| Type | Reliability | Key Detail |
|---|---|---|
| **Audited** | Highest | Independent auditor has verified the statements — most trustworthy |
| **Reviewed** | Medium | Accountant has reviewed for plausibility but hasn't verified everything |
| **NTR (Notice to Reader)** | Lowest | Accountant compiled what management provided — **minimal independent verification** |

**If you're working with NTR financials:**
- You may need to **substantiate key numbers** with supporting documents (bank statements, tax returns, contracts)
- Flag this in your analysis: "Financials are NTR; bank statements obtained to corroborate cash position"

### Consolidated vs. Single Entity

- For a **group of companies**, clarify whether you're looking at **consolidated financials** or just one entity
- If consolidated, note which entities are included
- Watch for **inter-company revenue/expenses** between related parties — these should be **eliminated** during consolidation

### Revenue and Margin Deep Dive

| Question to Answer | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **How are they making money?** | Sales, services, rental income, other? |
| **Sales trend and margins** | Explain significant changes. Note top clients and any concentration |
| **Operating income vs. net income** | Is there income from investment activities or disposition of fixed assets inflating the bottom line? |
| **Components of COGS** | Are they using subcontractors? (common in transport/construction). Manufacturer vs. retailer vs. distributor? |
| **Inter-company revenue/expenses** | Between related parties — must be eliminated in consolidation |

**Why This Matters:** The income statement tells the **story of the company's operations** over the past year. You need to understand not just WHAT the numbers are, but WHY they changed.


### What to Highlight

| Element | What to Analyze |
|---|---|
| **TNW Calculation** | TNW to MHCCA-MHCCL investment. Are there intangibles? |
| **Assets/TNW Composition** | What does TNW consist of? Cash, A/R, Inventory, Equipment, Property? |
| **Off-Balance Sheet Assets** | Are there assets where Book Value ≠ Market Value? (e.g., real estate, equipment) |
| **Total Debts & Retirement Summary** | Include shareholder debts in the analysis |
| **Leverage Ratios** | D/TNW and SFD/EBITDA — prefer below 3.5x. May be higher for startups or young companies |

### Mitigants for High Leverage

When leverage exceeds the 3.5x benchmark, present mitigants:
- **PG from owner** who possesses decent personal net worth (PNW)
- **Injected shareholder loans** — subordinated debt that acts like equity
- **Strong CG of related company** — group strength can support a weaker entity
- **Company has good equity base** despite the ratio

### Mitsubishi HC Capital Case

When building projections or capital cases for MHCC:
- Base the case on **realistic sales figures and EBITDA margins** — don't be overly optimistic
- Impact of requested equipment should be **reasonable and even a bit conservative**
- Review **historical results and trends**, not one-time exceptions
- Include **interim performance** with year-over-year analysis and quality of prep (amortization, interest expense, etc.)

**Why This Matters:** The leverage section determines how much incremental debt the company can reasonably take on. If you're proposing a $2M equipment lease to a company that already has $8M in SFD and $2M in equity, that's a D/E of 5x — you need a strong story and strong mitigants.

## SECTION 9: Industry-Specific Questions

When spreading deals in specific industries, you need to ask the right questions. Here are the key questions for **Construction** and **Forestry** — two of MHCCNA's core segments.

### Construction

| Topic | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Operating History** | In operation since when? |
| **Short-Term Lender** | Who is it? What's the approved limit? What's the most recent balance? |
| **Revolving Facility** | Do they have a revolving facility **in addition** to the operating LOC? |
| **Funding Purpose** | Is this to **replace** equipment or **supplement/grow** the park? |
| **Activities** | What are the main activities? (Excavation, snow removal, crushing, demolition, civil engineering) |
| **Mining Subcontractor** | If mining sub, is equipment underground? Is there a ramp? (Important for possible recovery) |
| **Park Size** | How many units in the fleet? |
| **Key Customers** | Who are they? How often do they pay? |
| **Work in Progress** | Detailed WIP breakdown |
| **Pipeline/Projections** | Long-term work? Procurement? Contracts awarded or in the pipeline? |
| **Surety Company** | Who bonds their work? (Surety = bonding company for construction contracts) |
| **Seasonal Cash Flow** | Is cash flow seasonal? Interest payments only needed during off-season? |
| **Site Visit** | Have you visited the premises? If not, get a Google Street View photo + copies of utility bills |

### Forestry

| Topic | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Operating History** | In operation since when? |
| **Short-Term Lender** | Who? Approved limit? Most recent balance? |
| **Funding Purpose** | Replace or supplement the park? |
| **Key Customers** | Since when? Name of sawmill? Short wood or long wood? Private or public land? |
| **Payment Terms** | How often are they paid? |
| **Volume & Revenue** | How many cubic meters per year? Average revenue per cubic meter? Average cost per cubic meter? |
| **Equipment Location** | Where are the machines located? |
| **Off-Season** | Need interest payment only during the off-season? |
| **Integration** | Full integration? (Harvesting, transportation, shipping) |
| **Park Size** | Fleet size? |
| **Pipeline** | Long-term work commitments? |
| **Site Visit** | Visit the premises? If not, Google Street View + utility bills |

**Why This Matters:** These industry-specific questions fill in the gaps that financial statements can't tell you. A construction company's financial health depends heavily on its contract pipeline; a forestry company's depends on sawmill relationships and wood volumes. Without these details, the 4Bloc is incomplete.

## SECTION 10: Documents to Always Request

For **every large deal spread**, you should request the following documents upfront. Don't wait until credit asks for them — be proactive.

### The Must-Have List

| # | Document | Why You Need It |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Full financials including notes (3 years)** | You need 3 years to establish trends — one year tells you nothing about direction |
| 2 | **Interim statements** | Shows current-year trajectory — are things better or worse than the last fiscal year? |
| 3 | **AR and AP listings** (if over $1M exposure) | Needed to assess customer concentration, quality of receivables, and payment patterns |
| 4 | **Confirmation of operating line and balance** | Verifies LOC availability and current utilization — critical for liquidity analysis |
| 5 | **Org chart** (if group of companies) | Need to understand the corporate structure, who owns what, and how entities relate |
| 6 | **Contract details** | Are they taking on a new contract? Do they have any active contracts? What's the pipeline? |
| 7 | **Projected revenues** for the current year | Forward-looking context — does the company expect to grow, maintain, or decline? |
| 8 | **3 months bank statements** | Shows current cash balance and cash flow patterns — **especially important if cash balance is declining YoY** |

**Why This Matters:** Missing documents slow down deals and create back-and-forth with credit. Requesting everything upfront shows professionalism and speeds up the approval process. The 3-month bank statements are particularly important — they prove the cash position is real and current, not just a snapshot from a year-old fiscal year-end.

## SECTION 11: Work in Progress (WIP)

WIP is a concept you'll encounter frequently in **construction company** financials. Understanding it is important because it affects revenue, margins, and risk assessment.

### What Is WIP?

**Work in Progress (WIP)** = The estimated value of work that has been **performed but not yet invoiced**. It is recorded as revenue on the financial statements even though the company hasn't been paid yet.

### Why It Matters for Spreads

- WIP **inflates revenue** on the income statement — the company is recognizing revenue for work done, but the cash hasn't come in yet
- This means **revenue on the income statement may not match cash received**
- WIP also shows up on the balance sheet as a current asset, which can inflate the current ratio and working capital

### Questions to Ask About WIP

| Question | Why You're Asking |
|---|---|
| **Which contract is the WIP related to?** | You need to know what project this revenue is tied to |
| **Which customer?** | Is the customer reputable and likely to pay? |
| **What is the risk you won't get paid?** | If the WIP is for a risky customer or a disputed contract, that "revenue" may never turn into cash |
| **What percentage of total revenue is WIP?** | If WIP represents a large portion of revenue, the company's actual cash generation is lower than reported |

**Why This Matters:** A construction company can show $10M in revenue, but if $3M of that is WIP on a single municipal contract that's under dispute, the real cash-generating revenue is only $7M. WIP is real revenue from an accounting standpoint, but you need to understand the collection risk behind it.

Keep these numbers in your head. They are the benchmarks you'll reference in every spread.

### Profitability Benchmarks

| Metric | Good Benchmark | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|

| **EBITDA Margin** | Over 10% | Under 10% declining YoY |

| Metric | Good Benchmark | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|

### Liquidity Benchmarks

| Metric | Good Benchmark | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Current Ratio** | Over 1.0x | Under 1.0x |

### Leverage Benchmarks

| Metric | Good Benchmark | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| **Debt-to-Equity** | Under 3.0x | Over 3.0x without mitigants |
| **D/TNW** | Under 3.5x | Over 3.5x |

### Financial Statement Quality

| Type | Reliability | Action Needed |
|---|---|---|
| **Audited** | Highest | Trust the numbers |

| **NTR** | Lowest | Substantiate key numbers with bank statements, contracts, tax returns |

## SECTION 13: Practice Questions

### Short Answer

**Q1:** A client's FCCR is 0.85x. What does this tell you, and what two things can mitigate it?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

An FCCR of 0.85x means the client **cannot fully cover its fixed charges from operations** — for every $1 of fixed costs, it only generates $0.85. This is below the 1.0x minimum. Two mitigants: **(1)** A cash down payment on the proposed deal (reduces the incremental fixed charge), and **(2)** Personal Guarantee (PG) support from the owner.

</details>

---

**Q2:** You're reviewing a client's financials and the FCCR shows 2.8x, which seems surprisingly high. The statements are NTR. What should you check?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Check whether the financial statements actually report **CPLTD (Current Portion of Long-Term Debt)**. NTR financials sometimes don't break out CPLTD, which means the denominator of the FCCR calculation is missing a key component. This would make the FCCR appear **artificially inflated**. Note in your analysis: "FCCR may be inflated as financials do not report CPLTD."

</details>

---

**Q3:** A construction company shows $12M in revenue, but $4M of it is WIP from a single contract with a municipality. Is this a concern? What questions should you ask?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Yes, this is a concern. One-third of reported revenue hasn't been invoiced yet and is tied to a single customer. Questions to ask: **(1)** Which contract is the WIP tied to? **(2)** What is the payment status — has any portion been billed? **(3)** Is the municipality in good standing and likely to pay? **(4)** Is there any dispute on the contract? If the WIP is uncollectable, effective cash revenue is only $8M, which changes every margin and coverage ratio.

</details>

**Q4:** A client's current ratio is 0.75x. What does this mean, and what should you look for to potentially improve the picture?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

A current ratio of 0.75x means the client has **$0.75 in current assets for every $1.00 in current liabilities** — they cannot cover short-term obligations from short-term resources alone. Look for: **(1)** Cash held in a related company or reserve that isn't on this entity's balance sheet, **(2)** LOC availability that provides an additional liquidity buffer, **(3)** Whether a refinancing is planned that would inject working capital, **(4)** Bank statements showing actual cash balances higher than reported on the fiscal year-end balance sheet.

</details>

**Q5:** A company has a D/E ratio of 4.2x but SFD has dropped from $6M to $4.5M over two years. How do you present this?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Present the ratio honestly but frame the **trend** as a positive: "While the company remains highly leveraged at 4.2x D/E, it is **actively deleveraging** — Senior Funded Debt has decreased 25% from $6.0M to $4.5M over two years, indicating a disciplined approach to debt reduction." Then add any mitigants: PG support, strong TNW, cash down payment on the proposed deal.

</details>

**Q7:** What is the difference between Equity and TNW? Why do credit analysts prefer TNW?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Equity** = Total Assets − Total Liabilities (includes everything). **TNW (Tangible Net Worth)** = Equity − Intangible Assets (goodwill, patents, investments in related companies). Credit analysts prefer TNW because intangible assets **cannot be easily liquidated** — you can't sell goodwill to cover a missed payment. TNW represents the hard, realizable value of the company's net assets.

</details>

**Q8:** You're spreading a forestry company. Name 5 industry-specific questions you would ask that go beyond the financials.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

1. Who are the key customers (which sawmills)? Short wood or long wood? Private or public land?
2. How many cubic meters per year, and what is the average revenue and cost per cubic meter?
3. Is the company fully integrated? (Harvesting, transportation, shipping)
4. Do they need interest-only payments during the off-season?
5. Where are the machines physically located? Have you visited the premises?

</details>

**Q9:** A client has very little cash on the balance sheet, but you suspect they have more liquidity than reported. What do you do?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Ask for **bank statements** or an explanation of where cash is being held. Common explanations: cash held in a **cash reserve account**, cash held in a **related company**, or cash sitting in an **investment account** not captured on the operating entity's balance sheet. Obtaining bank statements is the best way to verify the actual cash position and prove a stronger liquidity story to credit.

</details>

**Q10:** In the 4Bloc, which quadrant needs to be the strongest and why?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The **top left quadrant — Company & Situation (KYC)**. This is where first impressions are formed. Credit analysts need to clearly understand what the company does, who runs it, how experienced management is, and what the deal request is. A weak Company & Situation section undermines everything that follows, no matter how strong the financials are. You need a **strong top left quadrant**.

</details>

### Scenario-Based Questions

**Scenario 1:** You receive NTR financials for a construction company requesting $2.5M in equipment financing. Revenue is $15M with an EBITDA margin of 8%. FCCR is 1.3x. Current ratio is 0.9x. D/E is 3.5x. $3M of revenue is WIP. They have a $1M LOC with $400K available. What are the key points you'd highlight in your spread?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Concerns to flag:**
1. **NTR financials** — lowest reliability. Need bank statements and supporting docs to substantiate key numbers
2. **EBITDA margin at 8%** — below the 10% benchmark. Explain why (high subcontractor costs? one-time expense?)
3. **Current ratio at 0.9x** — below 1.0x. Company may struggle with short-term obligations
4. **D/E at 3.5x** — above the 3.0x benchmark. Company is highly leveraged
5. **$3M WIP** — 20% of revenue hasn't been invoiced. Which contract? Which customer? Collection risk?

**Positives to highlight:**
1. **FCCR at 1.3x** — above 1.0x, company can service its fixed charges (BUT verify CPLTD is included since NTR)
2. **$400K LOC available** — provides some liquidity buffer
3. **$15M revenue** — sizeable company with established operations

**Mitigants to propose:**
- Request cash down payment to reduce leverage impact
- PG from owner with strong PNW
- Obtain bank statements to prove cash position
- Request contract details and AR aging to validate WIP and customer quality

</details>

---

**Scenario 2:** A forestry company has been in business for 12 years. Revenue has grown from $4M to $6M over 3 years. Net profit margin is 12%. FCCR is 1.6x. But the LOC is fully drawn ($0 available), and 70% of revenue comes from one sawmill. How do you present this in the key considerations?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Key Considerations — Positives:**
- Strong TIB: 12 years in business — experienced operator
- Revenue growth: 50% increase over 3 years ($4M → $6M)
- Net profit margin of 12% — above the 10% benchmark, strong profitability
- FCCR of 1.6x — comfortable coverage of fixed charges

**Key Considerations — Concerns with Mitigants:**
- **Customer concentration risk:** 70% of revenue from one sawmill. If that relationship ends, revenue drops to $1.8M.
  - **Mitigant:** Assess the quality and reputation of the sawmill. Long-standing relationship (how many years?). Is the sawmill a major player in the market? Are there contracts in place?
- **LOC fully drawn:** $0 availability creates a liquidity concern — no revolving credit cushion
  - **Mitigant:** Strong FCCR of 1.6x shows cash flow coverage is adequate. Request bank statements to verify actual cash position. Ask if LOC is seasonal (drawn in spring breakup, repaid in peak season)

**Transaction Rationale:** Despite concentration and LOC utilization, the company has a 12-year track record, strong profitability, and solid debt service coverage. Recommend requesting 3-month bank statements and a sawmill contract to substantiate the relationship.

</details>
