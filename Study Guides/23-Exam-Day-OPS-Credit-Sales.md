## SECTION 3: Document Validation Checklist

This is the **complete OPS reference** for reviewing deal files. Siraaj shared this checklist and asked that you review it and start checking these items on every new file.

### Void Cheque

| Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Complete legal name** | The lessee's full legal name must appear on the void cheque — must match the contract |
| **Must be a void cheque** | A void cheque is required, **NOT a handwritten PAD (Pre-Authorized Debit) form** |

**Why This Matters:** The void cheque confirms the bank account details AND verifies the legal name of the entity making payments. A handwritten PAD form can contain errors or be fraudulent — a printed void cheque from the bank is the standard of proof.

**Memory Trick:**
> **"Void cheque = verified. PAD form = NOT accepted."**

### Contract

| Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Legal name matches corp profile** | The legal name on the contract must exactly match the corporate profile (business registration) |
| **PG name matches ID** | The Personal Guarantor's name on the contract must match their government-issued ID |
| **Signature matches ID** | The signature on the contract must match the signature on the ID |
| **Equipment description matches BOS** | The equipment listed in the contract must match what's on the Bill of Sale |
| **Vendor name correct** | The vendor name must be correct on both the authorization and acceptance sections |
| **Initials on all relevant pages** | Every page that requires initials must be initialed — missing initials = incomplete contract |

**Why This Matters:** Every mismatch is a potential compliance failure or fraud indicator. If the legal name on the contract doesn't match the corporate profile, you may have the wrong entity. If the PG name doesn't match their ID, someone else may be guaranteeing under a false identity.

### Bill of Sale (BOS) and Schedule of Additional Fees (SAF)

This is where the **product type determines the billing entity** — a key distinction the OPS exam will test:

| Check | If It's a LEASE | If It's a LOAN |
|---|---|---|
| **Billed To** | **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc** | **The Client (lessee/borrower)** |
| **Ship To** | Client, with the client's address | Client, with the client's address |

**Why the billing entity changes:**
- In a **lease**, MHCCA Leasing owns the equipment and leases it to the client. The vendor bills MHCCA because MHCCA is the purchaser/owner
- In a **loan**, the client owns the equipment directly. The vendor bills the client because the client is the buyer — MHCCA just provides financing

**Additional BOS Requirements:**

| Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Full equipment description** | Make, model, serial number, year — must be complete and match the contract |
| **Does not apply to SAF** | Equipment description requirement is for BOS only, not SAF |
| **Cost & taxes are correct** | Verify the purchase price and all applicable taxes (GST/HST/QST) are calculated correctly |
| **Deposit noted** | If there is a deposit/down payment, it must be noted **after taxes** on the BOS |
| **Current dated** | The BOS must be current — not backdated or future-dated |
| **Tax numbers noted** | GST/HST/QST registration numbers must be listed |

**Memory Trick:**
> **"Lease = Billed to MHCCL (they own it). Loan = Billed to Client (they own it). Ship-to is ALWAYS the client."**

### ID Verification

| Check | Requirement |
|---|---|
| **Clear** | The ID must be clearly legible — not blurry, faded, or damaged |
| **No discrepancies with name** | The name on the ID must match the name on the contract and PG documents exactly |
| **Not expired** | The ID must be current and valid — expired IDs are not accepted |

See **Guide 20 (Driver's License Validation)** for province-by-province format details and security features.

## SECTION 4: Insurance Requirements

Insurance validation is a critical OPS exam topic. The requirements differ based on whether the deal is a **lease or a loan.**

### Universal Requirements (All Deals)

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Liability coverage** | Minimum **$2 million** |
| **Deductible** | Must be **below $25,000** |

### Lease-Specific Insurance Requirements

When the deal is a **lease**, MHCCA Leasing owns the equipment. The insurance must protect MHCCA's ownership interest:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Named entity** | **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc** |
| **Listed as** | **Additional Insured AND Loss Payee** |
| **OR** | **5A Endorsement** (SEF 5A — standard endorsement that gives the lessor priority claim) AND MHCCA listed as **Lessor** |

**What is a 5A Endorsement?**
A **SEF 5A (Standard Endorsement Form 5A)** is a standard insurance industry form that protects the **lessor's interest** in the equipment. It means that if the equipment is damaged or destroyed, MHCCA gets paid first (as the owner) regardless of what happens with the client's claim. This is the alternative to being listed as additional insured + loss payee.

### Loan-Specific Insurance Requirements

When the deal is a **loan**, the client owns the equipment but MHCCA has a **security interest** (lien). The insurance protects that lien:

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Named entity** | **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, Inc** (NOT "Leasing" — different legal entity) |
| **Listed as** | **Loss Payee** |

### The Critical Distinction

| | Lease | Loan |
|---|---|---|
| **Entity name** | Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada **Leasing**, Inc | Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, Inc |
| **Insurance role** | Additional Insured + Loss Payee **OR** 5A Endorsement + Lessor | Loss Payee only |
| **Why** | MHCCA Leasing **owns** the equipment — needs full protection | MHCCA has a **lien** — needs payment priority if asset is destroyed |

**Memory Trick:**
> **Lease = "Leasing, Inc" = Additional Insured + Loss Payee (or 5A). Loan = "Canada, Inc" = Loss Payee only.**

> **The legal entity changes because the ownership structure changes.** In a lease, the Leasing entity holds title. In a loan, the main entity holds the security interest.

**Why This Matters for the Exam:** The OPS exam will likely test whether you know which entity name goes on the insurance AND what designation they receive. Getting the entity name wrong (Leasing vs non-Leasing) or the designation wrong (additional insured vs loss payee only) is a compliance failure.

## SECTION 5: BOS and SAF Billing Rules

### The Rule in One Table

| Document | Lease | Loan |
|---|---|---|
| **BOS — Billed To** | Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc | Client (legal name) |
| **BOS — Ship To** | Client (with client's address) | Client (with client's address) |
| **SAF** | No equipment description required | No equipment description required |

### Why "Ship To" Is Always the Client

Even in a lease (where MHCCA Leasing buys the equipment), the **ship-to address is always the client's address** because:

1. The equipment is delivered to where the client will **use it**
2. The ship-to address determines the **provincial tax jurisdiction** — taxes are charged based on where the equipment is delivered, not where the buyer (MHCCA) is located
3. Getting the ship-to wrong means **incorrect tax calculation** — which is a compliance and audit issue

### BOS Checklist (Consolidated)

Use this as a quick-scan checklist for every deal:

- [ ] Billed to correct entity (Leasing for lease, Client for loan)
- [ ] Ship to client with correct client address
- [ ] Full equipment description (make, model, serial, year)
- [ ] Cost and taxes are correct
- [ ] Deposit/down payment noted after taxes (if applicable)
- [ ] Current dated
- [ ] Tax registration numbers (GST/HST/QST) noted

## SECTION 6: Ship-To Address

### Why It Matters for Tax

The ship-to address on the BOS determines **which province's tax rules apply** to the transaction. This is important because:

| Province | GST | PST/HST | Combined |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alberta | 5% | 0% | 5% |
| Ontario | 5% | 8% (HST) | 13% |
| Quebec | 5% | 9.975% (QST) | 14.975% |
| BC | 5% | 7% | 12% |
| Saskatchewan | 5% | 6% | 11% |

If equipment is shipped to Alberta but the BOS lists an Ontario address, the client is overcharged by 8% in tax. Conversely, if Ontario equipment is listed with an Alberta address, taxes are undercollected — creating a liability for MHCCA.

**The Rule:** The ship-to address must be the **actual location where the equipment will be used/delivered.** This is the client's operational address, not MHCCA's office.

## SECTION 7: Hazmat Deals

### Why Hazmat Must Be Structured as a LOAN, Not a Lease

This is directly from the RAC (Guide 14) but the **rationale** wasn't explained there. Siraaj clarified why:

**The Rule:** All hazmat (hazardous materials) transactions must be structured as a **LOAN**, not a lease.

**Three Reasons:**

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Insurance and liability** | In a lease, MHCCA **owns** the equipment. If a hazmat truck is involved in a spill or accident, the **owner** (MHCCA) could face environmental liability claims. Structuring as a loan means the **client owns** the equipment and bears the ownership liability |
| **Public perception** | MHCCA does not want to be publicly associated as the **owner** of equipment involved in hazmat incidents. A loan creates distance — MHCCA is the lender, not the owner |
| **Asset use determines structure** | It's not just about the type of asset (e.g., a truck). It's about **what the asset is used for.** A regular truck = can be leased. A truck **hauling hazardous materials** = must be a loan. The use case, not just the asset class, determines the structure |

**Key Point for the Exam:** The same physical truck could be either a lease or a loan depending on whether it's used for **hazmat transport.** This is a "trick question" topic — don't just look at the equipment type, look at the **intended use.**

**Also Applies to Buses:**
- All **bus transactions** must also be treated as **loans** with increased insurance requirements
- Same rationale: liability exposure from passengers

## SECTION 8: The 20% Residual Rule

### Off-Balance Sheet — The Fifth Detail

Guide 06 covers the four off-balance sheet criteria. Siraaj added a **critical detail** that isn't in that guide:

### The Existing 4 Criteria (from Guide 06)

| # | Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Lease term vs useful life | < 75% |
| 2 | Present value of payments vs FMV | < 90% |
| 3 | Bargain purchase option | NONE allowed |
| 4 | Automatic transfer of ownership | NONE allowed |

### The Additional Detail — Residual Value Must Be > 20%

Siraaj clarified that for a lease to remain off-balance sheet:

> **The residual value must be higher than 20% of the original equipment value.**

**What this means in practice:**

| Equipment Cost | Minimum Residual Value | Maximum PV of Payments |
|---|---|---|
| $100,000 | $20,000 (20%) | $80,000 |
| $500,000 | $100,000 (20%) | $400,000 |
| $1,000,000 | $200,000 (20%) | $800,000 |

**How this connects to Criterion 2 (the 90% rule):**
- Criterion 2 says PV of payments < 90% of FMV
- The 20% residual rule is more conservative — it effectively limits payments to 80% of FMV
- **The 20% residual rule is MHCCA's internal policy** that provides a cushion above the accounting standard's 90% threshold

**Why This Matters:** If MHCCA sets a residual at 15% of FMV, the lease payments would be 85% of FMV — still under the 90% accounting threshold, but **too low for MHCCA's internal policy.** This is a guardrail to ensure MHCCA has meaningful ownership interest (skin in the game) and that the off-balance sheet classification is defensible.

**Impact on Rate and Structure:**
- A higher residual means **lower lease payments** for the client (less of the equipment cost is being amortized)
- But it also means **more residual risk** for MHCCA (if the equipment is worth less than the residual at end of term, MHCCA takes a loss)
- The 20% minimum ensures MHCCA always retains a significant equipment interest

## SECTION 9: Compliance Thresholds

### When to Escalate — Dollar Triggers

Certain compliance actions are triggered at specific dollar thresholds. These are found in the credit and documentation policies.

| Threshold | Action Required |
|---|---|
| **Over $1M exposure** | Org chart required (if group of companies) — see Guide 21 (Spreads) |
| **Over $1M** | AR and AP listings required — see Guide 21 (Spreads) |
| **Over $3 million** | Documentation must be sent to **Legal** for review |

### The $3M Legal Review Threshold

> When total deal exposure reaches **$3 million**, the documentation package must be escalated to MHCCA's legal team for review before proceeding.

**What this means for you:**
- Deals under $3M follow standard documentation and approval procedures
- Deals at or above $3M require an additional step: **legal review of all documentation**
- This applies to **total exposure**, not just the current transaction — if a client already has $2.5M with MHCCA and you're adding $600K, the total ($3.1M) triggers the threshold

**Why This Matters for the Exam:** This is exactly the kind of "policy vs practice" question Siraaj warned about. In practice, you might not always see this happen. But the **policy** says $3M = legal review. The exam tests policy, not practice.

## SECTION 10: Third-Party Due Diligence

### Vendor and Broker Approval

The third-party due diligence process applies to both **vendors (dealers)** and **brokers** that MHCCA works with. Siraaj described it as covering both perspectives.

### What Is Third-Party Due Diligence?

Before MHCCA accepts deals from a vendor or broker, that third party must be **vetted and approved.** This process ensures:

- The vendor/broker is a **legitimate business**
- They have no history of **fraud or compliance violations**
- They meet MHCCA's standards for **business practices and ethics**
- They are not on any **blocked or restricted lists**

### Vendor Perspective

| Item | What's Checked |
|---|---|
| **Business legitimacy** | Is this a real, operating business? Corporate registration, physical location, website |
| **Industry reputation** | Are they known in the industry? How long have they been operating? |
| **Financial stability** | Can this vendor support ongoing business? (Warranties, service commitments) |
| **Compliance history** | Any past issues with fraud, misrepresentation, or regulatory violations? |
| **Blocked list check** | Is this vendor on MHCCA's blocked vendor list? |

### Broker Perspective

| Item | What's Checked |
|---|---|
| **Licensing** | Is the broker properly licensed in their jurisdiction? |
| **Reputation** | Industry standing, references, track record |
| **Deal quality history** | Past deals submitted — default rates, documentation quality, fraud incidents |
| **Compliance** | Anti-money laundering (AML), sanctions screening |
| **Blocked list check** | Is this broker on MHCCA's blocked broker list? |

### The Blocked Lists

From Guide 12 (TAM Program): **"If you submit a deal involving a blocked vendor or broker, it will be rejected."** These lists exist because entities have been flagged for:
- Fraud
- Compliance issues
- Serious business practice concerns

> **Always check the blocked lists before submitting a deal.** This applies to TAM deals AND standard deals.

### Dealer Classification

Siraaj mentioned dealer approval and classifications. From a credit perspective, you should understand:

| Classification | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Approved vendor** | Full access to submit deals — has passed due diligence |
| **Restricted vendor** | Can submit deals but with additional scrutiny or limitations |
| **Blocked vendor** | Cannot submit deals — flagged for fraud, compliance, or serious issues |

**Why This Matters:** The OPS exam may ask about what happens when a deal comes from an unapproved or blocked vendor. The answer is always: **it gets rejected until due diligence is completed and the vendor is approved.**

## SECTION 11: KYC and Fraud Prevention

### Consolidated Reference

KYC (Know Your Client) and fraud prevention appear across multiple guides. Here's the consolidated view for exam prep.

### Why KYC Matters

> Siraaj: "Understanding a little bit about the KYC and preventing fraud — those are the big things that we do on a day-to-day basis."

KYC is **common sense at its core** (Siraaj's words) but has specific compliance requirements:

| Requirement | What You Need |
|---|---|
| **Verify identity** | Government-issued photo ID — must be clear, current, matching (see Guide 20) |

### Fraud Red Flags

Be alert for these indicators:

| Red Flag | Why It's Suspicious |
|---|---|
| **ID doesn't match documents** | Name, address, or signature discrepancies between ID and contract/PG documents |
| **New business with large request** | No track record but asking for significant financing |
| **Rush to close** | Pressure to skip due diligence or expedite approval |
| **Inconsistent information** | Details don't line up between application, financials, and verbal conversations |
| **Equipment use doesn't match business** | A flower shop financing heavy construction equipment |
| **Vendor/broker on blocked list** | Previously flagged for issues |
| **Reluctance to provide documents** | Won't provide financials, ID, or bank statements |
| **Related party transactions** | Money moving between related companies without clear business reason |
| **Duplicate applications** | Same client or equipment appearing in multiple applications |

### What KYC Covers (From the Policy)

The KYC section of the exam is described as "largely common sense" but covers:

1. **Why** you need to know your client (regulatory compliance, risk management, anti-money laundering)
2. **What** you need to verify (identity, business, ownership, purpose)
3. **How** you verify it (documents, databases, site visits, references)
4. **When** to escalate (red flags, high-risk indicators, sanctions matches)

## SECTION 12: Lessee vs Guarantor

### Jurisdiction Matters

Siraaj mentioned that the distinction between **lessee** and **guarantor** can change based on **jurisdiction.** This is particularly relevant for Quebec vs common-law provinces.

### Basic Definitions

| Role | Who They Are |
|---|---|
| **Lessee** | The entity (company or person) that is **leasing the equipment** — they are the borrower, the one making payments |
| **Guarantor (PG)** | A person (usually the owner/principal) who **personally guarantees** the lessee's obligations — if the lessee defaults, the guarantor is liable |

### Why Jurisdiction Matters

In **common-law provinces** (Ontario, BC, Alberta, etc.):
- The lessee and guarantor roles are straightforward
- The lessee signs the lease, the guarantor signs the personal guarantee
- Standard security interest rules (PPSA) apply

In **Quebec** (civil law):
- Different terminology may apply (e.g., "caution" for guarantor)
- **Bill 96 requirements** layer on top (French-first documentation — see Guide 17)
- Registration is through the **RDPRM** (Registre des droits personnels et réels mobiliers), not PPSA
- Certain legal concepts (like hypothecs vs liens) differ from common-law equivalents

### Exam Application

The exam may present a scenario where:
- A client is incorporated in one province but operating in another
- The guarantor resides in a different province than the lessee
- You need to determine which jurisdiction's rules apply

**General Rule:** The jurisdiction is determined by the **location of the lessee (the borrowing entity)**, not the guarantor. But insurance and registration requirements follow the **location of the equipment.**

## SECTION 13: OPS Exam Traps

### Common Trick Questions and How to Handle Them

Siraaj warned that the OPS exam has **counterintuitive content** and **trick questions.** Here are the most likely traps:

### Trap 1: Lease vs Loan Entity Names

**Question type:** "Which entity should be listed on the insurance for a [lease/loan]?"

**Trap:** Mixing up "Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada **Leasing**, Inc" (leases) with "Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, Inc" (loans). These are **different legal entities** and using the wrong one is a documentation failure.

### Trap 2: BOS Billing — Who Gets Billed?

**Question type:** "Who should the bill of sale be addressed to?"

**Trap:** Assuming it's always the client or always MHCCA. **It depends on the product:**
- Lease = Billed to MHCCL (they're buying the equipment)
- Loan = Billed to the client (they're buying the equipment, MHCCA just funds it)

### Trap 3: Void Cheque vs PAD Form

**Question type:** "Is a handwritten PAD form acceptable?"

**Trap:** Assuming any banking document works. **Only a void cheque is acceptable** — a handwritten PAD form is NOT.

### Trap 4: Hazmat/Bus = Loan Only

**Question type:** "A client wants to lease a truck that will be used for hazmat transport..."

**Trap:** Approving a lease structure. **Hazmat and bus deals must be loans, regardless of client preference.** It's the asset USE, not the asset TYPE, that determines this.

### Trap 5: Insurance Designations

**Question type:** "What insurance designation does MHCCA need on a lease?"

**Trap:** Saying only "loss payee." For leases, MHCCA needs **additional insured AND loss payee** (or 5A endorsement). Loss payee alone is for **loans only.**

### Trap 6: Policy vs Practice

**Question type:** Any question where the "right" answer based on what you've seen at work differs from what the policy document says.

**Trap:** Answering based on experience instead of policy. **The exam tests policy, not practice.** If the policy says X and everyone does Y, the correct exam answer is X.

### Trap 7: Ship-To Address

**Question type:** "Where should the equipment be shipped on a lease BOS?"

**Trap:** Thinking MHCCA's address since MHCCA is buying the equipment. **Ship-to is ALWAYS the client's address** — that's where the equipment goes, and it determines tax jurisdiction.

### Trap 8: Deposit Placement on BOS

**Question type:** "Where should the deposit be reflected on the BOS?"

**Trap:** Showing the deposit before taxes. **The deposit must be noted AFTER taxes** on the BOS.

## SECTION 14: Credit Exam Case Studies

### How to Approach Them

The two case studies are the highest-value portion of the credit exam. Here's how to approach them systematically:

### Step 1: Read Everything First

Don't start writing immediately. Read the entire case study. Identify:
- What **industry** is this client in?
- What **credit rating** would they likely fall under?
- What **asset** are they financing?
- What **dollar amount** are they requesting?

### Step 2: Apply the RAC

Go through the RAC criteria for that industry:
- Is this industry/asset **eligible**?
- What are the **auto-decision limits** for this credit rating?
- What is the **max term/amortization**?
- Is a **down payment required?** How much?
- Is a **PG required?** What score threshold?
- Are there any **special requirements** (site visit, UNL, etc.)?

### Step 3: Identify the Structure

Based on the information given, determine:
- **Lease or loan?** (Consider hazmat, bus, client preference, off-balance sheet need)
- **Term and amortization** — within RAC limits
- **Down payment** — as required by credit rating
- **PG/CG requirements** — based on deal size and credit rating
- **Documents needed** — based on deal size and complexity

### Step 4: Flag Risks and Mitigants

This is what separates a good answer from a great one:
- What are the **concerns** based on the information? (Weak financials? New business? Customer concentration?)
- What are the **mitigants?** (Strong PG? Cash down? Long TIB? Strong contracts?)
- Reference Guide 21 (Spreads) for the analysis framework

### Step 5: Write Clearly and Concisely

You're handwriting under time pressure. Be **structured:**
- Lead with your recommendation (approve/decline/approve with conditions)
- List the key RAC requirements and how this deal meets (or doesn't meet) them
- Flag concerns with mitigants
- Keep it organized — bullet points are fine

## SECTION 17: Practice Questions

### OPS-Style Questions

**Q1:** A deal file includes a handwritten PAD form instead of a void cheque. Is this acceptable?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No.** A void cheque is required — a handwritten PAD form is **not acceptable.** The void cheque confirms bank account details and verifies the legal name of the entity through the bank's own printed records.

</details>

---

**Q2:** On a lease deal, the BOS is billed to the client. Is this correct?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No.** On a lease, the BOS must be billed to **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc** because MHCCA Leasing is purchasing the equipment (they are the owner/lessor). If it's a loan, then the BOS is billed to the client. The billing entity depends on who is buying the equipment.

</details>

---

**Q3:** What is the minimum liability coverage required on all deals, and what is the maximum acceptable deductible?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Minimum liability coverage: **$2 million.** Maximum deductible: **$25,000** (deductible must be below $25K).

</details>

---

**Q4:** For a lease, what insurance designation does MHCCA need? Name the entity and the designation.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Entity: **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc.** Designation: **Additional Insured AND Loss Payee**, OR alternatively a **5A Endorsement (SEF 5A)** with MHCCA listed as **Lessor.** Either option is acceptable.

</details>

---

**Q5:** For a loan, what insurance designation does MHCCA need? Name the entity and the designation.

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

Entity: **Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, Inc** (note: NOT "Leasing"). Designation: **Loss Payee** only. No need for additional insured or 5A endorsement because MHCCA doesn't own the equipment — they only have a security interest (lien).

</details>

---

**Q6:** A client wants to lease a truck. You later learn the truck will be used to transport hazardous materials. Can you proceed with the lease?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No.** All hazmat transactions must be structured as a **LOAN, not a lease.** This is because in a lease, MHCCA would own the equipment and could face environmental and liability exposure from hazmat incidents. The same rule applies to buses. It's the **asset use** (hazmat transport), not the asset type (truck), that triggers this requirement. The deal must be restructured as a loan with increased insurance requirements.

</details>

---

**Q7:** Where should the deposit be noted on a Bill of Sale?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The deposit must be noted **after taxes.** The BOS should show the full equipment cost, applicable taxes, and then the deposit amount applied after taxes have been calculated.

</details>

---

**Q8:** A deal is for $2.8 million. An existing client already has $500K in exposure with MHCCA. Do you need to send documentation to legal?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Yes.** The total exposure is $3.3M ($2.8M + $500K existing), which exceeds the **$3 million threshold** for mandatory legal review. Documentation must be sent to Legal before proceeding.

</details>

---

**Q9:** What does it mean if the ship-to address on a BOS is different from the client's operating address?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

The ship-to address determines the **provincial tax jurisdiction.** If the ship-to address is incorrect, the wrong provincial taxes may be applied, creating a compliance and audit issue. The ship-to must be the **actual location where the equipment will be delivered and used** — this is the client's operational address, and it determines which province's GST/HST/PST/QST rates apply.

</details>

---

**Q10:** You receive an ID for a Personal Guarantor. The name reads "Robert J. Smith" but the contract says "Bob Smith." Is this acceptable?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**No — there is a discrepancy.** The PG name on the contract must **match the ID exactly.** "Bob" is not the same as "Robert J." The contract should be corrected to match the legal name on the government-issued ID before proceeding. Name discrepancies are a fraud red flag and a documentation compliance failure.

</details>

### Credit Case Study Practice

**Mini Case Study:** Joe's Excavation Inc. operates in the construction industry in Ontario. Joe has been in business for 6 years, has a credit score of 680, and is requesting $350,000 to finance a new 2026 CAT 320 excavator. His most recent financials show $4M revenue, net profit margin of 8%, and D/E of 2.5x. He has an existing $150K lease with MHCCA in good standing.

Using the RAC, analyze this deal. Can MHCCA do it? What structure? What requirements?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Industry:** Construction
**Asset:** CAT 320 excavator — **Tier 1** equipment (CAT is Tier 1 in construction per RAC)
**Amount:** $350K
**Likely Credit Rating:** Based on 6 years in business and 680 score, likely **CR3** (would need full underwriting to confirm)

**RAC Requirements for CR3 Construction, Tier 1 New Equipment:**
- Auto-decision limit: **$200,000** — this $350K deal **exceeds auto-decision.** It goes to an analyst for manual review
- Max amortization (new T1, CR3): **96 months**
- Down payment: **5-10% recommended** (prefer 10%) = $17,500-$35,000
- PG required: Yes, with minimum score threshold (680 meets the general requirement)
- Total MHCCA exposure will be $500K ($350K + $150K existing) — under the $1M org chart threshold

**Analysis:**
- **Positives:** 6 years TIB, Tier 1 asset (CAT), existing relationship in good standing, revenue of $4M shows established business, D/E at 2.5x is within the 3.0x benchmark
- **Concerns:** Net profit margin at 8% is below the 10% benchmark — should explain why (could be heavy depreciation on fleet). Credit score of 680 is adequate but not strong
- **Mitigants:** Recommend 10% down payment ($35K), PG from Joe, existing relationship history with MHCCA

**Structure:** Loan or capital lease (ask client preference), up to 96 months amortization on new T1 equipment, with PG and 10% down payment. If the client wants off-balance sheet treatment, FMV lease is an option (must meet 4 criteria + 20% residual).

**Documents needed:** 3 years financials, interim statements, void cheque, PG info (ID, credit check), BOS from dealer, insurance (if lease: MHCCL as additional insured + loss payee, $2M liability, deductible < $25K)

</details>

### Sales Scenario Practice

**Scenario:** You're at a trade show and meet the owner of a mid-size trucking company. She has 40 Class 8 trucks, has been in business for 12 years, and is thinking about adding 5 new Kenworth T680s worth $900K total. She currently finances everything through her bank. What do you discuss?

<details>
<summary>Answer</summary>

**Conversation approach (LEAD methodology):**

**Legitimize:** Introduce MHCCA, explain your role and the company's position as a specialized equipment finance provider (subsidiary of Mitsubishi HC Capital — one of the largest non-bank financial institutions globally).

**Explore:** Ask questions to understand her situation:
- What does her current financing structure look like? (Lease, loan, LOC?)
- What's frustrating about her bank? (Speed? Flexibility? Industry understanding?)
- Are the 5 new trucks replacing existing units or expanding the fleet?
- Does she want to own the equipment outright or is she open to leasing structures?
- Does she have contracts in place that justify the fleet expansion?

**Product knowledge to draw on:**
- Transport Class 8: Kenworth is **Tier 1** (top tier per RAC)
- With 12 years in business and 40 trucks, she's likely a strong credit — possibly CR1 or CR2
- At $900K, this is within standard flow business range
- Product options: Capital lease ($1 buyout), FMV lease (lower payments, off-balance-sheet if criteria met), or loan
- MHCCA advantage vs bank: **speed** (she complained about slow process), **industry expertise** (we specialize in transportation equipment), **flexibility** in structuring

**Accentuate:** Highlight that MHCCA understands trucking — we know Kenworth is premium equipment, we know fleet cycles, we know the industry. Unlike her bank, we're not generalists trying to learn her business. Plus, we can likely move faster on approval.

**Decide:** Ask for the opportunity to quote the deal. Get her contact info, ask for recent financials, and set up a follow-up meeting.

**What NOT to discuss:** Deep credit underwriting details, specific rates (you don't know her credit rating yet), or guarantees about approval. Keep it client-facing and consultative.

</details>
