# STUDY GUIDE: Answers to Most Common Objections

---

## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

This is your objection-handling playbook. When you're prospecting and calling dealers or potential partners, they're going to push back. That's normal. This document gives you **scripted responses** for the most common objections you'll hear on the phone.

The key principle behind every response: **Never accept the "no" at face value. Always pivot to securing a meeting.** Notice how every single response ends with asking for a specific meeting time.

---

## THE GOLDEN RULE OF OBJECTION HANDLING

Every response in this document follows the same pattern:

1. **Acknowledge** what the prospect said (show empathy, don't argue)
2. **Reframe** the situation to show value
3. **Close for the meeting** by offering specific times

This is important because you're not trying to sell over the phone. You're trying to **get the face-to-face meeting.** Remember the Vampire Rule from Combo Prospecting: "Once they let you in, it's over."

---

## OBJECTION 1: "What's your rate?"

### Why They Ask This

They want to compare you to other financing companies on price alone. If you give a flat rate, you become a commodity — just another number to compare.

### The Response

> *"Well that's exactly why I would like to meet you, since we offer **tailor-made financing solutions**, instead of flat rates. Would you be available to meet either Monday afternoon or Wednesday morning?"*

### Why This Works

You don't give a rate. Instead, you reframe MHCCA as a **custom solutions provider**, not a commodity lender. This positions you as someone who needs to understand their business before quoting — which is true, because the rate depends on the deal structure, credit profile, and asset type.

**Key phrase to remember:** "tailor-made financing solutions, instead of flat rates"

---

## OBJECTION 2: "Let me call you when I have something"

### Why They Say This

They're trying to politely get you off the phone. They don't have an immediate financing need and see no reason to talk now.

### The Response

> *"I understand you may not have anything to finance as we speak, and that's why **timing is great right now.** When you will have financing needs, you will already have an idea of what we can offer as attractive financing solutions to your clients. So would you be available to meet either Wednesday morning or Thursday afternoon?"*

### Why This Works

You flip the timing argument. Instead of "I don't need you now," you reframe it as "Now is the PERFECT time to meet BECAUSE you don't have anything urgent — we can have a relaxed conversation without pressure." When they do have a deal, they'll already know who to call.

**Key phrase to remember:** "timing is great right now"

---

## OBJECTION 3: "I am not interested"

### Why They Say This

The classic brush-off. They don't know enough about you to be interested, and they want to end the conversation.

### The Response

> *"I wasn't expecting you to be interested out of the box. That's why we should meet so you can see what we do and look how we can implement our solutions to help your customers. Are you available on...?"*

### Why This Works

You **disarm the objection** by agreeing with them — of course they're not interested yet! They haven't seen what you offer. This takes the pressure off. You're not asking them to commit to anything except a conversation.

**Key phrase to remember:** "I wasn't expecting you to be interested out of the box"

---

## OBJECTION 4: "There has been a lot of changes in your company"

### Why They Say This

They've had dealings with MHCCA (or its predecessor Hitachi Capital) before and are wary because of organizational changes, mergers, or inconsistency.

### The Response

> *"This is true. With the huge expansion we had in the last 2 years, there has been a lot of changes in the company. **Now that everything is settled** it is a great time to meet and take a look at the great new programs that we have in place. Are you available on...?"*

### Why This Works

You **acknowledge the truth** (yes, there have been changes) but pivot to the positive — the changes are done, things are settled, and the result is better programs than before. Honesty builds trust.

**Key phrase to remember:** "Now that everything is settled"

---

## OBJECTION 5: "I had a bad experience with Hitachi Capital"

### Why They Say This

They've been burned before — slow service, lost paperwork, bad communication, or some other negative experience with the company under its old name.

### The Response

> *"I'm sorry to hear that. We've been through an important growth in the company for the last years and we work hard to fix it. I'd like to meet you and show you **how we improved our operations process.**"*

### Why This Works

You **apologize without being defensive.** You acknowledge the problem existed, show that the company has been actively fixing it, and offer to demonstrate the improvements in person. This is honest and respectful.

**Key phrase to remember:** "how we improved our operations process"

---

## OBJECTION 6: "I don't have time"

### Why They Say This

Could be genuine (they're busy) or a polite dismissal. Either way, they're telling you their time is precious.

### The Response

> *"Yes, I understand how valuable your time is. As I mentioned earlier, Hitachi Capital focuses on **tailor-made financing solutions customized for your customer's needs.** By investing a small amount of your time talking with me, I am confident you will be able to **save far more time** for your customers with the quality products we offer that I would like to tell you about."*

### Why This Works

You respect their time, then reframe the meeting as a **time-saving investment**, not a time-wasting activity. A small amount of time now = better products for their customers later = less hassle for them.

**Key phrase to remember:** "investing a small amount of your time... save far more time"

---

## OBJECTION 7: "I have other companies I finance with"

### Why They Say This

They already have financing partners and don't see why they need another one.

### The Response (Two Options)

**Option A:**
> *"That's a good thing! **Every company should have several financing options** that allows you to offer the most to your customers. If you give me a few minutes, I'd love to sit down together to explain how Hitachi Capital can provide your customers with added value to service all of their financing needs."*

**Option B:**
> *"Great! This will give you a good sense of how financing companies like Hitachi Capital operate. I would love to sit together and explain some of the ways **our products can be customized to service the areas that your current lenders can't!**"*

### Why This Works

You don't compete head-on with their existing partners. Instead, you position MHCCA as **complementary** — filling gaps that their current lenders don't cover. Having multiple financing options is presented as a smart business practice, not a sign of disloyalty.

**Key phrase to remember:** "service the areas that your current lenders can't"

---

## OBJECTION 8: "Our customers pay cash"

### Why They Say This

They believe their customers don't need financing because they pay in full upfront.

### The Response

> *"Yes, I understand some customers can pay cash. But there are also **lots of benefits of financing.** I would like to sit with you and discuss the benefits your customer can get from financing their purchase. Can we meet next Monday?"*

### Why This Works

You don't argue that cash is bad. You acknowledge it, then open the door to a conversation about why financing can be BETTER than cash even for companies that can afford to pay outright (preserving working capital, tax benefits, off-balance-sheet treatment, etc.).

**Key phrase to remember:** "lots of benefits of financing" (even for cash-rich customers)

---

## OBJECTION 9: "Please send me documents"

### Why They Say This

They want information but don't want to commit to a meeting. It's often a stalling tactic — they'll look at documents briefly (or not at all) and forget about you.

### The Response

> *"Yes, I will. But I want to **optimize your time** by going through the information together and have all your questions addressed immediately. Hitachi Capital can develop a very customized solution to help you grow your business. I will be in your area next Monday. Can we meet then?"*

### Why This Works

You agree to send documents (so they feel heard), but explain that meeting in person is more efficient because you can answer questions on the spot. You also add urgency by mentioning you'll be in their area.

**Key phrase to remember:** "optimize your time by going through the information together"

## PATTERNS TO NOTICE ACROSS ALL OBJECTIONS

### Pattern 1: Never Say "No" or Argue

In every single response, the first words are acknowledgment: "I understand," "That's true," "That's a good thing," "I'm sorry to hear that." You **never** push back or tell the prospect they're wrong.

### Pattern 2: Always End with a Meeting Request

Every response ends with a specific meeting time: "Are you available Monday afternoon or Wednesday morning?" Not "Can we meet sometime?" — **specific options.**

### Pattern 3: The "Alternative Close"

Notice the technique: "Monday afternoon **or** Wednesday morning?" This gives the prospect a choice between two "yes" options, not a yes/no choice. This is a classic sales technique called the **alternative close** — either answer moves the conversation forward.

### Pattern 4: Reframe, Don't Deflect

Every objection gets reframed into a reason TO meet, not a reason to back off:
- "No rate to give" → "That's why we should meet — we customize"
- "No immediate need" → "Perfect timing to get to know us"
- "Bad experience" → "We've improved — let me show you"
- "No time" → "Small time investment saves big time later"

## PRACTICE QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**Q1: A prospect asks "What's your rate?" How do you respond?**
> **A:** Don't give a flat rate. Say that we offer tailor-made financing solutions instead of flat rates, and that's exactly why we should meet — to understand their needs and customize a solution. Then ask for a specific meeting time.

**Q2: A prospect says "I already have financing partners." What's your approach?**
> **A:** Acknowledge it positively — "That's a good thing! Every company should have several financing options." Then position MHCCA as complementary, able to service areas that their current lenders can't. Ask for a meeting to explain the added value.

**Q3: A prospect says they had a bad experience with Hitachi Capital. How do you handle it?**
> **A:** Apologize sincerely, acknowledge that the company went through significant growth and changes, and explain that you've worked hard to fix operational issues. Offer to meet and show them how operations have improved.

**Q4: What is the "alternative close" technique used in these objection responses?**
> **A:** Instead of asking "Can we meet?" (which invites a yes/no answer), you offer two specific times: "Would you be available Monday afternoon or Wednesday morning?" This gives the prospect a choice between two positive options, making it harder to say no and easier to commit.

**Q5: A prospect says "Our customers pay cash." What's the key reframe?**
> **A:** Acknowledge that some customers can pay cash, but explain there are many benefits to financing even for cash-rich customers (preserving working capital, flexibility, tax benefits). Ask to meet to discuss these benefits.

**Q6: What pattern do all the objection responses follow?**
> **A:** Three steps: (1) Acknowledge/empathize with the objection, (2) Reframe the situation to show value or a reason to meet, (3) Close for a specific meeting time using the alternative close technique.

**Q7: Why shouldn't you give a flat rate when asked "What's your rate?"**
> **A:** Because MHCCA offers tailor-made financing solutions, not flat rates. Giving a rate commoditizes you — the prospect will just compare your number to other lenders. By meeting first, you can understand their needs and propose a customized solution that demonstrates value beyond price.
