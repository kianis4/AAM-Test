# STUDY GUIDE: Bill 96 — Quebec French Language Law

### *MHCCA Internal Training Guide*

---

## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

**Bill 96** is Quebec's law strengthening the French language charter (*Loi sur la langue officielle et commune du Québec, le français*). For MHCCA, it creates specific rules about how contracts must be handled when they are concluded in Quebec. In plain English: **if a deal touches Quebec in any way, you must follow French-first rules or risk invalidating the entire contract.**

Think of it this way: even if your client speaks fluent English and wants everything in English, the law says you must send the French version FIRST, get their explicit request for English, describe equipment in French on the contract, and file all PPSA registrations (called RDPRM in Quebec) entirely in French. Skip any of these steps and you could lose your security registration, face fines up to $30,000, or have the contract thrown out in court.

**Why should you care?** Quebec is a major market for MHCCA. A huge portion of deals touch Quebec in some way — the client is based there, the equipment operates there, or a guarantor lives there. Getting Bill 96 wrong doesn't just mean paperwork hassles — it means **unsecured positions on financed contracts.**

---

## SECTION 1: WHAT THE LAW SAYS

### Article 45 of the French Language Charter

Two critical rules:

| Rule | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **French contract first** | The FR contract must be sent **BEFORE** the EN one |
| **Explicit preference** | The client must then **explicitly state** their preference for the English version |

You cannot simply send both versions simultaneously. The French version goes first, and the client must actively request English.

### Articles 2984 and 3006 of the Civil Code of Quebec

| Rule | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Registration requisitions in French** | All RDPRM filings must be drafted **exclusively in French** |
| **Matching descriptions** | The description of goods in the RDPRM must **match** what's on the contract |
| **Certified translations** | Documents in a language other than French must be accompanied by a **certified translation** |

> **Why This Matters:** This is why equipment must be described in French on an English contract — because the RDPRM registration must be in French and must match the contract. If your contract says "excavator" but your RDPRM says "excavatrice," you have an inconsistency that could invalidate your security registration.

## SECTION 2: HOW TO DETERMINE IF BILL 96 APPLIES

**ANY ONE of the following factors** is sufficient to trigger Bill 96 requirements. You don't need all of them — just one.

| # | Factor | How to Check |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **The client (company) is based in Quebec** | Head office in Quebec / Corp Profile in Quebec (REQ) |
| 2 | **The signatory resides in Quebec** | Quebec ID / QC Address |
| 3 | **Equipment will be used and/or plated in Quebec** | This means there will be a publication in the RDPRM |
| 4 | **A personal guarantee with a Quebec guarantor** | The guarantor resides in Quebec |

### Special Rule for Personal Guarantees

If a personal guarantee is included and the **guarantor** resides in Quebec, the Bill 96 process must be followed for **both:**
- The contract itself
- The guarantee document (for the guarantor only)

This applies even if the client themselves is NOT in Quebec. The guarantor's Quebec residence is enough.

### The Exception

**Bill 96 does NOT apply** for a Quebec dealer signing a SSC (Supplier Support Certificate), a buyback agreement, etc. The law targets the client/lessee relationship, not dealer-side documents.

> **Why This Matters:** The most common mistake is thinking Bill 96 only applies when the client is in Quebec. It also applies if the signatory lives in Quebec, or the equipment is in Quebec, or even if just the guarantor is in Quebec. One factor = full compliance required.

## SECTION 3: THE 3 REQUIREMENTS FOR ENGLISH CONTRACTS IN QUEBEC

To accept a contract in English that is concluded in Quebec, you need ALL THREE of the following:

### Requirement 1: Proof of French-First Process

The client must have received the French version first and then requested English.

**Accepted proof:** An **email chain from the client** stating their preference for the English version over the French version, with the attached contract(s) that can be **clicked and opened** for verification.

Key details:
- The email must come **from the client** (not from MHCCA)
- The contract attachments must be **verifiable** (clickable and openable)
- A verbal request is NOT sufficient — it must be documented

### Requirement 2: Equipment Description in French on the Contract

Even on an English contract, the equipment description must include French.

| Situation | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Brand & Model names** | Can remain in their **original language/version** |
| **Proving brand/model** | Must be able to prove the word refers to a brand name and/or model (web search) |
| **Acronyms** | "VIN" → correct to "NIV" / "SUV" → correct to "VUS" — can be done manually without returning to client |
| **Clerical errors** | No return to client necessary for initials in cases of clerical errors — Clause 3 of MHCCA contracts allows these corrections |

> **Why This Matters:** You don't need to translate "Caterpillar 320" into French — it's a brand and model. But "hydraulic excavator" must appear as "excavatrice hydraulique" on the contract. And if someone wrote "VIN" instead of "NIV," you can fix it yourself based on Clause 3.

### Requirement 2.1: If the Contract Refers to an Invoice

When the contract references an invoice for the equipment description:
- **Option A:** Obtain a **French invoice**
- **Option B:** If unable to get a French invoice, the person issuing the contract must **manually prepare a regular Appendix A** with the description in French

### Requirement 3: Full RDPRM Drafting in French

Everything in the RDPRM (Quebec's PPSA) must be in French:

| Element | Rule |
|---|---|
| **MHCCA's name** | Use the **French version** |
| **Client's name** | If the client has a legal name in French, use that one (**not DBA/FASRS**) |
| **Equipment description** | In French |
| **General Collateral** | In French |

## SECTION 5: CONSEQUENCES OF NON-COMPLIANCE

This section is critical. There are **4 potential consequences**, and they escalate in severity:

| # | Consequence | Details |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **RDPRM registration refused** | Non-French words cause RDPRM to refuse your registration request. You're informed too late → **unsecured position** with a financed contract not registered within the **7-day deadline** |
| 2 | **Inconsistency between contract and registration** | French registration + English equipment description on contract = inconsistency → affects **validity of both** the registration and the contract |
| 3 | **Contract invalidation in court** | Client or third party could invalidate the contract if they find a loophole → could **create a legal precedent** |
| 4 | **OQLF fines** | The OQLF (Office québécois de la langue française) conducts increasing audits and investigations → authorized to impose fines up to **$30,000** |

**Important note:** There is **still NO case law** to guide how courts interpret this law. This means there's uncertainty — which actually makes compliance MORE important, not less, because you can't predict how a judge would rule.

> **Why This Matters:** The worst-case scenario is #1 — your RDPRM registration is refused, and by the time you find out, you've already funded the deal. You now have money out the door with NO security registration. The 7-day registration deadline makes this especially dangerous because there's no time to fix it retroactively.

## SECTION 6: KEY FRENCH TERMS TO KNOW

| French Term | English Equivalent | Context |
|---|---|---|
| **RDPRM** | Registre des droits personnels et réels mobiliers | Quebec's version of PPSA |
| **REQ** | Registraire des Entreprises du Québec | Quebec corporate registry |
| **OQLF** | Office québécois de la langue française | Government enforcement agency |
| **NIV** | Numéro d'identification du véhicule | French for VIN (Vehicle Identification Number) |
| **VUS** | Véhicule utilitaire sport | French for SUV |
| **FASRS / DBA** | Faisant affaires sous la raison sociale | "Doing Business As" name |
| **Loi 96** | Bill 96 | The law itself |
| **Charte de la langue française** | French Language Charter | The charter Bill 96 amends |

## SECTION 7: DECISION FLOWCHART

Use this flowchart to determine your obligations:

```
Does ANY of these apply?
├─ Client based in QC?
├─ Signatory resides in QC?
├─ Equipment used/plated in QC?
└─ Guarantor resides in QC?

  YES to ANY → Bill 96 APPLIES
  │
  ├─ Client wants English contract?
  │   ├─ Send French version FIRST
  │   ├─ Get written request for English (email chain)
  │   ├─ Describe equipment in French on the English contract
  │   └─ File RDPRM entirely in French
  │
  └─ Client wants French contract?
      ├─ Proceed normally
      └─ File RDPRM in French (same as always)

  NO to ALL → Bill 96 does NOT apply
  └─ Proceed with standard process
```

## PRACTICE QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

Test yourself. Cover the answers and try to answer from memory.

---

### Question 1
**What is Bill 96, and what is its core requirement for MHCCA?**

**Answer:** Bill 96 is Quebec's law strengthening the French language charter. Its core requirement for MHCCA is that contracts concluded in Quebec must follow a **French-first process**: the French contract must be sent before the English one, the client must explicitly request English, equipment must be described in French on the contract, and all RDPRM filings must be entirely in French.

---

### Question 2
**Name the 4 factors that determine whether Bill 96 applies to a contract. How many must be present?**

**Answer:** Only **ONE** of the following is sufficient:
1. The client (company) is based in Quebec
2. The signatory resides in Quebec
3. The equipment will be used and/or plated in Quebec
4. A personal guarantee is included and the guarantor resides in Quebec

---

### Question 3
**A client is based in Ontario, but the equipment will be used in Quebec. Does Bill 96 apply?**

**Answer:** **Yes.** Even though the client is in Ontario, the equipment being used in Quebec is sufficient to trigger Bill 96. There will be a publication in the RDPRM, which must comply with the law.

---

### Question 4
**What constitutes acceptable proof that the client received the French version first and requested English?**

**Answer:** An **email chain from the client** stating their preference for the English version over the French version, with the attached contract(s) that can be **clicked and opened** for verification. A verbal request is not sufficient.

---

### Question 5
**A contract in English describes equipment as a "hydraulic excavator" with a VIN number. What corrections are needed for Bill 96 compliance?**

**Answer:** Two corrections: (1) The equipment description must include the French equivalent — "excavatrice hydraulique." (2) "VIN" must be corrected to **"NIV"** (Numéro d'identification du véhicule). The "VIN" → "NIV" correction can be made manually without returning to the client. Brand/model names like "CAT 320" can stay in their original language.

---

### Question 6
**What must be in French in the RDPRM filing? List all 4 elements.**

**Answer:**
1. **MHCCA's name** in its French version
2. **Client's legal name** in French (if they have one — not the DBA)
3. **Equipment description** in French
4. **General Collateral** in French

---

### Question 7
**What is the maximum fine the OQLF can impose for non-compliance with Bill 96?**

**Answer:** Up to **$30,000**.

---

### Question 8
**A contract concluded in Quebec references an invoice for the equipment description, but the invoice is only available in English. What are your options?**

**Answer:** Two options:
- **Option A:** Obtain a French invoice from the vendor
- **Option B:** If unable to get a French invoice, the person issuing the contract must **manually prepare a regular Appendix A** with the equipment description in French

---

### Question 9
**True or False: Bill 96 applies when a Quebec dealer signs a Supplier Support Certificate (SSC).**

**Answer:** **False.** The law does NOT apply for a Quebec dealer signing a SSC, a buyback agreement, etc. It applies to the client/lessee relationship.

---

### Question 10
**What is the most dangerous consequence of non-compliance with Bill 96's RDPRM requirements?**

**Answer:** The RDPRM could **refuse the registration request**, and MHCCA would be informed too late — leaving an **unsecured position** with a financed contract not registered within the **7-day deadline**. The money is already out the door with no security registration protecting MHCCA.

---

### Question 11
**A contract is for a client in Alberta. The client has no Quebec connections, but the personal guarantor lives in Montreal. Does Bill 96 apply?**

**Answer:** **Yes.** Even though the client is in Alberta, the fact that the guarantor resides in Quebec triggers Bill 96. The process must be followed for **both** the contract and the guarantee document (for the guarantor only — not applicable to the Alberta client).

### Question 13
**You notice that "SUV" appears on a Quebec contract instead of the French equivalent. Can you correct this yourself?**

**Answer:** **Yes.** You can manually correct "SUV" to **"VUS"** (Véhicule utilitaire sport) without returning the contract to the client. You must also ensure the correction is reflected in the PPSA/RDPRM (in French). This is permitted based on **Clause 3** of MHCCA contracts for clerical corrections.
