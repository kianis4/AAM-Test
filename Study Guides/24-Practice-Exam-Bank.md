**C1.** In the RAC, what is the auto-decision limit for Agriculture deals? What's the exception for tractors? [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Auto-decision for Agriculture is **$0 — all deals are manual.** The exception: tractors used in agriculture follow **Construction** segment rules because the equipment overlaps. So a farmer buying a John Deere tractor gets Construction auto-decision limits ($375K for CR1/CR2, $200K for CR3).
</details>

---

**C2.** Healthcare has different auto-decision limits than most segments. What are they? [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- CR1/CR2: **$350,000**
- CR3: **$150,000**
- CR4: **$0**

These are different from the standard $375K/$200K in Construction and Transport 1-7. Healthcare also has a max amort of **66 months** (not 96). Exception authority requires **Senior Manager or above** (not just analysts for CR1/CR2).
</details>

---

**C3.** What is the max amortization for Agriculture Tier 1 self-propelled equipment, and how does it compare to Construction? [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Agriculture Tier 1 self-propelled (SP) new equipment: **180 months** max for CR1-CR3. This is by far the longest in the RAC — it's 15 years. Construction Tier 1/2 new is only 96 months (8 years). The reason: agricultural equipment like large tractors and combines holds value much longer and has a longer useful life than construction equipment.
</details>

---

**C4.** MUSH stands for what? What is the auto-decision limit and max amort? [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

MUSH = **Municipalities, Universities, Schools, and Hospitals** (government/public sector). Auto-decision: **$0 — all deals are manual.** Max amort for new T1/T2 equipment: **96 months.** Max term: **72 months.** Analyst exception authority exists for CR1/CR2.
</details>

**C5.** SDG-Energy has a key rule about analyst exceptions. What is it? [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

SDG-Energy (Sustainable Development Goals — solar, ZEVs, clean energy) has **ZERO analyst exception authority** — same as Forestry, Transport Class 8, and Hospitality. All exceptions require Next Level Approval Authority. Max amort: **84 months.** Auto-decision: **$0.**
</details>

**C6.** The Hospitality segment is one of the most restricted. What 3 things make it unique? [Guide 14]

3. **ZERO analyst exception authority** — same as Forestry, Transport Class 8, and SDG-Energy

Hospitality covers restaurants, hotels, entertainment venues — industries with high failure rates, which is why it's so restricted.

**C10.** What is the difference between "analyst exception authority" segments and "zero exception" segments? Name all 4 zero-exception segments. [Guide 14]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

In segments with **analyst exception authority** (Construction, Transport 1-7, Industrial, MUSH, Agriculture, Manufacturing), analysts can approve certain exceptions for CR1/CR2 clients within defined parameters.

In **zero exception** segments, analysts have **no authority to approve ANY exceptions** — all exceptions require Next Level Approval Authority. The 4 zero-exception segments are:
1. **Forestry**
2. **Transport Class 8 OTR**
3. **SDG-Energy**
4. **Hospitality**
</details>

**C11.** Explain the difference between Equity and Tangible Net Worth (TNW). Why do credit analysts prefer TNW? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Equity** = Total Assets − Total Liabilities (includes intangible assets like goodwill, patents, and investments in related companies).

**TNW** = Equity − Intangible Assets.

Credit analysts prefer TNW because intangible assets **cannot be easily liquidated** to cover debts. Goodwill from an acquisition has no resale value if the company defaults. TNW represents the **hard, realizable value** of the company. The benchmark for D/TNW is below 3.5x.
</details>

---

**C12.** What is WIP (Work in Progress), why does it matter for construction spreads, and what 4 questions should you ask about it? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**WIP** = estimated value of work performed but **not yet invoiced.** It's recorded as revenue on the income statement and as a current asset on the balance sheet even though no cash has been received.

**Why it matters:** WIP inflates revenue and the current ratio. A company showing $10M revenue with $3M WIP has only generated $7M in actual cash revenue.

**4 questions to ask:**
1. Which contract is the WIP tied to?
2. Which customer? Are they reputable?
3. What is the risk of non-payment?
4. What percentage of total revenue is WIP?
</details>

**C14.** A construction company's LOC was 80% drawn last year and is now only 40% drawn. Is this a positive or negative signal? Why? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Positive signal.** The company repaid its revolving facility and improved liquidity — it now has 60% of the LOC available vs. only 20% before. This shows improved cash management and a stronger liquidity buffer. Always track LOC utilization **YoY** — the trend tells you more than the snapshot.
</details>

**C15.** What is the SFD/EBITDA ratio, what's the benchmark, and when is a higher number acceptable? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**SFD/EBITDA = Senior Funded Debt ÷ EBITDA.** It measures how many years of earnings it would take to pay off all debt.

Benchmark: **Below 3.5x.**

A higher number is acceptable for **startups or young companies** that are still scaling — they've taken on debt to fund growth but haven't yet generated the full EBITDA to support the ratio. In that case, present the trajectory: "SFD/EBITDA is currently 4.5x, but revenue has grown 40% YoY and EBITDA margins are expanding, indicating the company will grow into its debt load."
</details>

---

**C16.** When spreading a forestry company, what 5 industry-specific questions should you ask that go beyond the financials? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. Who are the key customers — which **sawmills**? Short wood or long wood? Private or public land?
2. How many **cubic meters per year**, and what is the average revenue and cost **per cubic meter**?
3. Is the company **fully integrated** (harvesting, transportation, shipping)?
4. Do they need **interest-only payments** during off-season (spring breakup)?
5. Where are the machines **physically located**? Have you **visited the premises**?
</details>

**C17.** A client's financials are NTR and show a very healthy FCCR of 2.8x. Should you be concerned? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Yes — be suspicious.** NTR financials often **don't report CPLTD** (Current Portion of Long-Term Debt). If CPLTD is missing from the FCCR denominator, the ratio is **artificially inflated**. The real FCCR could be significantly lower — possibly below 1.0x. Always note: "FCCR may be inflated as NTR financials do not appear to report CPLTD." Request bank statements and tax returns to substantiate.
</details>

**C18.** In the 4Bloc format, which quadrant needs to be the strongest and why? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The **top left quadrant — Company & Situation (KYC).** It needs to be strong because it's where credit analysts form their first impression. It includes: business description, management experience, location, fleet size, related parties, and the deal request. A weak top-left undermines everything else — even strong financials won't save a deal if the analyst doesn't understand who the company is.
</details>

**C19.** What is a "surety company" and why would you ask about one in a construction spread? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

A surety company provides **performance bonds** for construction contracts. When a construction company wins a public or large commercial project, the client often requires a surety bond guaranteeing the work will be completed. Asking about the surety company tells you: (1) the company qualifies for bonding (a positive credit signal), (2) they do larger/institutional work, and (3) another financial institution has underwritten their ability to perform.
</details>

---

**C20.** What is the "Cash Conversion Cycle" (CCC) in the spreads template, and why does it matter? [Guide 21]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**CCC = AR Turnover (days) + Inventory Turnover (days) − AP Turnover (days).** It measures how many days it takes the company to turn its investments in inventory and receivables back into cash. A shorter CCC = healthier cash flow. A long CCC means the company is waiting a long time to get paid while also paying its own bills — this strains liquidity. In construction, CCC can be very long due to WIP and retainage.
</details>

**C21.** In the Bigdan Vachon example from the Taxation guide, a $300,000 excavator is financed three ways. What are the 3 options and which gives the highest tax deduction in Year 1? [Guide 11]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The 3 options:
1. **Lease Acquisition (Capital Lease / $1 buyout)** — client claims CCA depreciation on the full asset value
2. **Operating Lease (FMV)** — full payment is an operating expense (100% deductible)
3. **Installment Sale (Loan)** — only interest is deductible

**Highest Year 1 tax deduction: Capital Lease.** The client can claim CCA (Capital Cost Allowance) accelerated depreciation on the full $300,000 in Year 1 (under the Accelerated Investment Incentive Program, potentially 100% deduction). The operating lease deduction depends on the monthly payment amount, and the loan only deducts interest.
</details>

**C22.** What is the difference between IFRS and ASPE, and why does it matter for operating leases? [Guide 06]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**IFRS (International Financial Reporting Standards)** — used by publicly traded companies. Under IFRS 16, almost all leases go ON the balance sheet as "right-of-use assets." This means the off-balance sheet advantage of operating leases is **largely eliminated** for public companies.

**ASPE (Accounting Standards for Private Enterprises)** — used by private companies. Under ASPE, operating leases can still qualify for **off-balance sheet treatment** if the 4 criteria are met (75% useful life, 90% PV, no bargain purchase, no auto-transfer).

**Why it matters:** The off-balance sheet selling point for FMV leases mainly applies to **private companies using ASPE.** If a client is publicly traded (IFRS), off-balance sheet is not the right pitch.
</details>

---

**C23.** What is a "programmatic" vs. "one-off" operating lease approach? [Guide 06]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Programmatic:** A structured, ongoing program with an OEM or dealer — e.g., the ZAXIS program with Hitachi/Wajax, or the Linde forklift program. MHCCA sets standard terms, residual values, and rates for a defined equipment line. Deals flow in volume with consistent structures. Higher efficiency, lower per-deal cost.

**One-off:** A single deal negotiated individually — custom structure, custom residual, custom pricing. Higher margin per deal but more work and less predictable volume.

**Strategic importance:** Siraaj emphasized that MHCCA wants to grow **programmatic** FMV business because it scales — one dealer agreement can generate dozens of deals versus negotiating each one individually.
</details>

**C24.** Why does MHCCA NOT target Transportation for operating leases (FMV)? [Guide 06]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Operating leases require the **lessor to take residual risk** on the equipment. Transportation assets (especially Class 8 trucks) **depreciate fast and unpredictably** — tied to economic cycles, mileage, and maintenance. The residual value is hard to predict and often lower than expected. MHCCA's target industries for FMV leases are **Manufacturing, Material Handling, Construction, and Technology** — where equipment has more predictable residual values and longer useful life.
</details>

**C25.** What is the EBR (Equipment Backed Revolver) and how does it differ from ABL? [Guide 13]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**EBR** = Equipment Backed Revolver — a revolving credit facility secured **only by equipment and machinery.** Range: $500K-$10M. Advance rate: up to 100% on Forced Liquidation Value (FLV).

**ABL** = Asset-Based Lending — a revolving credit facility secured by **A/R, inventory, AND equipment.** Range: $1M-$10M. Advance rates: up to 90% A/R, 80% NOLV inventory, 90% FLV equipment.

Key difference: EBR uses only equipment as collateral, ABL uses the full asset base. EBR is simpler but has a lower borrowing base. ABL is more complex but gives the client more borrowing capacity.
</details>

---

**C26.** What is Cash Flow Lending (from Private Credit) and how is it different from ABL? [Guide 13]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Cash Flow Lending** = term loans based on the company's **EBITDA and earnings power**, not secured by specific assets. Range: $500K-$10M. The lending decision is based on **cash flow generation and debt service coverage**, not asset values.

**ABL** is asset-secured (A/R, inventory, equipment). **Cash Flow Lending** is cash-flow-secured (EBITDA multiples). Cash Flow Lending is riskier for the lender but provides financing to companies whose assets may not be sufficient collateral but whose cash flow is strong. It's used for acquisitions, management buyouts, and growth capital.
</details>

**C27.** What is the IPA (Installment Payment Agreement) and when would you use it? [Guide 03]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**IPA** = Installment Payment Agreement — designed specifically for **software purchases.** Title transfers from vendor to customer at point of sale. Taxes can be capitalized or paid upfront. Used when a customer wants to finance a multi-year software license.

Example: A company wants a 3-year cybersecurity XDR license costing $150K. They don't want to pay it all upfront. An IPA lets them spread the cost over 36 months. The software belongs to them from day one.
</details>

**C28.** What is DORF and why is it strategically important for MHCCA? [Guide 03]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**DORF** = Dealer Owned Rental Fleet financing. It finances new equipment acquisitions specifically for dealers who operate rental fleets. The dealer buys the equipment, puts it on rent, and the rental income covers the financing payments.

**Strategic importance:** It deepens MHCCA's dealer relationships beyond just retail finance. A dealer using DORF is financing their rental fleet through MHCCA — this is a larger, ongoing relationship that generates consistent volume. It's also a cross-sell opportunity from existing Floorplan or Equipment Finance relationships.
</details>

**C29.** For AITC (Atlantic Investment Tax Credit), what are the 7 qualifying industries and what provinces are eligible? [Guide 07]

**C30.** What is "residual risk sharing" in the context of operating leases, and what are the mechanisms for it? [Guide 06]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

When MHCCA offers an FMV lease, it assumes **residual risk** — the risk that the equipment will be worth less than the residual value set at lease inception. If the client returns the equipment and it's worth less than expected, MHCCA takes the loss.

**Risk sharing mechanisms:**
1. **Dealer/OEM buyback agreements** — the dealer agrees to repurchase the equipment at a guaranteed minimum price
2. **UNL (Unlimited Guarantee)** — the dealer absorbs any loss on the residual
3. **Remarketing agreements** — MHCCA and the dealer share the upside/downside of resale
4. **Conservative residual setting** — MHCCA's 20% internal policy ensures a buffer

This is why programmatic deals with OEMs are preferred — the OEM shares the residual risk.
</details>

## SALES EXAM — SECTION A: LEAD Deep Dive (15 Questions)

---

**S1.** What is the BRAIN questioning framework? Give an example question for each letter. [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**BRAIN** is used before the meeting to prepare questions covering all important themes:

- **B — Budget/Business:** "What is your annual equipment spend?"
- **R — Relationships/Resources:** "Who else is involved in financing decisions?"
- **A — Authority/Ability:** "Who makes the final call on equipment financing?"
- **I — Issues/Interests:** "What challenges are you facing with your current financing?"
- **N — Needs/Next steps:** "What are your equipment needs for the next 12 months?"
</details>

---

**S2.** What is ISAQ and when do you use it? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**ISAQ** = the 4 ingredients of a meeting introduction, used in Step 1 (Legitimize):

- **I — Initiate dialogue:** Break the ice with small talk ("I noticed the new warehouse expansion")
- **S — Synchronize:** Find common ground, build rapport ("I saw on LinkedIn we went to the same university")
- **A — Assure about the casting:** Explain who you are and why you're credible ("I've been working with companies in your sector for 5 years")
- **Q — Quickly differentiate:** Brief hook about what makes MHCCA unique ("We understand the equipment itself, not just the numbers")
</details>

---

**S3.** What is OPA and what is the key rule about the "O"? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**OPA** structures the formal meeting opening after ISAQ:

- **O — Objective:** State why you're here — **framed in terms of CLIENT BENEFIT**, not yours. Say "I want to help you find ways to reduce fleet costs" NOT "I want to tell you about our leasing products"
- **P — Presentation:** Give a mini agenda ("First I'll learn about your operations, then share ideas, then we'll see if there's a fit")
- **A — Agreement:** Get agreement on agenda and duration ("We'll need about 40 minutes. Does that work? What do YOU expect from this meeting?")
</details>

---

**S4.** What is the "NO on the table" concept? Why does it paradoxically increase your chances of getting a yes? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

You tell the prospect early that it's okay to say no. Example: "I'm not here to sell you anything today. If this isn't a fit, that's completely fine."

**Why it works:** Reducing pressure makes people more open to saying yes. When clients feel trapped, they put up walls. When they feel free to walk away, they relax and actually listen. The materials say: "Always show that you want it but don't need it." Paradoxically, removing the threat of commitment makes commitment more likely.
</details>

---

**S5.** What is PIVAS? Why does the "S" matter even on phone calls? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**PIVAS** = voice delivery technique:
- **P — Pace:** Don't rush — slow down, it projects confidence
- **I — Intonation:** Vary your tone, don't be monotone
- **V — Volume:** Project confidence, match the room
- **A — Articulation:** Enunciate every word clearly
- **S — Smile:** People can hear a smile

The "S" matters on phone calls because smiling physically changes the muscles around your mouth, which changes how your voice sounds — it comes across as warmer and more friendly, even though the listener can't see you.
</details>

---

**S6.** Name the 6 client motivations. A client keeps asking about guarantees, what-if scenarios, and references. Which motivation drives them, and how should you respond? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The 6 motivations: **Security, Pride, Novelty, Comfort, Money, Likeability.**

This client is driven by **Security** — they want to feel safe and fear risk. They ask about guarantees and references because they need reassurance.

**How to respond:** Emphasize MHCCA's stability (backed by the global Mitsubishi group), the company's long track record, risk management expertise, and reference stories from similar clients. Do NOT pitch novelty or innovation — that would make a security-motivated client MORE nervous, not less.
</details>

---

**S7.** What are the three types of questions in the "traffic light" system? Which one should you AVOID and why? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **Pink (Assumption):** You impose your point of view and assume the answer — "So you must be unhappy with your bank, right?" **AVOID** — makes clients defensive
- **Blue (Closed):** Yes/No answers — "Do you currently finance through a bank?" Use to **confirm** facts
- **Green (Open):** Who/What/Where/When/How/Why — "What challenges do you face with your financing?" Use to **discover** needs

**Golden rule:** Start with Green (get them talking), then use Blue to confirm and narrow down. Never use Pink.
</details>

---

**S8.** What is the difference between a "question of FACT" and a "question of OPINION"? Why do you need both? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **Fact questions** seek objective information: "What type of equipment do you finance?" / "How many units in your fleet?"
- **Opinion questions** seek feelings or judgment: "How satisfied are you with the turnaround time?" / "What do you wish was different?"

**Why you need both:** Facts give you the DATA. Opinions give you the EMOTION. People buy based on emotion and justify with data. If you only ask factual questions, you'll know fleet size but not how they FEEL about their financing. If you only ask opinions, you'll know they're frustrated but not why.
</details>

---

**S9.** What are GEMS? Give a specific example you could share with a construction client. [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**GEMS** = valuable nuggets of information or insight shared with the client to build trust and demonstrate expertise. They are NOT sales pitches — they are genuinely useful information.

**Example for construction:** "One trend I've been seeing across the construction sector — companies that switched from ownership to leasing on excavators are reporting 15-20% lower total cost of ownership because they avoid the maintenance cliff that hits around year 4. Given the age of your fleet, that might be worth considering."

GEMS position you as a **trusted advisor** rather than a salesperson.
</details>

---

**S10.** According to LEAD, what is the formal definition of a "need"? What 3 elements must be present? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

> "A need is a GAP between what we have today and what we would like to have... or a gap between what we have today and what we will need to have in the future... WITH A REAL INTENTION TO CHANGE."

The 3 elements:
1. **Current state** — where they are now
2. **Desired/required future state** — where they want or need to be
3. **Real intention to change** — they're not just daydreaming

If there's no gap = no need = no sale. If there's a gap but no intention = no sale. Your job is to uncover the gap AND build the intention.
</details>

**S11.** What are the 7 "efforts" a client must make when buying? Why is this list important? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

When you ask someone to buy, you're asking them to:
1. **Make a decision** (stressful)
2. **Change supplier** (switching costs)
3. **Convince someone else** internally
4. **Change habits**
5. **Say no to someone** (current provider)
6. **Take time to analyze**
7. **Take a risk**
8. **Invest money**

(The materials list 8 efforts.)

**Why it matters:** Client resistance is usually about the EFFORT, not about you or your product. If a client seems resistant, identify which effort they're struggling with and address it specifically. Example: If they're nervous about convincing their board, offer to present to the board yourself.
</details>

**S12.** During Step 3 (Accentuate), the training says to "Dig then Neutralize" before arguing against an objection. What does this mean? How is it different from the 3-step objection pattern? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Dig then Neutralize** is a more nuanced approach used during the MEETING (Step 3 of LEAD):
1. **Dig:** Ask a clarifying question — "Can you tell me more about that concern?" — to understand the REAL objection
2. **Neutralize:** Softly reframe — "I understand that the cost seems higher at first glance..."
3. **Then argue:** Present your counter-point based on the real concern

**How it differs:** The 3-step pattern (Acknowledge → Reframe → Close for meeting) is used on **cold calls and initial objections** when you're trying to GET a meeting. Dig-then-Neutralize is used **during the meeting itself** when you're handling deeper objections in a consultative conversation.
</details>

**S13.** What are the 4 types of intelligence from Sales EQ? Which one is most important for sales? [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**S14.** What are the 4 pillars of Sales EQ? [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Empathy** — the ability to understand and share the feelings of the client
2. **Self-Control** — managing your own emotions (not reacting to rejection or pressure)
3. **Self-Awareness** — knowing your own tendencies, strengths, and blind spots
4. **Sales Drive** — the internal motivation to achieve, persist, and close
</details>

**S15.** What are the 5 stakeholder types in the BASIC model from Sales EQ? [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**B-A-S-I-C:**
1. **Buyers** — the decision-maker who signs the deal
2. **Amplifiers** — people who can amplify your message inside the organization (champions)
3. **Seekers** — people actively looking for a solution (they come to you)
4. **Influencers** — people who influence the decision but don't make it
5. **Coaches** — internal allies who guide you through the organization's politics and process

**Why it matters:** Selling only to the Buyer is a mistake. You need Coaches to navigate politics, Amplifiers to champion you, and you need to neutralize negative Influencers.
</details>

---

**S16.** What is the "Emotional Wall" concept from Sales EQ? [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The Emotional Wall is the psychological barrier a buyer puts up when they feel **pressured or manipulated.** It's an instinctive defense mechanism — when a client senses a "sales technique" being used on them, they shut down emotionally, stop trusting you, and become resistant to everything you say.

**How to avoid it:** Be authentic. Don't use canned scripts robotically. Put "NO on the table." Show genuine interest in their business. The moment a client feels you're running a technique on them, the wall goes up and the deal dies.
</details>

---

**S17.** What is "Buyer's Script Flipping" from Sales EQ? [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Buyer's Script Flipping is when a buyer takes control of the conversation using a pre-prepared script — things like "What's your rate?" or "Just send me an email" or "We're not interested." These are **reflexive responses** designed to get salespeople off the phone.

**How to handle it:** Recognize that these aren't real objections — they're **scripts.** The buyer hasn't actually evaluated your offer. Use the objection handling pattern (Acknowledge → Reframe → Close) to break through the script and start a real conversation.
</details>

**S18.** What is the "micro-commitments" concept from Sales EQ? Give an example. [Guide 09]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Micro-commitments are small, incremental "yeses" you get from a prospect throughout the sales process — each one moves them closer to the final decision without triggering the Emotional Wall.

**Examples:**
- "Can I send you a one-page overview?" (small ask)
- "Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week?" (slightly bigger)
- "Can you send me the details on your next equipment need so I can structure something?" (bigger — they're investing effort)
- "Would you like me to put together a formal proposal?" (commitment to evaluate)

Each micro-commitment builds investment in the relationship. By the time you ask for the deal, they've already said yes 4-5 times.
</details>

---

**S19.** What is the TAM program's pricing model? Are rates negotiable? [Guide 12]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

TAM pricing is **non-negotiable.** There is a fixed rate grid based on the client's credit rating — the rates are set by the third-party funder, not by MHCCA. You cannot discount or adjust TAM rates to win a deal.

**Why:** TAM is an originate-to-sell program — MHCCA originates the deal but sells it to a third-party funder. The funder sets the pricing. MHCCA earns a placement fee, not interest income. If the client doesn't like the rate, the deal doesn't happen through TAM.
</details>

---

**S20.** What is the 8-step TAM sales process? [Guide 12]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Identify** the opportunity (deal MHCCA declines or can't do)
2. **Qualify** — does it fit TAM criteria? Check restricted/blocked lists
3. **Price** — use the non-negotiable rate grid
4. **Present** to the dealer/client
5. **Submit** the application with required documentation
6. **Underwriting** by the third-party funder
7. **Document** — prepare closing docs
8. **Fund** — deal closes, MHCCA earns placement fee

Key rule: Always check the **restricted and blocked vendor/broker lists** before submitting. If the vendor or broker is blocked, the deal cannot proceed through TAM.
</details>

---

**S21.** From "Never Eat Alone" — what is the concept of "super-connectors" and why does it matter for MHCCA sales? [Guide 10]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Super-connectors** are people who have unusually large, diverse networks — they know people across many industries and can make introductions. In the context of "six degrees of separation," super-connectors reduce the degrees to 1-2.

**Why it matters for MHCCA:** Identifying and building relationships with super-connectors in your territory — equipment dealers, industry association leaders, accountants who serve multiple business owners, bankers — gives you access to exponentially more prospects. One strong relationship with a super-connector can generate more leads than 100 cold calls.
</details>

---

**S22.** What is the "follow-up within 12-24 hours" rule from Never Eat Alone? [Guide 10]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

After meeting someone — at a conference, a networking event, or even a cold meeting — you must follow up within **12-24 hours** while the connection is still fresh. The follow-up should reference something specific from your conversation to show you were paying attention. The longer you wait, the colder the connection becomes. Most people never follow up, so doing so immediately sets you apart.
</details>

---

**S23.** What is the MHCCA definition of "selling"? Why is it a two-part definition? [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

> **"Selling = Convincing someone of the advantage of our product, service, or idea AND obtaining an effort from them in return."**

It's two parts because:
1. You must convince them of an **advantage** (not just a feature — an advantage that matters to THEM)
2. They must make an **effort** in return (signing a deal, changing suppliers, investing money, convincing their board)

A presentation without commitment isn't selling — it's presenting. And asking for commitment without demonstrating advantage isn't selling — it's pushing.
</details>

## OPS EXAM — SECTION A: Deep Policy & Process (15 Questions)

---

**O1.** In a lease restructure (OP lease → CAP lease), what form do you use for an operating lease vs. a capital lease? What's the critical detail about PGs? [Guide 19]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **CON15.7** = Operating lease restructure
- **CON15.71** = Capital lease restructure

**Critical PG detail:** If the original deal had a Personal Guarantee, the PG signature **must appear on the restructure form.** If you forget this, the restructured deal lacks the PG protection that credit originally required — MHCCA loses that security.
</details>

---

**O2.** Why does MHCCA use a "restructure" instead of creating a brand-new deal when a client wants to finance the residual value? [Guide 19]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

To **maintain the original PPSA security registration.** Creating a new deal from scratch would require cancelling the old PPSA and registering a new one — this creates a **gap in security coverage** where MHCCA's lien is temporarily unprotected. The restructure keeps the original PPSA alive, amends the reference number to the new contract, and renews the term. No gap, no risk.
</details>

---

**O3.** In a restructure, what must be done to the PPSA? List the 3 specific actions. [Guide 19]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Keep** the original PPSA (do NOT cancel it)
2. **Amend** — change the reference number to the new contract number
3. **Renew** — extend the PPSA term to match the new restructure term

Additionally: take a **screenshot** of the renewal BEFORE saving the draft — this goes in the package so Validation can verify the term matches.
</details>

---

**O4.** In a lease restructure, what 3 rules apply to the buyout? [Guide 19]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. Must use the **full buyout amount** (no partial)
2. **EOT fees must be removed** — they may be included by default in the system but must be manually taken out
3. Must remain within the **same MHCCA entity** — buyout from MHCCL → new contract within MHCCL. Cannot restructure across entities.
</details>

---

**O5.** How does the Ontario driver's license encode the holder's information? What can you cross-verify? [Guide 20]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Ontario license number format: First character = **first letter of last name.** Last 6 digits = **birthdate in YYMMDD** format. For females, **50 is added** to the month (MM).

**Cross-verify:** Check that the first letter matches the stated last name, and the last 6 digits match the stated date of birth. If the license says "D6101-40706-60905" but the person says their last name starts with "M" — red flag.
</details>

---

**O6.** How does the Quebec driver's license encode information? What's the unique rule about expiry dates? [Guide 20]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Quebec format: **A####DDMMYY##** — First letter = first letter of last name, #### = four digits encoding last name, DDMMYY = full date of birth, ## = check digits.

**Unique Quebec rule:** The expiration date always falls on the holder's **birthday.** This gives you another cross-verification point: if the birthday on the license doesn't match the expiry date's day/month, something is wrong.

**Also:** All labels are in French ("Permis de conduire"), and the optically variable ink shifts from **gold to green** — check for this security feature.
</details>

---

**O7.** What are the 7 universal validation checks you should perform on EVERY driver's license, regardless of province? [Guide 20]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Raised lettering** — text should be tactile and slightly raised (tamper resistance)
2. **Holographic or optically variable elements** — holograms, color-shifting ink
3. **Ghost or secondary photo** — most cards have a secondary smaller photo
4. **Tactile surface checks** — feel for engraving, texture, embossing
5. **Visual inspection** — check for irregular fonts, color inconsistencies, tampering
6. **Card quality** — proper polycarbonate material, correct thickness
7. **Barcode & data alignment** — barcodes match printed data
</details>

---

**O8.** In the transfer process, what is the difference between pre-funding and post-funding handling of a COI on a GAI-covered contract? [Guide 18]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Pre-funding:** Send COI to GAI at 5860.VerifyInsurance@gaig.com. Include contract number. Add a note in the Audit request template. Do NOT validate the COI yourself. Do NOT integrate in Vision.

**Post-funding:** Send COI to GAI at the same email. **CC insuranceca@mhccna.com** (the MHCCA insurance team). Include contract number. Do NOT validate yourself. Do NOT integrate in Vision.

**Key difference:** Post-funding, you must CC the MHCCA insurance team. Pre-funding, you don't. In BOTH cases, you NEVER validate the COI content yourself — GAI handles it.
</details>

---

**O9.** The GAI program has a "false warning" issue with excavators. Explain the problem and the fix for Quebec vs. other provinces. [Guide 18]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Problem:** Excavators ARE covered by GAI, but Vision shows a **false warning** saying they're not. This happens because excavators are plated in Quebec (need plates to operate on roads), and the system can't differentiate by province.

**Fix — varies by province:**
- **Quebec:** Go to the **Insurance tab** → check the **automated insurance program box**
- **All other provinces:** Go to the **Equipment tab** → uncheck the **plated box**

This is a classic "exception to the exception" that's highly testable.
</details>

---

**O10.** In a transfer, when does the insurance team NOT need to be looped in? [Guide 15, 18]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

When the contract is covered by **GAI (Great American Insurance).** If GAI covers the contract:
1. Insurance documents are **not required** in the transfer package
2. The insurance team (assurance@mhccna.com) does **not need to be CC'd** on the Welcome Package email

If the contract is NOT GAI-covered, standard insurance documents are required AND assurance@mhccna.com must be CC'd.
</details>

---

**O11.** What is the "same entity" rule for lease restructures? Give an example of a violation. [Guide 19]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

A restructured deal must stay within the **same MHCCA legal entity** as the original:
- Buyout from **MHCCL** → new contract within **MHCCL**
- Buyout from **MHCCA** → new contract within **MHCCA**

**Violation example:** Original lease is under MHCCL (Leasing, Inc). The restructure team accidentally creates the new contract under MHCCA (Canada, Inc). This is invalid — the entity must match.
</details>

---

**O12.** A cooperative wants to sign a lease. How do you determine who has signing authority? What 3 documents do you need? [Guide 16]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Cooperatives are the **most complex** for signing authority because there's no universal rule. You must request:
1. **Extract from a resolution** naming the authorized signatories
2. **The Cooperative's Certificate of Conformity**
3. **The internal management regulations**

Then have the **Legal team validate** who has signing authority. NEVER assume who can sign for a cooperative — always get the documents and Legal review.
</details>

---

**O13.** What is a "Unanimous Shareholder Agreement" (USA) and why is it dangerous to miss? How do you check for one in Quebec vs. ROC? [Guide 16]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

A USA can **withdraw signing authority from directors** and assign it to shareholders instead. If a USA exists and you let a director sign, that signature may be **invalid** — the contract could be unenforceable.

**How to check:**
- **Quebec (REQ):** The REQ **indicates** whether a USA exists — look for "Il existe une convention unanime des actionnaires"
- **Rest of Canada (Securfact):** Securfact does **NOT indicate** this. The customer must notify you — which is riskier because they might not volunteer the information.

This is why you should ALWAYS check the REQ for Quebec companies and specifically ask ROC companies about shareholder agreements.
</details>

---

**O14.** In Bill 96, what happens if your RDPRM registration is refused because of non-French words? What's the worst-case scenario? [Guide 17]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

If RDPRM refuses your registration due to non-French content, you're informed **too late** — the deal may already be funded. This means MHCCA has money out the door with **no security registration.** Combined with the **7-day registration deadline**, there may not be time to fix it. You now have a financed contract with an **unsecured position** — MHCCA has no legal claim on the equipment if the client defaults.

This is the worst-case consequence of Bill 96 non-compliance — worse than fines, because it creates actual financial exposure.
</details>

---

**O15.** What's the difference between "VIN" and "NIV"? Between "SUV" and "VUS"? Can you fix these yourself? [Guide 17]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **VIN** (Vehicle Identification Number) → must be corrected to **NIV** (Numéro d'identification du véhicule)
- **SUV** (Sport Utility Vehicle) → must be corrected to **VUS** (Véhicule utilitaire sport)

**Yes, you can fix these yourself** without returning to the client. Clause 3 of MHCCA contracts allows corrections for clerical errors — these acronym corrections fall under that provision.
</details>

## MIXED EXAM — SECTION: Scenario-Based Curveballs (10 Questions)

These are the questions designed to trip you up by combining concepts across exams.

---

**X1.** A CR2 client in Ontario wants to finance a new Volvo FH16 (Class 8 truck). You check the equipment tiers and see Volvo listed as Tier 1 in Construction. Can you apply Construction Tier 1 terms?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Absolutely not.** Equipment tiers are **segment-specific.** Volvo is Tier 1 in **Construction** but Tier **3** in Transport Class 8. This is one of the biggest exam traps. In Class 8, only **Kenworth and Peterbilt** are Tier 1. The client's Volvo FH16 would get Tier 3 terms — shorter max term, potentially higher down payment, lower advance rates.
</details>

---

**X2.** A client has a capital lease and an operating lease with MHCCA. They want to add a loan for a propane delivery truck. Which legal entity name goes on the insurance for each of the 3 products?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **Capital Lease:** Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada **Leasing**, Inc (Additional Insured + Loss Payee)
- **Operating Lease:** Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada **Leasing**, Inc (Additional Insured + Loss Payee) — same as capital lease, it's still a lease
- **Loan (propane truck — HAZMAT):** Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, Inc (Loss Payee only). **AND** this MUST be structured as a loan because propane = hazmat. If anyone tries to make it a lease, decline — hazmat is always a loan.
</details>

---

**X3.** You're preparing a large deal spread for a $2M construction deal. The client is a group of 3 related companies. Total exposure across all entities is $2.4M. What additional documentation is triggered?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Total exposure is $2.4M — this exceeds the **$1M threshold**, which triggers:
- **Org chart** required (especially important here since it's a group of 3 companies)
- **AR/AP listings** required

It does NOT trigger the $3M Legal threshold (total is $2.4M, not $3M). However, if the client adds another deal that pushes total over $3M, documentation goes to Legal.

Also: since it's a group, verify whether you're looking at **consolidated financials** or single-entity, and watch for **inter-company revenue** that should be eliminated in consolidation.
</details>

---

**X4.** A client wants an FMV lease. They're publicly traded. What accounting concern should you flag? [Guide 06]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**IFRS 16.** Publicly traded companies must follow IFRS, which requires almost ALL leases to go **on the balance sheet** as "right-of-use assets." The off-balance sheet advantage of an FMV lease is **largely eliminated** for public companies under IFRS. The off-balance sheet pitch only works for **private companies using ASPE.**

You should still offer the FMV lease for its other benefits (flexibility, lower payments, return option), but don't lead with "off-balance sheet" for a public company.
</details>

---

**X5.** A deal is structured as a loan for $450K. The BOS is billed to MHCCL. The insurance shows "Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada Leasing, Inc" as Additional Insured + Loss Payee. The client is in New Brunswick. The deposit is listed after taxes. How many errors?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Two errors:**

1. **BOS billing:** This is a LOAN — the BOS should be billed to the **client**, not MHCCL. MHCCL is the bill-to only on leases.

2. **Insurance entity + designation:** This is a LOAN — the entity should be "Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada, **Inc**" (no "Leasing") and the designation should be **Loss Payee only** (not Additional Insured). "Additional Insured + Loss Payee" is the lease designation.

**Not errors:**
- Client in New Brunswick — no Bill 96 issue (not Quebec-related)
- Deposit after taxes — this is CORRECT (deposits must be listed after taxes)
</details>

---

**X6.** A dealer asks you to finance a deal for a client you're about to decline. The client doesn't fit MHCCA's credit box. What do you do, and why? [Guide 12]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Use the TAM program.** Instead of saying "no" to the dealer (which damages the relationship), place the deal with a third-party funder through Capital Markets.

**Why:**
1. You **never say "no" to a dealer** — you say "we have another solution"
2. It **preserves the dealer relationship** — they keep sending you other deals
3. It generates **fee income** for MHCCA (placement fee)
4. It helps hit **volume targets**

**Before submitting:** Check the restricted and blocked vendor/broker lists. If the vendor is blocked, the deal cannot proceed through TAM either.
</details>

---

**X7.** A client with $1.8M existing MHCCA exposure submits a new $1.5M deal. List ALL the compliance thresholds that are now triggered.

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

New total exposure: $1.8M + $1.5M = **$3.3M**

**Threshold 1 — Over $1M:** Org chart + AR/AP listings required (was already triggered at $1.8M, but should be updated)

**Threshold 2 — Over $3M:** Documentation must go to **Legal** for review. This is the new trigger — at $3.3M total, the deal file goes to Legal before it can proceed.

Both thresholds apply simultaneously. The $3M threshold is the critical new one.
</details>

---

**X8.** An Alberta driver's license is presented as ID. Name 3 security features specific to Alberta that you should check. [Guide 20]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Alberta has the MOST security features of any province. Three specific ones:

1. **Albertosaurus image** — a laser-engraved dinosaur with precise alignment to the Alberta window, tail travels front-to-back, raised above the card surface with 3D effect
2. **Three transparent windows** — one Alberta-shaped (with smaller photo), two maple-leaf-shaped
3. **Raised text** — license number, DOB, expiry, and signature are engraved into the card core, making them difficult to alter

If an Alberta license lacks these features, it's likely fraudulent.
</details>

---

**X9.** You're in a sales meeting. The client says "everything is fine, we don't need anything." Walk through all 4 steps of APEP with a specific MHCCA example for each step. [Guide 08]

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**A — Analyze:** "Can you walk me through how your current equipment financing works? What do you appreciate most about your bank's setup?" (Neutral, factual — don't attack their current solution)

**P — Probe:** "If there was just ONE thing you could improve about the financing process — just one — what would it be?" (They say: "Approvals take forever, sometimes we lose equipment because the deal isn't ready in time.")

**E — Emphasize Pain:** "When you lose a piece of equipment because financing wasn't ready, what does that cost you? In the last year, how many deals have you missed because of approval timing? At $150K per unit, that's $450K in missed equipment that your competitors are putting to work instead."

**P — Propose:** "If you had a financing partner who could turn around approvals in 24-48 hours instead of 2 weeks, what would that mean for your ability to close deals? Can I structure your next equipment request and show you the difference?"

**Key:** The last P is optional — if they're already leaning in, don't ask a formulaic question. Read the room.
</details>

---

**X10.** A first-time owner-operator with a 780 credit score, verified homeowner, 3 years in business, wants to finance a new Peterbilt 579 ($210K) in Quebec. His guarantor lives in Toronto. Identify every issue.

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Issue 1 — HARD DECLINE: First-time owner/operator.** Class 8 has a hard rule: first-time O/Os are NOT eligible regardless of credit score. 780 score is irrelevant. This alone kills the deal.

**Issue 2 — HARD DECLINE: Less than 5 years in business.** Class 8 requires minimum 5 years TIB. Client has only 3 years.

**Issue 3 — Bill 96:** Client is in Quebec → French-first contract, RDPRM in French. (Guarantor is in Toronto, so trigger #4 doesn't add anything — trigger #1 already applies.)

**Issue 4 — No auto-decision:** Even if the deal could proceed, all Class 8 deals are manual.

**Result:** DECLINE on two hard rules. Refer through TAM program. Client should come back after 5+ years TIB and after gaining operating experience (no longer first-time O/O).

**Note:** The guarantor in Toronto is a bit of a red herring — Bill 96 already applies because the client is in Quebec. The Toronto guarantor does NOT trigger Bill 96 (only Quebec guarantors do).
</details>
