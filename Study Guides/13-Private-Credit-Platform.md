## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

This guide covers MHCCA's **Private Credit platform** -- the suite of financing products that go BEYOND traditional equipment leasing. If the Product Guidebook is the full menu, Private Credit is the "chef's specials" section: higher-value, more complex, and aimed at mid-market companies that need something banks cannot (or will not) provide.

Private Credit includes **three product categories:**

1. **Asset-Based Lending (ABL)** -- loans secured by the borrower's assets
2. **Equipment Finance (Private Credit version)** -- larger, structured equipment deals
3. **Cash Flow Lending** -- loans based on the borrower's ability to generate cash flow

For the associate test, you need to understand: **What are these products? How do they differ from each other? Who are they for? And why does MHCCA offer them?**

## PRODUCT 1: ASSET-BASED LENDING (ABL)

### What Is It?

ABL is a **revolving credit facility** where the loan is secured by the borrower's own assets -- specifically **accounts receivable (A/R), inventory, equipment, and sometimes real estate.** Think of it like a line of credit, but instead of your credit score determining how much you can borrow, it is the VALUE of your assets that determines the borrowing limit.

### Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Deal Range** | **$1MM to $10MM** |
| **Advance Rate -- A/R** | Up to **90%** on eligible accounts receivable |
| **Advance Rate -- Inventory** | Up to **70%** on eligible inventory |
| **Structure** | **Revolving facility** -- borrower draws and repays as needed (like a line of credit) |
| **Reporting** | Monthly reporting required to update the borrowing base |

### Who Is It For?

ABL targets companies that have **strong assets but maybe weaker cash flow or credit.** These are businesses that a traditional bank might turn down because their income statement looks rough -- but their balance sheet (the assets they own) is solid.

**Target Industries:** Manufacturing, Distribution, Wholesale, Services

**Common Use Cases:**
- **Working capital** -- funding day-to-day operations
- **Growth financing** -- expanding without diluting ownership
- **Turnaround situations** -- companies restructuring or recovering from a downturn
- **Acquisitions** -- funding a purchase using the target's own assets as collateral

### How ABL Works (Step by Step)

This is a process you should be able to walk through on the test:

| Step | What Happens |
| --- | --- |
| **Step 1** | MHCCA evaluates the borrower's assets (A/R, inventory, equipment) |
| **Step 2** | MHCCA establishes a **borrowing base** -- the maximum amount the borrower can draw, based on eligible assets |
| **Step 3** | The borrower **draws funds as needed** against the borrowing base |
| **Step 4** | As the borrower collects receivables or sells inventory, the borrowing base **adjusts** (goes up or down) |
| **Step 5** | **Monthly reporting** is required so MHCCA can recalculate the borrowing base |

> **Why This Matters:** The borrowing base is the KEY concept in ABL. It is not a fixed number -- it moves every month based on what the company's assets are worth RIGHT NOW. If the company collects a big receivable, their borrowing base drops (because that A/R is gone). If they book new sales on credit, the base may increase (because new A/R was created).

### ABL vs. The Product Guidebook's ABL

You may notice ABL also appears in the Product Guidebook under Commercial Finance. That is the same product. The Private Credit platform provides **additional context** about how ABL fits into MHCCA's broader lending strategy beyond just equipment. The advance rates differ slightly between sources:

| Source | A/R Advance Rate | Inventory Advance Rate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Private Credit Overview** | Up to 90% | Up to 70% |
| **Product Guidebook** | Up to 90% | Up to 80% on NOLV |

> **Test Tip:** If asked about ABL advance rates, the safest answer is: "Up to 90% on eligible A/R and up to 70-80% on eligible inventory, depending on valuation methodology."

## PRODUCT 2: EQUIPMENT FINANCE (PRIVATE CREDIT VERSION)

### What Is It?

This is equipment financing for **larger, more complex deals** -- the kind that do not fit neatly into MHCCA's standard vendor finance channel. Instead of a dealer submitting a lease application for a single piece of equipment, these are **structured transactions** originated directly by MHCCA.

### Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Ticket Size** | Larger than standard vendor finance (specific range varies) |
| **Structure** | More complex -- can be syndicated, multi-tranche |
| **Origination** | **Direct origination** (NOT through the dealer channel) |
| **Product Types** | Sale-leaseback, refinancing, project finance elements |

### How Is This Different from Standard Equipment Finance?

This is a critical distinction:

| Feature | Standard Equipment Finance (Vendor) | Equipment Finance (Private Credit) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Channel** | Through dealers/vendors | Direct origination by MHCCA |
| **Ticket Size** | Smaller, standardized | Larger, customized |
| **Complexity** | Straightforward leases and loans | Multi-tranche, syndicated, project finance |
| **Examples** | A dealer submits a lease for 3 forklifts | MHCCA structures a $5MM sale-leaseback of an entire production line |

### What Are the Structures?

- **Sale-Leaseback:** The company sells equipment it already owns to MHCCA, then leases it back. This frees up cash while the company continues to use the equipment.
- **Refinancing:** Restructuring existing equipment debt to get better terms, lower payments, or unlock equity.
- **Project Finance Elements:** Financing tied to a specific project or revenue-generating asset, where repayment comes from the project's cash flows.
- **Syndicated/Multi-Tranche:** Large deals split among multiple lenders or structured in layers (tranches) with different risk/return profiles.

> **Why This Matters:** This product shows MHCCA can handle BIG, COMPLEX deals -- not just one-off equipment leases. It positions the company as a serious player in the mid-market lending space, competing with banks and specialty finance companies.

## PRODUCT 3: CASH FLOW LENDING

### What Is It?

Cash Flow Lending is the **opposite of ABL** in terms of what secures the loan. Instead of looking at the borrower's assets (what they OWN), MHCCA looks at the borrower's **ability to generate cash flow** (what they EARN). The loan is underwritten based on **EBITDA multiples and cash flow analysis.**

### Key Features

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| **Deal Range** | **$500K to $10MM** |
| **Basis for Lending** | EBITDA multiples and cash flow analysis |
| **Collateral** | **Less collateral-dependent** -- focused on the business's earning power |
| **Structure** | Term loans (not revolving like ABL) |

### Who Is It For?

Cash Flow Lending targets companies with **strong cash flow but lighter assets.** These are businesses that generate plenty of revenue and profit but may not have a massive pile of receivables or inventory to pledge as collateral.

**Common Use Cases:**
- **Growth capital** -- funding expansion plans
- **Acquisitions** -- financing the purchase of another company
- **Management buyouts (MBOs)** -- management team buying the company from current owners
- **Recapitalizations** -- restructuring the company's debt and equity mix

### What Is EBITDA and Why Does It Matter Here?

**EBITDA** stands for **Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization.** It is the standard measure of a company's operating cash flow -- essentially, how much money the business generates from its core operations before accounting for financing costs, taxes, and non-cash charges.

In Cash Flow Lending, MHCCA uses **EBITDA multiples** to determine how much a company can borrow. For example:

| Scenario | EBITDA | Multiple | Maximum Loan |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Conservative | $2MM | 2.5x | $5MM |
| Moderate | $2MM | 3.0x | $6MM |
| Aggressive | $2MM | 4.0x | $8MM |

> **Note:** The actual multiples used depend on the industry, company quality, and risk appetite. The numbers above are illustrative.

### ABL vs. Cash Flow Lending -- The Key Comparison

This is one of the most testable distinctions in the Private Credit platform:

| Feature | ABL | Cash Flow Lending |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **What determines the loan?** | Value of the borrower's ASSETS | Borrower's ability to generate CASH FLOW |
| **Collateral** | Heavy -- A/R, inventory, equipment, real estate | Light -- focused on earning power |
| **Best for companies with...** | Strong assets, weaker cash flow | Strong cash flow, lighter assets |
| **Structure** | Revolving (draw and repay) | Term loan |
| **Range** | $1MM to $10MM | $500K to $10MM |
| **Monitoring** | Monthly borrowing base updates | Periodic financial reporting and covenant compliance |

> **Why This Matters:** ABL and Cash Flow Lending are two sides of the same coin. One looks at the balance sheet (assets), the other looks at the income statement (earnings). Together, they allow MHCCA to serve companies regardless of whether their strength is in what they own or what they earn.

## COMMON TEST TRAPS AND CLARIFICATIONS

### Trap 1: "ABL is just a loan"
**Wrong.** ABL is a **revolving facility** -- the borrower draws and repays as needed, like a line of credit. It is not a one-time disbursement with fixed payments.

### Trap 2: "Cash Flow Lending and ABL are the same thing"
**Wrong.** They are fundamentally different. ABL is **asset-based** (secured by what you own). Cash Flow Lending is **earnings-based** (secured by what you earn). They serve opposite company profiles.

### Trap 3: "Private Credit Equipment Finance is the same as standard Equipment Finance"
**Wrong.** Private Credit Equipment Finance is for **larger, more complex, directly originated deals** (syndicated, multi-tranche, sale-leaseback). Standard Equipment Finance goes through the **dealer/vendor channel** for smaller, more straightforward transactions.

### Trap 4: "Private Credit is a new business line"
**Partially wrong.** It is not a standalone business line like Aviation Finance or Sustainable Finance. Private Credit products fall under the **Commercial Finance** segment led by Kevin Hall. It is a PLATFORM within an existing segment.

### Trap 5: "The borrowing base in ABL is fixed"
**Wrong.** The borrowing base is **dynamic** -- it changes monthly based on the current value of the borrower's eligible assets. As receivables are collected and inventory is sold, the base adjusts.

## KEY TERMS GLOSSARY

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| **ABL** | Asset-Based Lending -- revolving credit secured by A/R, inventory, equipment, and/or real estate |
| **Accounts Receivable (A/R)** | Money owed to the company by its customers for goods or services already delivered |
| **Advance Rate** | The percentage of an asset's value that the lender will lend against (e.g., 90% advance rate on A/R means you can borrow 90 cents for every dollar of receivables) |
| **Borrowing Base** | The maximum amount a borrower can draw under an ABL facility, calculated from eligible assets multiplied by advance rates |
| **Cash Flow Lending** | Lending based on the borrower's ability to generate cash flow (EBITDA), not collateral |
| **EBITDA** | Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization -- a measure of operating cash flow |
| **EBITDA Multiple** | The ratio used to determine loan size in cash flow lending (e.g., 3x EBITDA) |
| **Eligible Assets** | Assets that qualify to be included in the borrowing base (must meet specific criteria regarding age, quality, and collectibility) |
| **Management Buyout (MBO)** | When a company's management team purchases the business from its current owners |
| **Mid-Market** | Companies that fall between small businesses and large corporations -- typically $5MM to $500MM+ in revenue |
| **Multi-Tranche** | A deal structured in multiple layers, each with different terms, risk levels, or priority of repayment |
| **Recapitalization** | Restructuring a company's mix of debt and equity, often to improve financial position or facilitate ownership changes |
| **Revolving Facility** | A credit arrangement where the borrower can draw, repay, and redraw funds up to a set limit (like a credit card) |
| **Sale-Leaseback** | Selling an asset you own, then immediately leasing it back -- frees up cash while retaining use of the asset |
| **Syndicated** | A deal funded by multiple lenders working together to share risk |
| **Term Loan** | A loan with a fixed repayment schedule over a set period (as opposed to revolving credit) |
| **Turnaround** | A company in financial distress that is restructuring to return to profitability |

## PRACTICE QUESTIONS & ANSWERS

**Q1: What are the three product categories within MHCCA's Private Credit platform?**
> **A:** (1) Asset-Based Lending (ABL), (2) Equipment Finance (Private Credit version -- larger, structured deals), and (3) Cash Flow Lending.

**Q2: What is the deal range for ABL, and what are the advance rates?**
> **A:** ABL ranges from **$1MM to $10MM**. Advance rates are up to **90% on eligible accounts receivable** and up to **70% on eligible inventory.**

**Q3: What is the fundamental difference between ABL and Cash Flow Lending?**
> **A:** ABL is secured by the borrower's **assets** (A/R, inventory, equipment, real estate) -- it depends on what the company OWNS. Cash Flow Lending is based on the borrower's **ability to generate cash flow** (EBITDA) -- it depends on what the company EARNS. ABL is for companies with strong assets but weaker cash flow; Cash Flow Lending is for companies with strong cash flow but lighter assets.

**Q4: What is a "borrowing base" in ABL, and is it fixed or dynamic?**
> **A:** The borrowing base is the **maximum amount a borrower can draw** under an ABL facility, calculated by applying advance rates to eligible assets. It is **dynamic, not fixed** -- it adjusts monthly as the borrower collects receivables, sells inventory, or acquires new assets. Monthly reporting is required to update it.

**Q5: What is the deal range for Cash Flow Lending, and what is it based on?**
> **A:** Cash Flow Lending ranges from **$500K to $10MM**. It is based on **EBITDA multiples and cash flow analysis** -- the borrower's demonstrated ability to generate earnings to service the debt.

**Q6: Name four common use cases for Cash Flow Lending.**
> **A:** (1) Growth capital, (2) Acquisitions, (3) Management buyouts (MBOs), (4) Recapitalizations.

**Q7: How does Private Credit Equipment Finance differ from standard Equipment Finance offered through the vendor channel?**
> **A:** Private Credit Equipment Finance involves **larger ticket sizes, more complex structures** (syndicated, multi-tranche, sale-leaseback, project finance elements), and is **directly originated by MHCCA** rather than coming through the dealer/vendor channel. Standard Equipment Finance is smaller, more straightforward, and dealer-driven.

**Q8: Who oversees the Private Credit platform within MHCCA's organizational structure?**
> **A:** **Kevin Hall**, VP of Commercial Finance. Private Credit products (ABL, Direct Finance, structured Equipment Finance) fall under the Commercial Finance segment, which also includes Technology & Healthcare and Syndication.

**Q9: How does the Private Credit platform support MHCCA's strategic transformation?**
> **A:** Private Credit is part of MHCCA's **diversification strategy** -- moving beyond traditional equipment leasing to become a full-service mid-market financial platform. It supports the **dual approach** of Product Diversification (new product types like ABL and Cash Flow Lending) and Industry Diversification (serving manufacturing, distribution, wholesale, and services beyond just transportation). It positions MHCCA as a **one-stop-shop** for mid-market financing needs.

**Q10: What industries does ABL primarily target?**
> **A:** Manufacturing, Distribution, Wholesale, and Services.

**Q11: Explain the five-step process of how ABL works.**
> **A:** (1) MHCCA evaluates the borrower's assets (A/R, inventory). (2) MHCCA establishes a borrowing base based on eligible assets. (3) The borrower draws funds as needed against the borrowing base. (4) As the borrower collects receivables or sells inventory, the borrowing base adjusts. (5) Monthly reporting is required to update the borrowing base.

**Q12: A company has $3MM in EBITDA and is applying for Cash Flow Lending. If MHCCA applies a 3x EBITDA multiple, what is the maximum loan amount?**
> **A:** $3MM x 3.0 = **$9MM** maximum loan. (Note: This is within the $500K-$10MM range for Cash Flow Lending, so it would be eligible from a size perspective.)

**Q13: What is a sale-leaseback, and which Private Credit product might include it?**
> **A:** A sale-leaseback is when a company **sells equipment it already owns** to MHCCA and then **immediately leases it back**, freeing up cash while continuing to use the equipment. This structure is available through **Equipment Finance (Private Credit version).**

**Q14: True or False -- Cash Flow Lending is heavily collateral-dependent.**
> **A:** **False.** Cash Flow Lending is **less collateral-dependent** than ABL or equipment-based lending. It focuses on the borrower's **earning power and cash flow generation** rather than the value of specific physical assets.

**Q15: If a mid-market company has strong receivables and inventory but inconsistent earnings, which Private Credit product would be most appropriate -- ABL or Cash Flow Lending? Why?**
> **A:** **ABL** would be most appropriate. Since the company has strong assets (A/R and inventory) but inconsistent earnings, ABL allows them to borrow against the VALUE of those assets rather than their cash flow. Cash Flow Lending would be less suitable because it relies on consistent EBITDA, which this company lacks.
