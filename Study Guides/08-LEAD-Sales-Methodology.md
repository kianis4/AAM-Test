# STUDY GUIDE: The L.E.A.D. Sales Methodology
### *MHCCA Formation LEAD -- The 4 Steps of a Sales Interview*

---

## WHAT IS THIS DOCUMENT ABOUT?

This is MHCCA's official framework for **how to run a sales meeting from start to finish**. Think of it as a step-by-step recipe for a successful client conversation. The acronym **L.E.A.D.** gives you four phases that happen in order, every single time you sit down with a prospect or client.

Here is the short version you need to memorize right now:

| Letter | Step | Plain-English Translation |
|--------|------|---------------------------|
| **L** | **Legitimize** your presence | *"Here is who I am and why you should give me 40 minutes of your day."* |
| **E** | **Explore** the issues | *"Let me ask smart questions so I understand YOUR world."* |
| **A** | **Accentuate** (Emphasize) the difference | *"Here is why MHCCA is different and better for YOUR situation."* |
| **D** | **Decide** (Trigger the Decision) | *"Let's agree on what happens next."* |

**Why This Matters:** Every other study guide you have looked at -- Combo Prospecting, the Product Guidebook, Common Objections -- feeds INTO this framework. Prospecting gets you the meeting. LEAD is what you do once you are IN the meeting. The products you studied are what you present during "A" (Accentuate). The objection-handling techniques you learned are what you use during "A" and "D." LEAD is the backbone that holds everything together.

---

## THE MHCCA DEFINITION OF "SELLING"

Before diving into the four steps, the training materials define what a "sale" actually is:

> **Selling = Convincing someone of the advantage of our product, service, or idea AND obtaining an effort from them in return.**

Read that again. It is a two-part definition:
1. You convince them there is an **advantage** (not just a feature -- an advantage that matters to THEM).
2. They make an **effort** in return (signing a deal, changing suppliers, investing money, etc.).

### What "Efforts" Does a Client Have to Make?

This is important because it explains why clients hesitate. When you ask someone to buy from MHCCA, you are asking them to:

- **Make a decision** (people hate deciding -- it is stressful)
- **Change supplier** (switching costs, learning curves)
- **Convince someone else** internally (their boss, their board, their CFO)
- **Change habits** (the way they have always done things)
- **Say no to someone** (their current provider, a colleague who recommended a competitor)
- **Take time to analyze** your proposal
- **Take a risk** (what if it does not work?)
- **Invest money**

> **Your role is to minimize these efforts, reassure your client, and help them make a decision.**

**Why This Matters:** If a client seems resistant, they are probably not resisting YOU -- they are resisting the effort. Your job is to make the effort feel smaller and the reward feel bigger.

## BEFORE THE MEETING: PREPARATION (The Step Before Step 1)

You do not just show up and wing it. The training materials are very clear: **preparation happens BEFORE you walk through the door.**

### The Pre-Meeting Checklist

| Task | What It Means in Practice |
|------|---------------------------|
| **Research the client (KYC -- Know Your Client)** | Visit their website, read recent news, check LinkedIn profiles, understand their industry |
| **Know the client's jargon and KPIs** | If they are in trucking, know what "deadhead miles" means. If they are in construction, know what "utilization rate" means. Speak their language. |
| **Define your OBJECTIVE** | What do you want to walk out of this meeting with? A second meeting? A signed deal? A referral to the decision-maker? Write it down. |
| **Prepare your questions using BRAIN** | Use the BRAIN acronym (below) to make sure your questions cover all angles |
| **Go to the client's website** | Find levers to start the interview, adapt your argumentation to their reality |
| **Research your contact on Google/social media** | Identify common points (same university, same city, shared connections) for rapport-building |
| **Know your products** | Understand how MHCCA solutions solve problems specifically for THIS client |
| **Anticipate objections** | Think about what they will push back on and prepare your responses |

### The BRAIN Questioning Framework

**BRAIN** is a memory aid to make sure your pre-prepared questions cover every important theme. Before the meeting, write at least one or two questions for each letter:

| Letter | Theme | Example Questions You Might Prepare |
|--------|-------|-------------------------------------|
| **B** | **Budget / Business** | "What is your annual equipment spend?" / "How is business trending this year?" |
| **R** | **Relationships / Resources** | "Who else is involved in financing decisions?" / "What resources do you have for fleet management?" |
| **A** | **Authority / Ability** | "Who makes the final call on equipment financing?" / "What is your approval process?" |
| **I** | **Issues / Interests** | "What challenges are you facing with your current financing?" / "What matters most to you in a financial partner?" |
| **N** | **Needs / Next steps** | "What are your equipment needs for the next 12 months?" / "What would a good next step look like for you?" |

**Why This Matters:** Walking into a meeting without prepared questions is like walking into an exam without studying. BRAIN ensures you do not forget to ask about budget (awkward to circle back to), authority (you might be pitching the wrong person), or next steps (you leave with no commitment).

## STEP 1: LEGITIMIZE YOUR PRESENCE (The "L" in LEAD)

This is the opening of your meeting. You have about **5-7 minutes** to accomplish something critical: make the client feel that this meeting is worth their time.

### The Objectives of a Good Meeting Introduction

According to the training materials, a strong opening achieves ALL of these:

1. **Create a positive atmosphere** -- the client should feel comfortable, not defensive
2. **Reassure the client** -- they should feel they are in good hands
3. **Be in control of the meeting** -- you set the agenda, not the client
4. **Obtain time and information from the client** -- they agree to give you 40 minutes and answer your questions
5. **Know the client's expectations** -- what do THEY want from this meeting?
6. **Stand out from the competition** -- within the first few minutes, they should sense you are different
7. **Motivate the client to continue the meeting** -- they should not be looking at their watch
8. **Legitimize your presence** -- they should feel your visit is justified and valuable

### The 4 Ingredients of a Meeting Introduction: ISAQ

Think of these as four things you do (roughly in order) in the first few minutes:

| Letter | Ingredient | What You Actually Do | Real-World Example |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| **I** | **Initiate dialogue** | Break the ice. Small talk. Make them human, not just a "prospect." | "I noticed the new warehouse expansion on my way in -- that must be exciting for the team." |
| **S** | **Synchronize** | Find common ground. Build rapport. Show you did your homework. | "I saw on LinkedIn that you also attended [university] -- small world!" |
| **A** | **Assure about the casting** | Explain who you are and why you are credible. Reassure them about your role. | "I have been working with companies in the transportation sector for 5 years, and I specialize in helping fleets optimize their financing." |
| **Q** | **Quickly differentiate** | Briefly explain what makes MHCCA unique. Not a full pitch -- just a hook. | "What sets us apart is that we are not just a lender -- we are part of the Mitsubishi group, which means we understand the equipment itself, not just the numbers." |

**Why This Matters:** Most salespeople skip straight to "let me tell you about our products." ISAQ forces you to build rapport and credibility FIRST. Clients buy from people they trust. Trust starts in the first 5 minutes.

---

### The OPA Framework (Structuring Your Meeting Opening)

After ISAQ (the warm-up), you use **OPA** to set the formal structure of the meeting:

| Letter | Element | What It Means | Key Rule |
|--------|---------|---------------|----------|
| **O** | **Objective** | State why you are here | Frame it in terms of **CLIENT BENEFIT**, not your benefit. Say "I want to help you find a way to reduce your fleet costs" NOT "I want to tell you about our leasing products." |
| **P** | **Presentation** | Explain how the meeting will proceed | Give them a mini agenda: "First, I would like to learn about your operations. Then I will share some ideas. Finally, we will see if there is a fit." |
| **A** | **Agreement** | Get their agreement on the agenda and duration | "We will need about 40 minutes. Does that work for you? And before we begin -- what do YOU expect from this meeting?" |

### The Sample Opening Script (Memorize This Pattern)

The training materials provide a model script. You do not need to recite it word-for-word, but you should internalize its structure:

> *"The main reason I wanted to meet with you is that we have implemented extremely innovative solutions that are appreciated by our clients for their simplicity and return on investment. I felt it was essential for you to be informed about this, but since each client is unique, I suggest we get to know each other better. Also, I have a few questions about your organization and operations in order to see if our solutions will provide you with added value. As I mentioned to you on the phone, we would need about 40 minutes for this first exploratory meeting. Before going any further, I would like to know what YOU expect from this meeting."*

Let us break down WHY this script works:

| Part of the Script | What It Accomplishes |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| "...innovative solutions appreciated by our clients for their simplicity and ROI" | **Quick differentiation** -- gives them a reason to listen |
| "I felt it was essential for you to be informed" | **Legitimizes your presence** -- you are here because they NEED to know this |
| "since each client is unique, I suggest we get to know each other better" | **Positions you as consultative**, not pushy -- you want to understand THEM first |
| "I have a few questions about your organization" | **Sets expectation** that you will be asking questions (so they are not caught off guard) |
| "about 40 minutes" | **Agreement on duration** -- respects their time |
| "what do YOU expect from this meeting?" | **Flips the conversation** -- now THEY are talking, and you learn what matters to them |

### The "NO on the Table" Concept

This is a powerful psychological technique taught in the LEAD materials:

**Put "NO" on the table early.** Tell the prospect it is okay to say no. Show that at this stage, you are EXPLORATORY.

**Example:** "Look, I am not here to sell you anything today. If at the end of our conversation you feel this is not a fit, that is completely fine. I would rather we both know that now than waste each other's time."

**Why This Matters:** This sounds counterintuitive -- why would you make it easy for them to say no? Because **reducing pressure paradoxically makes people more open to saying yes.** When a client feels trapped, they put up walls. When they feel free to walk away, they relax and actually LISTEN. The materials describe it this way: "Always show that you want it but do not need it."

---

### The Client's Internal Questions

While you are talking, the client is silently asking themselves these questions. Your opening must answer ALL of them:

| What They Are Thinking | How Your Opening Answers It |
|------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **"Who is this person?"** (Credibility) | ISAQ -- the "A" (Assure about the casting) |
| **"Why are they here?"** (Purpose) | OPA -- the "O" (Objective) |
| **"What is in it for me?"** (Value) | Your objective framed in terms of CLIENT benefit |
| **"Can I trust them?"** (Trust) | ISAQ -- the "I" and "S" (rapport-building) + "NO on the table" |
| **"What do they want from me?"** (Ask) | OPA -- the "P" and "A" (transparent agenda) |
| **"Will they adapt to our particularities?"** | Your research (KYC) showing you understand their world |
| **"Will they waste my time?"** | Agreement on 40-minute duration |
| **"Does this person know their subject?"** | Quick differentiation + speaking their jargon |
| **"Will they get along with my teams?"** | Rapport-building, likability, professionalism |

### Voice Delivery: PIVAS

HOW you say things matters as much as WHAT you say. The training materials use the acronym **PIVAS**:

| Letter | Element | What It Means | Pro Tip |
|--------|---------|---------------|---------|
| **P** | **Pace** | Do not rush. Speak at a measured speed. | Nervous salespeople speed up. Slow down deliberately -- it projects confidence. |
| **I** | **Intonation** | Vary your tone. Do not speak in a monotone. | Raise your pitch slightly on key benefits. Lower it for serious/important points. |
| **V** | **Volume** | Project confidence. Do not mumble. | Match the room. Louder in a noisy office, softer in a quiet boardroom. |
| **A** | **Articulation** | Speak clearly. Enunciate every word. | Practice saying "Mitsubishi HC Capital Canada" until it rolls off your tongue smoothly. |
| **S** | **Smile** | People can hear a smile, even on the phone. | A genuine smile changes the muscles around your mouth, which changes how you sound. |

### Additional Legitimize Tips from the Training Slides

The summary slides add these specific tactical tips for Step 1:

- **Reassure about the "casting"** from the very beginning of the interview -- let them know you are the right person to be having this conversation with them
- **If the client is not asking questions, sharpen their interest** by presenting some "GEMS" (valuable nuggets), innovative ideas, and/or ROI figures
- **Initiate the dialogue by encouraging the client to introduce themselves** -- do not do all the talking
- **Avoid trying to make the client speak at all costs** before presenting your strengths -- find the right balance
- **Tell success stories** -- brief anecdotes about similar clients who benefited from MHCCA

## STEP 2: EXPLORE THE ISSUES (The "E" in LEAD)

This is the discovery phase -- the most important part of the entire sales meeting. You are asking questions, listening, and uncovering what the client actually needs.

### The HOOK Technique (Also Called "Hanging")

Think of this as a three-part method for keeping the client engaged during the exploration phase:

1. **Explain the "why"** you are here and how the meeting will proceed (you already did this in OPA)
2. **Respond to the client's motivations** (the 6 motivations below -- this is critical)
3. **Offer "GEMS"** (nuggets of valuable insight) to the client throughout the conversation

The metaphor is a fish hook: you are "hanging" something valuable in front of the client to keep them interested and talking.

---

### The 6 Client Motivations (MEMORIZE THESE)

Every client is driven by one or more of these six motivations. Your job is to figure out which ones matter most to THIS client, and then frame everything you say through that lens:

| # | Motivation | What Drives This Client | How to Spot It | How to Respond at MHCCA |
|---|-----------|------------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| 1 | **Security** | They want to feel safe. They fear risk, change, and the unknown. | They ask about guarantees, track records, references, "what if" scenarios | Emphasize MHCCA's stability (Mitsubishi backing), long track record, risk management expertise |
| 2 | **Pride** | They want to feel important, respected, and recognized. | They mention their achievements, their company's reputation, being "the best" | Acknowledge their success. Position MHCCA as the partner that top companies choose. |
| 3 | **Novelty** | They want something new, innovative, cutting-edge. | They ask "what is different?", they talk about innovation, they are early adopters | Highlight MHCCA's innovative solutions, technology platforms, new product features |
| 4 | **Comfort** | They want easy, convenient, hassle-free solutions. | They complain about complexity, paperwork, slow processes | Emphasize MHCCA's simplicity, one-stop-shop approach, easy documentation |
| 5 | **Money** | They want to save money or make money. | They focus on rates, fees, total cost, ROI, payback period | Show TCO savings, flexible structures, how financing preserves cash flow |
| 6 | **Likeability** | They want to work with people they like and trust. | They value relationships, ask personal questions, prefer face-to-face | Be genuine. Build rapport. Show you care about them as a person, not just a transaction. |

**Why This Matters:** If your client is motivated by **Security** and you keep pitching **Novelty** ("We are so innovative!"), you are actually making them MORE nervous, not less. Matching your message to their motivation is the difference between a deal and a dead end.

---

### GEMS -- Nuggets of Value

**GEMS** are valuable pieces of information, insight, or data that you share with the client during the conversation to build trust and demonstrate expertise. They are NOT sales pitches. They are genuinely useful things like:

- An industry trend they might not be aware of
- A regulatory change that could affect their business
- A benchmark or statistic relevant to their operations
- A success story from a similar company (without naming names)
- A new approach to a common problem in their industry

**Example:** "One thing I have been seeing across the construction sector this year -- companies that switched from ownership to leasing on their excavators are reporting 15-20% lower total cost of ownership because they are avoiding the maintenance cliff that hits around year 4. I thought that might be relevant given the age of your fleet."

**Why This Matters:** GEMS position you as a trusted advisor, not a salesperson. When you give value BEFORE asking for anything, the client becomes much more willing to open up about their real challenges.

### Three Types of Questions (The Traffic Light System)

The training materials use a color system to categorize questions:

| Color | Type | What It Does | When to Use | Example |
|-------|------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Pink** | **Assumption** | You impose your point of view and assume you know the answer | **AVOID this type.** It makes clients defensive. | "So you must be having problems with your current provider, right?" |
| **Blue** | **Closed** | Yes/No answers. You validate your understanding or direct the answer. | Use to **CONFIRM** facts or reach agreement | "Do you currently finance your equipment through a bank?" / "Does that timeline work for you?" |
| **Green** | **Open** | Who, What, Where, When, How, Why, How many. The client talks freely. | Use to **DISCOVER** needs, feelings, and context | "What challenges are you facing with your current fleet?" / "How do you handle equipment replacement decisions?" |

### How to Tell Open from Closed Questions

| | Open Questions | Closed Questions |
|---|----------------|------------------|
| **Start with...** | Who, What, Where, When, How, Why, In what way, With whom, With what | Do you think?, Do you have?, Do you agree?, Do you do?, Are you considering? |
| **Client's response** | Long, detailed, in their own words | Short -- Yes, No, or a brief fact |

### Open vs. Closed: Advantages and Limitations

| | Open Questions | Closed Questions |
|---|----------------|------------------|
| **Advantages** | Relaxes the client, stimulates expression, encourages dialogue, allows real exchange | Re-centers discussions, requires little time, allows verification |
| **Limitations** | Requires time, risk of losing the thread, can get lost with talkative clients | Gives little information, favors silence/mutism, can feel like an interrogation |
| **Best used for** | **Discovery** (learning about their situation) | **Reaching agreement** (confirming what you heard) |

**The golden rule:** Start with open questions (get them talking), then use closed questions to confirm and narrow down.

---

### Two Types of Exploratory Questions

Within your open questions, there are two subtypes you should alternate between:

| Type | Purpose | Examples |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Questions of FACT** | To understand the current or past situation -- the objective reality | "What is your current financial institution?" / "What type of material do you finance?" / "How many units are in your fleet?" |
| **Questions of OPINION** | To obtain a position, feeling, or judgment on those facts | "How does your current solution meet your expectations?" / "How is that going for you?" / "What do you wish was different?" |

**Why you need both:** Facts give you the DATA. Opinions give you the EMOTION. People buy based on emotion and justify with data. If you only ask factual questions, you will know their fleet size but not how they FEEL about their current financing. If you only ask opinion questions, you will know they are frustrated but not why.

---

### Exploration Tips from the Training Slides

The summary slides for "Explore the Issues" add these tactical pointers:

- **If the client IS requesting (actively expressing a need):** Rely on the expressed needs (the "what") to uncover the real issues (the "why"). Dig deeper behind what they say they want.
- **Ask a pointed question that demonstrates your expertise** on a subject -- this builds credibility mid-conversation
- **Identify (and spell out) the stakeholders** in the decision-making process and their role -- who else needs to be convinced?
- **Ask "trigger" questions** to generate client buy-in and/or validate your understanding
- **Alternate between factual questions and open questions** -- factual for information gathering, open for involvement, opinions, and feelings
- **With a less demanding client** (one who is not actively expressing needs), first ask neutral and factual questions to probe the strengths and weaknesses of their current solution

### When a Client Does NOT Express a Need

This is one of the most challenging situations in sales: the client says "We are fine. We do not need anything." The training materials give you a specific system for handling this.

**Your two goals when a client does not express a need:**

1. **MAKE THEM TALK** -- This is an analytical approach that will reveal needs they themselves are unaware of
2. **MAKE THEM AWARE** -- Help them see the negative consequences of the status quo by asking the right questions

### The APEP Method (Your Secret Weapon for Silent Clients)

**APEP** stands for **Analyze, Probe, Emphasize, Propose.** It is a four-step questioning sequence designed to take a client from "I do not need anything" to "Actually, I might be interested in hearing your solution."

---

#### A -- Analyze the Situation

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **WHAT you do** | Make the client comfortable talking about their current solutions |
| **WHY you do it** | To understand their solutions and current situation; to learn what benefits they see from their perspective |
| **HOW you do it** | Ask ONLY factual and neutral questions. Do NOT try to demonstrate that their current solution is bad or needs to be optimized. Stay curious, not critical. |
| **EXAMPLE questions** | "How would you describe your current situation?" / "When was the last time you updated your solution?" |

**Sample "Analyze" Questions:**
- What is the current situation?
- What is the existing solution?
- What are the advantages of the existing solution?
- What do you appreciate most about the existing solution?
- Initially, why did you choose this solution?

**Real-World MHCCA Example:** A trucking company says they are happy with their bank financing. You say: "That is great. Can you walk me through how your current financing works? What do you like most about the arrangement?" You are NOT attacking their bank. You are genuinely understanding their world.

---

#### P -- Probe the Problem

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **WHAT you do** | Reveal potential risks and performance gains; identify possible cost reductions (TCO) |
| **WHY you do it** | To make them aware of issues they may not have considered; to prepare for the next step |
| **HOW you do it** | Ask "new benefit" questions; use hooks to get them thinking about improvements |
| **EXAMPLE questions** | "If there was only one thing to improve today, what would it be?" / "What would happen if...?" |

**Sample "Probe" Questions:**
- If there was one thing to improve, what would you say?
- And apart from that, what else could be improved?
- How could you increase performance?

**Real-World MHCCA Example:** After they describe their happy bank relationship, you say: "If there was one thing you could improve about your current financing process -- just one thing -- what would it be?" Almost nobody says "nothing." There is ALWAYS something. Maybe it is speed. Maybe it is flexibility. Maybe it is the fact that their bank does not understand equipment. Whatever they say, that is your opening.

---

#### E -- Emphasize the Pain

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **WHAT you do** | Emphasize the negative consequences of the issues they just raised; create a sense of urgency |
| **WHY you do it** | To help them measure the financial impacts; to increase their perception of complexity and urgency |
| **HOW you do it** | Ask engagement questions about consequences, effects, and impact |
| **EXAMPLE questions** | "How does this problem affect you?" / "In the event that [X] occurs, how will you manage it?" |

**Sample "Emphasize" Questions:**
- What are the direct negative consequences of this problem?
- What are the risks if you do not find a solution?
- How important is it to you?
- What is the level of urgency in finding a solution to resolve the problem?

**Real-World MHCCA Example:** They said their bank is slow to approve financing. You say: "When approvals take too long, what happens to the deal you are trying to close with the equipment dealer? Have you ever lost a piece of equipment because the financing was not ready in time? What did that cost you?" Now THEY are doing the math on how much their current problem is costing them. You did not tell them their bank is bad -- they told THEMSELVES.

---

#### P -- Propose a Solution

| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **WHAT you do** | Make them a requester and listener of a possible solution |
| **WHY you do it** | To raise their awareness of the value, importance, and usefulness of your solutions |
| **HOW you do it** | Ask "reveal" questions. **NOTE: This step is OPTIONAL and should not appear stereotypical or formulaic.** |
| **EXAMPLE questions** | "If this problem could be solved, what would be the benefits for your company?" |

**Sample "Propose" Questions:**
- If this problem could be solved, what would be the benefit for your company?
- What would be the ideal solution for you?
- If I had a solution that helps you solve the problem, would you be willing to consider it?

**Real-World MHCCA Example:** After they realize slow approvals have cost them three deals this year, you say: "If you had a financing partner who could turn around approvals in 24-48 hours instead of two weeks, what would that mean for your business?" Now THEY are imagining the better future. They are selling themselves.

**IMPORTANT:** The training materials specifically flag that this last "P" step is **optional** and should not come across as a canned technique. If the client is already clearly interested, you do not need to ask "If I had a solution, would you consider it?" -- that would sound patronizing. Read the room.

### What Is a "Need"? (The Formal Definition)

The training materials provide a precise definition you should know:

> **A need is a GAP between what we have today and what we would like to have... or a gap between what we have today and what we will need to have in the future... WITH A REAL INTENTION TO CHANGE.**

The three elements of a true need:
1. **Current state** -- where they are now
2. **Desired/required future state** -- where they want or need to be
3. **Real intention to change** -- they are not just daydreaming; they actually want to do something about it

**Why This Matters:** If there is no gap, there is no need, and there is no sale. If there is a gap but no intention to change, there is no sale either. Your job in the Explore phase is to uncover gaps AND build the intention to close them.

## STEP 3: ACCENTUATE THE DIFFERENCE (The "A" in LEAD)

Now that you understand the client's world (from Step 2), it is time to show them why MHCCA is the right choice. This is where you present your solution -- but NOT as a generic pitch. Everything you say should be **tailored to what you learned in Step 2.**

### Key Principles for Accentuating the Difference

From the training slides, here are the specific tactics:

| Tactic | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| **Tell success stories** | Share brief anecdotes of similar clients who benefited from MHCCA -- this is social proof |
| **Argue by presenting benefits in THEIR context** | Do not list features. Connect every benefit to something THEY said they cared about, according to THEIR expectations |
| **Dig (question) then Neutralize objections** | When an objection arises, first ask a clarifying question ("Can you tell me more about that concern?"), then reframe/soften the objection before arguing against it |
| **Raise objections in advance** | If you KNOW they are going to object to something (e.g., "You are not a bank"), bring it up yourself first. This shows confidence and takes the sting out of it. |
| **Avoid responding immediately to an objection** | Do not be reactive. First ask for clarifications. Understand the REAL objection before you try to answer it. |

### The Connection to Client Motivations

Remember the 6 motivations from Step 2? This is where they pay off:

| If Their Motivation Is... | Accentuate the Difference By... |
|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| **Security** | Emphasizing Mitsubishi's global stability, MHCCA's track record, your risk management expertise |
| **Pride** | Positioning MHCCA as the choice of leading companies, acknowledging their standards |
| **Novelty** | Highlighting innovative solutions, new technology, forward-thinking approaches |
| **Comfort** | Showing how easy and seamless your process is, less paperwork, one point of contact |
| **Money** | Demonstrating TCO savings, ROI figures, cash flow benefits, flexible payment structures |
| **Likeability** | Being personable, reinforcing the relationship, showing genuine care for their success |

## STEP 4: DECIDE -- TRIGGER THE DECISION (The "D" in LEAD)

This is the closing phase. You have legitimized your presence, explored their issues, and shown them how MHCCA is different. Now it is time to get commitment.

### Key Tactics for Triggering the Decision

From the training slides:

| Tactic | What It Means in Practice |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **Propose a test** | Suggest a pilot or trial: "Why do not we start with one unit and see how our process compares to what you are used to?" |
| **Put the client in a "real-life" situation** using your product/service | Walk them through a concrete scenario: "Let me show you what it would look like if you financed your next 5 trucks through MHCCA..." |
| **Offer the client to meet your experts** | "I would love for you to meet our credit team -- they can walk you through the specifics at your place or ours." |
| **Make a comparative assessment (before/after)** | Show them their current state vs. what life would look like with MHCCA: "Right now you are paying X and waiting Y days. With us, it would be A and B days." |

**Why This Matters:** The "D" step is not about pressure. It is about making the decision feel easy, logical, and low-risk. Notice that every tactic above reduces the perceived effort: a test is lower commitment than a full deal; meeting experts is lower commitment than signing; a before/after comparison does the thinking FOR them.

## THE FULL FLOW: PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER

Here is what a complete LEAD sales interview looks like from start to finish:

```
BEFORE THE MEETING
    |-- Research the client (KYC)
    |-- Prepare questions using BRAIN
    |-- Define your objective
    |-- Anticipate objections
    v
STEP 1: LEGITIMIZE (5-7 minutes)
    |-- ISAQ: Initiate dialogue, Synchronize, Assure about casting, Quickly differentiate
    |-- OPA: State Objective (client benefit), Present agenda, get Agreement
    |-- Put "NO on the table"
    |-- Use PIVAS for delivery
    |-- Ask: "What do YOU expect from this meeting?"
    v
STEP 2: EXPLORE (15-20 minutes)
    |-- Use HOOK technique: explain why you are here, respond to motivations, offer GEMS
    |-- Identify which of the 6 motivations matter to THIS client
    |-- Alternate between open questions (discover) and closed questions (confirm)
    |-- Alternate between questions of FACT and questions of OPINION
    |-- If client does NOT express a need --> use APEP:
    |       A: Analyze the situation (neutral, factual questions)
    |       P: Probe the problem ("one thing to improve?")
    |       E: Emphasize the pain (consequences, urgency, financial impact)
    |       P: Propose a solution (optional -- "if this could be solved...")
    |-- Define the client's NEED (gap + intention to change)
    v
STEP 3: ACCENTUATE THE DIFFERENCE (10-15 minutes)
    |-- Present benefits in THEIR context, based on THEIR motivations
    |-- Tell success stories
    |-- Handle objections: dig first, neutralize, then argue
    |-- Raise objections in advance if you know they are coming
    v
STEP 4: TRIGGER THE DECISION (5-10 minutes)
    |-- Propose a test or pilot
    |-- Put client in a real-life scenario
    |-- Offer to connect them with MHCCA experts
    |-- Show before/after comparison
    |-- Get clear commitment on next steps
```

## COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID

| Mistake | Why It Is a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Skipping preparation | You look unprofessional and ask generic questions | Spend 15-30 minutes on KYC and BRAIN before every meeting |
| Pitching too early | You talk about MHCCA before understanding their needs | Complete Step 2 (Explore) FULLY before Step 3 (Accentuate) |
| Using assumption questions | "So you must be unhappy with your bank, right?" puts clients on the defensive | Use open and factual questions instead |
| Only asking closed questions | The meeting feels like an interrogation; you learn nothing deep | Lead with open questions; use closed questions only to confirm |
| Attacking the client's current solution | "Your current provider is terrible" makes THEM feel terrible for choosing it | Ask neutral questions; let THEM identify the problems (APEP method) |
| Responding to objections immediately | You appear defensive and may answer the wrong objection | Dig first: "Can you tell me more about that concern?" |
| Leaving without clear next steps | The deal dies in no-man's-land | Always end with a concrete next step (Step 4) |
| Ignoring the client's dominant motivation | You pitch novelty to someone who wants security | Listen for cues and match your message to their motivation |

## ANSWER KEY

**Answer 1:** **L** = Legitimize your presence. **E** = Explore the issues. **A** = Accentuate (Emphasize) the difference. **D** = Decide (Trigger the Decision).

**Answer 2:** Selling is convincing someone of the advantage of our product, service, or idea AND obtaining an effort from them in return. It is a two-part definition: advantage + effort.

**Answer 3:** **B** = Budget/Business. **R** = Relationships/Resources. **A** = Authority/Ability. **I** = Issues/Interests. **N** = Needs/Next steps.

**Answer 4:** **I** = Initiate dialogue (break the ice with small talk). **S** = Synchronize (find common ground, build rapport). **A** = Assure about the casting (explain who you are and why you are credible). **Q** = Quickly differentiate (briefly state what makes MHCCA unique).

**Answer 5:** The objective must be stated in terms of CLIENT BENEFIT, not your own benefit. Say "I want to help you find ways to reduce your fleet costs" NOT "I want to tell you about our leasing products."

**Answer 6:** You tell the prospect early that it is okay to say no. You show that you want the business but do not need it, and that the meeting is exploratory. This works because it reduces pressure, which paradoxically makes clients more open and willing to engage honestly. When people feel free to say no, they relax and actually listen.

**Answer 7:** (1) **Security** -- "MHCCA is backed by the global Mitsubishi group, so your financing is with a stable, long-term partner." (2) **Pride** -- "Companies like yours that lead their industry deserve a financing partner that matches their standards." (3) **Novelty** -- "We have just launched an innovative digital platform that streamlines the entire process." (4) **Comfort** -- "We handle everything from approval to payout with a single point of contact, so you do not have to chase paperwork." (5) **Money** -- "Our clients typically see 15-20% lower total cost of ownership compared to traditional bank financing." (6) **Likeability** -- Build genuine rapport, be personable, show interest in them as a person.

**Answer 8:** **Pink = Assumption** (you impose your point of view and assume -- AVOID because it makes clients defensive). **Blue = Closed** (Yes/No -- use to CONFIRM). **Green = Open** (Who/What/Where/When/How/Why -- use to DISCOVER).

**Answer 9:** A **question of FACT** seeks objective information about the current or past situation (e.g., "What type of equipment do you currently finance?"). A **question of OPINION** seeks the client's feelings or judgment about those facts (e.g., "How satisfied are you with the turnaround time on your current financing approvals?").

**Answer 10:** APEP = Analyze the situation, Probe the problem, Emphasize the pain, Propose a solution. You use it when a client does NOT express a need -- when they say they are fine and do not need anything.

**Answer 11:** During "Analyze," you must ask ONLY factual and neutral questions. You should NOT try to demonstrate that their current solution is bad or needs to be optimized. Stay curious, not critical. Let them describe their world without judgment.

**Answer 12:** **Analyze:** "Can you walk me through how your current bank financing works? What do you appreciate most about it?" **Probe:** "If there was one thing you could improve about the process -- just one thing -- what would it be?" (They say approvals are slow.) **Emphasize:** "When approvals take too long, have you ever lost a deal on a piece of equipment? What did that cost you in lost revenue?" **Propose:** "If you had a financing partner who could approve in 24-48 hours, what would that mean for your ability to close deals faster?"

**Answer 13:** **P** = Pace (do not rush). **I** = Intonation (vary your tone). **V** = Volume (project confidence). **A** = Articulation (speak clearly). **S** = Smile (people can hear a smile). The "S" matters on phone calls because smiling changes the muscles around your mouth, which physically changes how your voice sounds -- it comes across as warmer and more friendly.

**Answer 14:** When a client raises an objection, do NOT respond immediately with a counter-argument. Instead: (1) **Dig** -- ask a clarifying question to understand the real objection ("Can you help me understand what concerns you about that?"). (2) **Neutralize** -- reframe or soften the objection with a softened reformulation ("I understand that the cost seems higher at first glance..."). (3) Only THEN present your argument. This ensures you are addressing the real concern, not a surface-level one.

**Answer 15:** Any 3 of: (1) Propose a test/pilot. (2) Put the client in a "real-life" situation using your product/service. (3) Offer the client to meet your experts (at their place or yours). (4) Make a comparative assessment with the client showing before/after.

**Answer 16:** A need is defined as a GAP between what we have today and what we would like to have... or a gap between what we have today and what we will need to have in the future... WITH A REAL INTENTION TO CHANGE. Three elements: (1) current state, (2) desired or required future state, (3) real intention to change.

**Answer 17:** She is driven by **Pride** (wanting to look impressive, mentioning achievements like the government contract) and possibly **Novelty** (wanting modern-looking equipment). You would tailor your pitch by acknowledging her success ("Winning that government contract is a major achievement -- congratulations"), positioning MHCCA as the partner that top-tier companies choose, and showing how MHCCA can help her keep her fleet modern and impressive without tying up all her capital.

**Answer 18:** GEMS are valuable nuggets of information or insight that you share with the client to build trust and demonstrate expertise. They are NOT sales pitches. Example for a transportation client: "One trend I have been seeing across the trucking industry is that companies are moving to shorter lease terms on tractors -- 3 years instead of 5 -- because the technology in new trucks improves fuel efficiency by about 8-10% per generation. Companies that cycle faster are spending less on fuel overall. I thought that might be worth considering for your fleet."

**Answer 19:** This line is important because it flips the conversation from salesperson-led to client-led. It accomplishes three things: (1) It shows respect for the client's time and priorities. (2) It reveals what the client actually cares about (which may be different from what you assumed). (3) It gives the client a sense of control, making them more comfortable and open.

**Answer 20:** The 7 efforts: (1) Make a decision. (2) Change supplier. (3) Convince someone else internally. (4) Change habits. (5) Say no to someone (current provider). (6) Take time to analyze. (7) Take a risk. (8) Invest money. (Note: the materials list 8 efforts.) Understanding this list is important because client resistance is often about the EFFORT, not about YOU or your product. When you understand what effort they are struggling with, you can address it specifically -- e.g., if they are nervous about convincing their board, you can offer to present to the board yourself.

## FINAL STUDY TIP

The L.E.A.D. framework is sequential. You cannot skip steps. You cannot Accentuate the difference (Step 3) if you have not Explored the issues (Step 2), because you will not know what to emphasize. You cannot Trigger the Decision (Step 4) if you have not Accentuated the difference (Step 3), because they will not have a reason to decide.

Think of it like cooking:
- **Preparation (before)** = buying your ingredients (research, BRAIN questions)
- **L (Legitimize)** = preheating the oven (setting the right conditions)
- **E (Explore)** = mixing the ingredients (understanding what you are working with)
- **A (Accentuate)** = putting it in the oven (applying heat -- your solution -- to their specific ingredients)
- **D (Decide)** = serving the dish (presenting the finished result and asking them to taste it)

Skip a step and the whole recipe falls apart.
